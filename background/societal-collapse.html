Title: Goliath’s Curse: How Societies Age and Collapse

  1. Aging of Societies: Over time, many hierarchical societies become more fragile, exhibiting patterns akin to aging. This trend is observed in both early Goliaths like Cahokia and Tiwanaku, as well as developed empires and kingdoms such as the Western Roman Empire and Lowland Maya.

  2. Inequality Growth with Lootable Resources: The emergence of lootable resources (e.g., caged land, monopolizable weapons) fuels inequality within communities. This phenomenon has been consistent throughout history; inequality tends to rise once a community becomes hierarchical and remains high until a major institutional reform or “great leveller” occurs (e.g., bloody revolution, mass mobilization warfare, pandemic, or deep collapse).

  3. Extractive Institutions and Unequal Exchange: More concentrated power within societies leads to elites extracting more wealth, energy, and labor from the rest of the population. This is measured by extractive institutions, which indicate the degree of social power concentration. Examples include ancient Athens (more egalitarian) vs. Assyrian Empire (highly unequal militarized dictatorship), or modern Norway (relatively democratic) and Saudi Arabia (autocratic).

  4. How Extraction Creates Fragility: Inequality creates a wealth pump, where social arrangements increasingly benefit elites at the expense of the poor. This “popular immiseration” or impoverishment of the masses leads to worsening health and decreasing standards of living. More extractive institutions also hinder societies’ capacity to respond effectively to crises, making them more vulnerable to collapse when faced with shocks like drought, disease, or invaders.

  5. Triggering Collapse: Climate change is a common presence across collapses, often acting as the final trigger that explodes societies already riven with discontent and rising inequality. However, not all droughts are equally damaging; more severe ones can do greater harm (e.g., the final termination of Puebloans). Other triggers may include disease or invaders.

  6. Survival of the Fairest: More democratic societies tend to be more resilient, experiencing less famine, disease, conflict, and social change during crises like climate shifts. This is due to local people’s ability to respond quickly and effectively to new challenges compared to distant elites, as well as stronger social connections that foster collaboration during stressful periods.

  7. The Wisdom of Democracy: Over the long run, large diverse groups make better decisions on average than small elite groups or dictators (wisdom of crowds). This is because diverse perspectives and information lead to more accurate predictions and problem-solving. Inclusive political institutions promote flexibility, effective responses, social cohesion, and prevent abuses of power compared to extractive ones.

  8. Gang Warfare: The history of state formation often involves military strongmen who conquer their neighbors through force, creating a template for sovereign leadership. Warlordism, organized crime, and states share similar ingredients: hierarchical structures that coercively extract resources from territories and populations. Understanding the rise of warlords like Narmer and Charles Taylor can shed light on collapse’s causes and bloodshed involved.

  9. State Creation as a Criminal Enterprise: States, mafias, and crime syndicates share similarities in their establishment through violence, coercion, and patronage. They monopolize resources, extract wealth from citizens, and provide services to maintain control—akin to running protection rackets. This perspective can help explain the dynamics of state formation, collapse, and the role of violence throughout history.

  10. War of the Few Against the Many: Creating states involves a significant amount of bloodshed as a few individuals establish structures of authority and monopolize lootable resources through force and unequal exchanges. The text discusses two significant collapses in ancient history: the Early Bronze Age (2200-2000 BCE) and the Late Bronze Age (1185 BCE).

Early Bronze Age Collapse:

This collapse affected various civilizations, including the Akkadian Empire, Old Egyptian Kingdom, Indus Valley Civilization, and Malta’s Temple Period. The primary cause is believed to be a mega-drought that led to resource scarcity, famine, and abandonment of settlements across the region. In Mesopotamia, it led to a switch back to more autonomous city-states and localized forms of collective governance. The Indus Valley Civilization transformed gradually, with people moving from cities into a more village-dominated economy.

Late Bronze Age Systems Collapse:

This collapse occurred around 1185 BCE and affected numerous civilizations in the eastern Mediterranean, southern Asia Minor, and Anatolia, including the Mycenaeans, Minoans, Hittites, Cypriots, Canaanites, Egyptians, Mitanni, Assyrians, and Kassites.

The collapse was likely triggered by a combination of factors:

  1. Drought: A prolonged drought caused crop yield shortages, famine, and economic hardship across the region. The Egyptians bred more tolerant cattle in Canaan and extended dry farming to compensate for lower grain production in the north.

  3. Pestilence: Epidemics may have occurred; for example, pharaoh Ramesses V died of smallpox, and the entire royal Hittite family was eliminated by an epidemic around 1350 BCE. However, evidence for widespread outbreaks is sparse.
  4. Sea Peoples: Invaders from various groups, including refugees, migrants, pirates, and savvy mobile people from the Aegean and eastern Mediterranean, contributed to the collapse due to drought, warfare, oppression, and economic hardship.
