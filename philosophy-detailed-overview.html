Title: A Participatory Universe in the Realist Mode: On the Separation of Observational and Agentive Perspectives in Classical and Quantum Mechanics

Author: Jenann Ismael

Summary:

This paper explores the concept of interference, both in classical and quantum mechanics, to understand the differences between these two frameworks. The author introduces “Interference” as a phenomenon that arises when a system’s representation of its domain is connected to its own actions or knowledge-gathering activities. This connection makes it impossible to stabilize certain features of the world independently of the act of assigning them probabilities.

In classical physics, interference can be channeled and focused into the future. However, in quantum mechanics, interference is endemic due to the nature of quantum interactions (measurements). The author argues that this distinction highlights the sense in which the universe is participatory.

Ismael critiques the traditional epistemic attitude towards the world, suggesting that it’s only possible in settings where our actions don’t significantly affect what we’re trying to represent or learn about. In a quantum setting, the act of measurement disturbs the system and creates interference, making it impossible to stabilize properties independently.

The author argues that this perspective is aligned with John Wheeler’s observer-participancy but incorporated into an ontological account of material processes and interactions. She proposes that quantum measurements should be reconceptualized as choices rather than discoveries, reflecting the agentive nature of quantum interactions.

Ismael discusses various interpretations of quantum mechanics (Everettian quantum mechanics, relational quantum mechanics, retrocausal interpretation) to illustrate how these theories accommodate this participatory view while maintaining an objective ontology. She concludes by emphasizing that the deep insight of Wheeler was the breakdown of the epistemic/ontic distinction, revealing the ultimate inseparability of subject and object, self and world, and representation and represented.

The paper contributes to the ongoing debate on quantum foundations by offering a perspective that respects the reality of an objective world while acknowledging the participatory nature of our interactions with it, particularly at the quantum level.

Curie’s Principle: Summary and Explanation

Curie’s Principle, proposed by Pierre Curie in 1894, states that the symmetry of a cause (Curie-cause) is always preserved in its effects (Curie-effect). This principle has been subject to criticism and misinterpretation, often dismissed as false or irrelevant. However, Jenann Ismael argues for its truth and significance in physics and philosophy of science.

Key Points:

  1. Definition of Curie-Cause and Effect: A physical theory specifies the structure and relationships between objects. For any such theory, there is a many-one mapping from possible specifications of a Curie-cause (A) to specifications of its effect (B). In other words, A determines B according to the laws of the theory.

  2. Symmetry: Symmetries are transformations that preserve certain aspects of the system. Characteristic symmetries apply to all specifications of a cause or effect, while idiosyncratic symmetries only apply to some.

  3. Principle Statement: Curie’s Principle asserts that all characteristic symmetries of a Curie-cause are also characteristic symmetries of its effect. Conversely, any idiosyncratic asymmetry in the effect must correspond to an idiosyncratic asymmetry in the cause.

  4. Proof: The proof is straightforward from definitions. If Curie’s Principle were false, it would imply a characteristic symmetry of the cause not present in its effect, violating determinism. Deterministic laws ensure that each cause has a unique effect, preserving all relevant symmetries.

  5. Interpretation: The principle’s physical significance lies in understanding how symmetries relate causes and effects. Symmetries of a set of equations (laws) correspond to permutations of irrelevant parameters or compensating transformations of relevant parameters, preserving the described relation. Geometric symmetries can be understood similarly once formulated covariantly.

Ismael’s Defense: She argues against common objections: - The principle is a priori, addressing empirical questions appropriately by focusing on the logical structure of causes and effects. - ‘Spontaneous symmetry breaking’ (SSB) doesn’t contradict it; SSB refers to idiosyncratic asymmetries in specific instances rather than violating Curie’s Principle. - The principle applies in indeterministic contexts, guiding how to conceptualize causes and effects even when outcomes aren’t uniquely determined. - It’s not empty but profoundly informative about the nature of physical laws and their symmetries.

Implications: Recognizing Curie’s Principle can enhance our understanding of symmetry in physics, guiding how we interpret laws and their implications for causes and effects. It underscores that symmetries aren’t just mathematical niceties but carry profound physical meaning.

Doubts about Curie’s Principle stem from various misconceptions. One is the assumption of determinism, as the world might be indeterministic, meaning we cannot infer past asymmetries solely based on current conditions. The principle should be reformulated to state that we can infer either an asymmetric A or no A when B exhibits an asymmetry.

Another doubt arises from restricting what counts as a cause in such a way that intrinsic features only are considered causes, excluding aspects like the state of motion of a system. However, this restricted notion of cause is inconsistent with examples Curie used and our general scientific practice.

A prevalent doubt originates from a faulty definition of symmetry, exemplified by A.F. Chalmers’ misunderstanding. Chalmers defined symmetries as transformations that leave the mathematical form of laws unchanged, leading to confusion in situations where laws predict different outcomes for symmetric initial conditions due to inherent asymmetries in the laws themselves.

The principle’s applicability in indeterministic contexts is also discussed. The author argues that while deterministic laws map state-descriptions onto other state-descriptions, indeterministic laws map them onto probability functions. Curie’s Principle can still apply here; the asymmetry of an effect results from an asymmetric probability distribution over possible states, not individual events.

The principle offers a criterion for distinguishing between chance and law-governed aspects of a system in indeterministic contexts. The ‘coarse state’ describes the complete state-description with unit probability, while ‘fine states’ are those with non-zero probabilities. Transformations that are symmetries of the coarse state but not fine states are considered permutations of chance features.

Finally, the text addresses spontaneous symmetry breaking—phenomena often thought to contradict Curie’s Principle. The author argues that these phenomena do not actually violate the principle. For instance, Euler buckling (a rod bending under load) might seem like a violation because an initially symmetric system becomes asymmetric when loaded beyond a critical point. However, this apparent symmetry breaking is due to the introduction of boundary conditions (the weight distribution and support at the base), which are not invariant under all possible transformations, thus aligning with Curie’s Principle.

The principle is not violated when symmetry breaks spontaneously due to non-linear dynamics or external perturbations. Instead, it suggests that there are hidden asymmetries in the initial state, which become apparent under certain conditions.

  2. Non-linear Dynamics: The principle also accommodates non-linear dynamics where small changes in parameters can lead to large, seemingly asymmetrical effects. In such cases, the causes are not entirely symmetrical; they contain hidden fluctuations or minute asymmetries that only become apparent under specific conditions (like reaching a critical parameter value).

  3. Hidden Variables: Curie’s Principle is connected to the concept of hidden variables – attributes of a system that are not directly observable but influence its behavior. If A is the cause of B, and B exhibits an asymmetry T, then at least one characteristic of A must also be asymmetric concerning T. This hidden asymmetry might not be immediately apparent in the causes but emerges under certain conditions or through careful analysis.

  4. Deterministic Evolution: In deterministic systems, if an initially symmetric state evolves into an apparently asymmetric state, Curie’s Principle implies that the initial state contained hidden asymmetries. For instance, molecular biological processes like frog zygote development, which seems symmetrical at each stage but introduces increasing asymmetry over time, are consistent with this principle. The apparent symmetry of early stages conceals inherent asymmetries that manifest later.

It suggests that the hidden causes postulated by our theories should collectively conceal as much, if not more, asymmetry as is revealed in their effects.

  6. Exegetical Support: Ismael argues that Curie intended his principle to apply to characteristic symmetries of cause and effect families rather than specific instances. This interpretation avoids counterexamples and aligns with how Curie applied the principle in his original paper to derive intrinsic symmetries of physical quantities like electric and magnetic fields.

In conclusion, Curie’s Principle, as interpreted by Ismael, is not trivial but rather a profound statement about the relationship between cause and effect, symmetry, and hidden variables. Title: How to Combine Chance and Determinism: Thinking about the Future in an Everett Universe* Author: Jenann Ismael Published: Philosophy of Science, Vol. 70, No. 4 (October 2003)

Jenann Ismael’s paper discusses how to reconcile the principles of chance and determinism in the context of Everett interpretations of quantum mechanics, also known as Many Worlds or Multi-World Theories. These theories propose that every quantum event results in multiple parallel universes (or “worlds”), each representing a possible outcome.

Ismael argues for an understanding where there can be genuine uncertainty about future events despite the deterministic nature of the universe and the complete knowledge of initial conditions. She challenges the tendency among Everettians to incorporate consciousness or non-physical elements into their physical description.

Key Points:

  1. Everett Universe: The universe is depicted as an infinite branching set of histories, represented by a tree-like structure where each node corresponds to a measurement, and branches represent possible outcomes. Each sequence of results is realized in this structure.

  2. Probability Interpretation: Providing an interpretation for probability (a measure over the space of histories) in an Everett universe requires three elements:

    • A method to extract a tree-like universe from the universal wave function without using a preferred basis.
    • Showing how chance can arise within a deterministic context.
    • Defending Born’s Rule as the quantitative method for calculating probabilities.

  3. The Problem: Deterministic evolution of the universe according to Schrodinger’s equation doesn’t leave room for chancy events, and this is especially problematic in Everett interpretations where all possible outcomes are realized in different branches. The challenge is to find something in an Everett universe for Born probabilities to attach to without introducing non-physical elements.

  4. Preliminary Attempt: Ismael suggests that the key lies in identifying ‘picking’ events in the history, i.e., points where outcomes are selected according to Born’s probabilities. However, due to the symmetrical nature of branches in an Everett universe, it seems impossible to pinpoint a unique trajectory or world for the probabilities to pertain to without invoking non-physical facts about personal identity.

  5. Second Pass: The author proposes that one need not single out a specific trajectory (or ‘humunculus’) as uniquely ‘mine’. Instead, Born’s probabilities can be interpreted as giving the probability of obtaining an up-result at a location in a post-measurement branch of the universe. Each observer uses Born’s Rule to compute probabilities for their respective worlds based on their pre-measurement histories. This approach doesn’t require non-supervenient facts about personal identity or non-physical relations between person stages.

  6. Complications: The major complication lies in the fact that individual measurement results cannot be identified descriptively from a pre-measurement perspective due to symmetrical relations they bear with the pre-measurement history. This leads to linguistic awkwardness but does not undermine the Everett picture, as no non-physical elements are added.

  7. Global Determinism and Local Chanciness: Ismael explains how global determinism can coexist with local chanciness by highlighting that symmetries in the universe allow for events to occur without being determined by their histories, creating a form of internal indeterminism.

  8. Conclusion: The paper concludes by emphasizing the importance of understanding personal identity within a deterministic framework and the role of indexical language in describing individual experiences without invoking non-physical entities. It also highlights how this approach can help combat the tendency to incorporate consciousness or homunculi into physical descriptions, maintaining a purely physical interpretation of quantum mechanics.

The idea is that entangled wholes are more fundamental than their parts, and the cosmos as a whole forms one vast entangled system due to initial entanglement at the Big Bang and Schrödinger evolution spreading entanglements.

  2. Wave Function Realism: This view treats the wave function in conﬁguration space (and perhaps the world-particle) as fundamental. The shape of the wave (and possibly the position of the world-particle) explains the coordinated behavior of Alice and Bob from a common source, even though it may seem to have multiple independent degrees of freedom at first glance. The text argues that these degrees of freedom are not actually independent due to global constraints on the wave function’s squared amplitudes and entanglement properties.

The main argument against positing new fundamental relations or treating components as distinct entities with additional entanglement relations is that it introduces unnecessary complexity without empirical support. The text also contrasts this holistic approach with an alternative, which posits new entanglement relations alongside spatiotemporal relations but does not treat components as joint manifestations of a common ground.

Finally, the text revisits Einstein’s objection to quantum mechanics’ “holism,” which stems from his belief that spatial separation implies metaphysical separation (Einstein’s Inference). However, the authors side with Bohr and argue that there are no compelling empirical or conceptual grounds for this connection between spatial separation and metaphysical grounding.

In summary, the text advocates for common ground explanations of quantum entanglement, such as Spacetime State Realism Streamlined and Wave Function Realism, over alternative approaches involving new fundamental relations or treating components as distinct entities with additional entanglement relations. These holistic views are favored due to their simplicity, consistency with empirical evidence, and lack of ad hoc complexity.

They aim to bridge the gap between objective and subjective understandings, showing how abstract scientific concepts are rooted in human experience rather than replace or diminish it.

Space-Quantum-Mechanics-and-Bohms-Fishtank

Title: Space, Quantum Mechanics, and Bohm’s Fish Tank

Author: Jenann Ismael

In this chapter from “The Foundation of Reality: Fundamentality, Space, and Time,” Jenann Ismael explores the possibility that space might not be a fundamental structure in physics. This idea is motivated by considerations from cosmology and quantum gravity, where many theories describe fundamental structures that are non-spatiotemporal.

The chapter focuses on two key aspects of quantum mechanics: entanglement and complementarity. To elucidate these concepts, Ismael introduces low-dimensional examples that mimic features of quantum phenomena while avoiding the technical complexities often associated with them.

  1. Bohm’s Fishtank: This example involves a 3D fish tank filmed from both front and side by two cameras projecting their images onto a 2D screen in an adjacent room. An observer conﬁned to this 2D screen would notice correlations between the images (for instance, if one image shows a lionfish’s tail from the side, the corresponding image will show it from the front). These correlations seem instantaneous but are actually due to redundancy in the space where the fish images appear—a lower-dimensional projection of the actual 3D tank. This example mirrors entanglement in quantum mechanics, where two particles can be correlated without direct interaction or signaling through spacetime.

  2. Complementarity: Ismael illustrates complementarity using the fish tank scenario. Certain aspects of a fish’s appearance (like front and back) are mutually exclusive; you cannot see both at once on the 2D screen, just as certain quantum observables (like position and momentum) are complementary—you can’t simultaneously measure them precisely due to Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle. This occlusion in the fish tank corresponds to non-commutativity of quantum observables.

Ismael then discusses two types of causal notions and spaces:

  1. Causal Notions: The directed acyclic graph (DAG) representation captures causal relationships by intervention counterfactuals, while causal processes involve exchanges of conserved quantities. In quantum mechanics, the search for such clear-cut causal processes in spacetime has led to various interpretations denying the necessity for them.

  2. Notions of Space: Ismael distinguishes between ‘Our-space’ (the manifest space we experience) and ‘Ur-space’ (a hypothetical underlying space that might explain quantum phenomena). She argues that treating Our-space as an “image space” could resolve the puzzles of entanglement and complementarity without needing extra dimensions or hidden variables.

The author concludes by emphasizing that this perspective isn’t driven by metaphysical predilections but rather by clues in quantum phenomena, suggesting a shift from viewing Our-space as fundamental to seeing it as a derivative structure emerging from a more fundamental Ur-space. This view aligns with the broader trend in modern physics of treating space and spacetime as emergent, derived structures rather than fundamental entities.

Authors: Jenann Ismael and Bas C. van Fraassen

Summary:

The article explores the role of symmetries in identifying superfluous theoretical structure within physical theories. The authors aim to provide a general framework applicable to all theories, addressing philosophical problems related to the nature of scientific theories and their representations.

Key Concepts: 1. Superfluous Structure: Excess elements or relations in a theory that are dispensable for its intended purpose but may have relevance from other perspectives (e.g., aesthetic, historical). 2. Theory Structure: A structured set of possibilities (possibility space) comprising an initial framework (ontology) and laws selecting physical possibilities. 3. Qualitative vs Measurable Quantities: The distinction between directly observable features accessible through perception and quantifiable values that may not have a direct observational correspondence but are still measurable under specific conditions. 4. Symmetries of Theory (ToT): Transformations preserving the satisfaction or non-satisfaction of laws, acting on the initial possibility space; they need not preserve any particular characteristic of physically possible worlds. 5.
