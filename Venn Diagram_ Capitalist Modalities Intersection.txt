Market Monopolization: Controlling access points between creators and consumers.

Value Extraction: Leveraging control to siphon profits from both ends of the market.

Profit from Death: Monetizing systems that manage or expose populations to death.

Dispossession: Stripping communities of land, rights, or resources for gain.

State-Corporate Synergy: Collaborations that prioritize economic interests over human life.

1. Mixed-Integer Polynomial Optimization QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \sum_i \delta_i \cdot \mathbb{I}[x_i \in \mathbb{Z}] + \sum_j \gamma_j p_j(x) \right\}

This relaxation addresses optimization problems involving both integer variables and polynomial terms. By relaxing integrality constraints and quadratizing higher-degree polynomials (e.g. via sum-of-squares decompositions), it transforms a hard symbolic-numeric problem into a solvable convex form. In symbolic cognition, this models hybrid compositional systems, where logic (integers) and continuous analog quantities (emotions, beliefs) co-evolve.


---

2. Combinatorial Optimization QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in [0,1]^n} \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \sum_i s_i \right\}, \quad \text{subject to relaxed discrete constraints}

Here, discrete structures such as paths, matchings, or groupings are approximated through continuous relaxations (e.g. LP or convex hull). Slack variables absorb violations of combinatorial constraints. This is vital in symbolic systems (e.g. SpherePop), where decision trees or logic branches must be traversed without hard commitment—modeling exploratory symbolic inference.


---

3. Integer Programming QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in [0,1]^n} \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \lambda \sum_i |x_i - \text{round}(x_i)| \right\}

Binary (0-1) variables are relaxed to lie within the unit interval, and penalty terms softly enforce integrality. In cognitive modeling, this enables approximate reasoning over logical spaces—useful in belief revision, conceptual ambiguity, or transitional logic states (e.g., between contradictory hypotheses).


---

4. Semidefinite Programming QPR

Formula:

\min_{X \succeq 0} \left\{ \mathrm{tr}(P X) + q^T x \right\}, \quad \text{with } X \approx xx^T

This approach drops rank constraints to allow convex optimization over positive semidefinite matrices. It is essential in modeling multi-layered affective-symbolic representations—for example, in Psychocinema where meanings are manifold and non-linear. The PSD matrix encodes distributed coherence across partially repressed narratives.


---

5. Polynomial Optimization QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left\{ \sum_i \left( \tfrac{1}{2} x^T P_i x + q_i^T x \right) + \text{slack for } x^d \text{ terms} \right\}

Highly nonlinear polynomial systems (common in recursive symbolic logic) are quadratized using relaxation techniques like sum-of-squares (SOS) or Lasserre hierarchies. This enables tractable recursive evaluation in systems like SpherePop where symbolic expressions grow in depth and nonlinearity.


---

6. Non-convex Optimization QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \sum_k \epsilon_k \right\}, \quad \text{using convex outer bounds}

Non-convex functions are approximated by convex surrogates or outer approximations, allowing solution by gradient-based methods. This enables cognitive flexibility under epistemic uncertainty, supporting navigation through belief landscapes without becoming trapped in local optima.


---

7. Markov Blanket Inference QPR

Formula:

\min_b \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2} b^T P b + q^T b + \alpha \|b - A s\|^2 + \kappa \|b - o\|^2 \right\}

8. Stochastic Optimization QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \mathbb{E}_\xi \left[ \tfrac{1}{2} x^T P(\xi) x + q(\xi)^T x \right] + \text{VarPenalty}(x)

Expectation over stochastic parameters  is minimized, often with added penalties for variance. This models decision-making under epistemic noise, essential in Geometric Bayesianism and affective forecasting (e.g., coping with symbolic uncertainty in Psychocinema).

9. Robust Optimization QPR

Formula:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \max_{u \in \mathcal{U}} \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2} x^T P(u) x + q(u)^T x \right\}

\subsection{1. Mixed-Integer Polynomial Optimization QPR} The Mixed-Integer Polynomial Optimization QPR addresses problems of the form:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i \cdot \mathbb{I}[x_i \in \mathbb{Z}] + \sum_{j} \gamma_j p_j(x) \right\}

\subsection{2. Combinatorial Optimization QPR} The Combinatorial Optimization QPR relaxes discrete optimization problems over sets. A general form can be expressed as:

\min_{x \in [0,1]^n} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} s_i \right\}

\subsection{3. Integer Programming QPR} For Integer Programming problems with binary variables, the QPR takes the form:

\min_{x \in [0,1]^n} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i - \text{round}(x_i)| \right\}

\subsection{4. Semidefinite Programming QPR} Semidefinite Programming Relaxations are applied to problems with matrix variables, often involving rank constraints. A typical form is:

\min_{X \succeq 0} \left\{ \mathrm{tr}(C X) + b^T y \right\}

\subsection{5. Polynomial Optimization QPR} Polynomial Optimization problems involve minimizing a polynomial function subject to polynomial constraints. QPRs for these problems often utilize Sum-of-Squares (SOS) relaxations, which can be formulated using Semidefinite Programming. A simplified representation for the objective relaxation is:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left\{ \sum_i \left( \frac{1}{2} x^T P_i x + q_i^T x \right) + \text{slack terms for higher-order monomials} \right\}

\subsection{6. Non-convex Optimization QPR} For general non-convex optimization problems, QPRs involve constructing convex relaxations, such as convex envelopes or outer approximations. A general form of the relaxed objective can be:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}x^T P x + q^T x + \sum_k \epsilon_k \right\}

\subsection{7. Markov Blanket Inference QPR} In the context of Markov Blanket inference within a probabilistic graphical model, a QPR for finding the optimal boundary variables $b$ might take the form:

\min_b \left\{ \frac{1}{2} b^T P b + q^T b + \alpha \|b - A s\|^2 + \kappa \|b - o\|^2 \right\}

\subsection{8. Stochastic Optimization QPR} Stochastic Optimization problems involve expectations over random variables $\xi$. A QPR might approximate the expected value of a quadratic objective:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \mathbb{E}_\xi \left[ \frac{1}{2} x^T P(\xi) x + q(\xi)^T x \right] + \lambda \cdot \text{RiskMeasure}(x, \xi)

\subsection{9. Robust Optimization QPR} Robust Optimization aims to find solutions that are feasible for all realizations of uncertain parameters $u$ within an uncertainty set $\mathcal{U}$. A QPR for a robust quadratic objective could be:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \max_{u \in \mathcal{U}} \left\{ \frac{1}{2} x^T P(u) x + q(u)^T x \right\}

\subsection{10. Large-Scale Optimization QPR} Large-Scale Optimization problems are characterized by a high number of variables. QPRs in this domain often involve decomposition techniques, such as block coordinate descent or distributed optimization, where the problem is broken into smaller, manageable quadratic subproblems:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \sum_{k=1}^K \left( \frac{1}{2} x_k^T P_k x_k + q_k^T x_k \right)
