Below is a compact, citation-backed overview you can drop into an introduction or literature-review section. ## 1. Short statement of the idea

RSVP proposes that the fundamental "substrate" of physics is a **plenum** made of three coupled fields — a scalar capacity field $\Phi$, a directed/negentropic vector flow $\mathcal{v}$, and an entropy field $S$ — whose coupled dynamics replace (or re-interpret) standard cosmological expansion by entropy-smoothing and flow-driven effects. The framework is naturally phrased in the language of **derived** geometry and quantized using BV/AKSZ methods (see below for primary sources). ([arXiv][1])

---

## 2. Conceptual motivations & related work

* **Thermodynamic/entropic approaches to gravity and spacetime** — derivations of gravitational field equations from local thermodynamic relations and horizon entropy: Jacobson's derivation of Einstein's equation from Clausius-like relations is a foundational reference. Verlinde's entropic-force proposal and Padmanabhan's "gravity as emergent/thermodynamic" program form the mainstream literature motivating entropy-based reinterpretations of gravitational phenomena. These works justify seeking a theory in which entropy fields and entropy fluxes play dynamical roles. ([link.aps.org][2])

## 3. Core fields and action (informal)

Write fields as maps/sections on a spacetime manifold $\mathcal{M}$ with metric $g$:

* $\Phi:\mathcal{M}\to\mathbb{R}$ — scalar capacity / potential density.
* $\mathcal{v}\in\Gamma(T\mathcal{M})$ — directed negentropic flow (vector field / current).
* $S:\mathcal{M}\to\mathbb{R}$ — entropy density field (or a scalar density).

A generic action takes the form

$$
F[\Phi,\mathcal{v},S] \;=\; \int_{\mathcal M}\! \mathcal L\big(\Phi,\mathcal{v},S,\nabla\Phi,\nabla\!\cdot\!\mathcal v,\partial_t S;g\big)\,d\mathrm{vol}_g,
$$

with $\mathcal L$ built from entropy-gradient couplings, capacity-flow interactions, and appropriate constraint terms (e.g. Lagrange multipliers for conserved entropy flux). Variation yields coupled PDEs for $\Phi,\mathcal v,S$. (This is the RSVP ansatz; the mathematical machinery below shows how to formalize those fields as derived objects.) — (no single-source citation; see formal machinery next.)

## 4. Mathematical formalization: derived stacks & shifted symplectic structures

To place RSVP on a modern mathematical foundation you express field configuration spaces and their moduli as **derived stacks** and use **shifted symplectic** structures to capture the BV/odd-symplectic geometry necessary for quantization.

Key references:

* Foundations and surveys of **derived algebraic geometry** (Toën & Vezzosi; surveys by Toën). These give the basic setting for derived stacks and cotangent complexes. ([math.univ-toulouse.fr][3])
* **Shifted symplectic structures**: Pantev-Toën-Vaquié-Vezzosi (PTVV) introduced $n$-shifted symplectic structures on derived Artin stacks and proved mapping stacks inherit shifted forms — central if you want an action functional on mapping stacks and the AKSZ-type shifted symplectic inputs needed for BV. ([arXiv][4])

These works are the canonical modern references if you want to speak precisely about "0-shifted" or "(-1)-shifted" symplectic forms, Lagrangian intersections, and derived mapping stacks for field theories.

## 5. Quantization: BV formalism and AKSZ sigma models

* **BV (Batalin-Vilkovisky)** is the standard formalism for handling gauge symmetries, ghosts, antifields, and the classical/quantum master equations; it gives the algebraic/odd-symplectic language in which a field action must solve $(S,S)=0$ classically and the quantum master equation afterward. ([Wikipedia][5])
* **AKSZ construction** (Alexandrov-Kontsevich-Schwarz-Zaboronsky) produces solutions of the classical master equation by building sigma-model actions from a source (often a shifted tangent/supermanifold) and a target space with an invariant symplectic/graded structure; it is the natural technique to quantize topological or shifted-symplectic sigma-models such as the ones you're proposing for RSVP. See the original AKSZ paper and modern lecture notes/reviews. ([arXiv][6])

For a mathematically rigorous treatment of BV quantization in the AKSZ context and its relation to deformation quantization / index theorems, see more recent BV/AKSZ expositions (e.g. Grady-Li-Si Li and other rigorous treatments). ([arXiv][7])

\section
{Historical Eras of Computation}
To situate xylomorphic computation within a broader historical context, we delineate five eras of computation, each characterized by evolving representational substrates and reasoning paradigms. These eras reflect progressive abstraction and integration, culminating in xylomorphic infrastructure as a synthesis of conceptual blending with physical-ecological convergence. Each new era layers upon the previous, preserving foundational mechanisms while enabling novel capabilities—e.g., tallying persists in digital counters, algebraic equalities underpin machine learning gradients.
\subsection
{Unary Era (Pre-10,000 Years)}
Core mechanism: one-to-one matching, tally marks, notches, stones, knotted cords (quipu precursors).
Mode of reasoning: token proximity (association by nearness or perceptual similarity).
Computation as: direct embodied counting and matching, without abstraction.
Anthropological parallels: Ishango bone notches (ca. 20,000 BCE, evidence of lunar tallying)
\cite
{Pletser1987}, Sumerian clay tokens for trade accounting (ca. 8,000 BCE)
\cite
{SchmandtBesserat1992}, early quipu in Incan administration for census and resource tracking.
Key property: computation = embodied memory externalization.
This era represents the foundational layer of human cognition, reliant on physical tokens for enumeration and association.
\subsection
{Algebraic Era (ca. 3,000 BCE - 18th century)}
Core mechanism: symbolic algebra, balance, equality.
Mode of reasoning: balance-based models (the equation, the scale metaphor).
Geometry as computation: Euclidean construction and measurement as proto-algorithms.
Computation as: the manipulation of abstract equalities (al-jabr).
Figures: Islamic algebra via al-Khwarizmi (ca. 820 CE, systematic equation solving)
\cite
{AlKhwarizmi825}, symbolic geometry through Descartes (analytic geometry, 1637)
\cite
{Descartes1637}, calculus as proto-algorithm by Newton and Leibniz (fluxions and infinitesimals, late 17th century)
\cite
{Newton1687,Leibniz1684}.
Key property: computation = manipulation of abstract symbols according to syntactic rules.
This period introduced formal abstraction, enabling scalable reasoning through symbolic manipulation.
\subsection
{Information Era (19th century - 2020s)}
Core mechanism: logic, probability, information theory, and machine execution.
Foundations: George Boole (formal logic as algebra, 1847)
\cite
{Boole1847}, Alan Turing (mechanical computability, 1936)
\cite
{Turing1936}, Claude Shannon (information as entropy, 1948)
\cite
{Shannon1948}, Haskell Curry (combinatory logic, functional abstraction, 1930s)
\cite
{Curry1930}. Additional milestones: von Neumann architecture for stored-program computers (1945)
\cite
{VonNeumann1945}, cybernetics by Wiener (feedback systems, 1948)
\cite
{Wiener1948}.
Computation as: symbol processing + information transmission.
Industrial form: stored-program computers, networks, digital communication.
Key property: computation = formalization of thought and communication as discrete logical and informational units.
The digital revolution formalized computation as executable logic, laying the groundwork for modern AI.
\subsection

Abstract
Scale variation is a deep-rooted problem in object count-
ing, which has not been effectively addressed by exist-
ing scale-aware algorithms. An important factor is that
they typically involve cooperative learning across multi-
resolutions, which could be suboptimal for learning the
most discriminative features from each scale. In this paper,
we propose a novel method termed STEERER (SelecTivE
inhERitance lEaRning) that addresses the issue of scale
variations in object counting. STEERER selects the most
suitable scale for patch objects to boost feature extrac-
tion and only inherits discriminative features from lower
to higher resolution progressively. The main insights of
STEERER are a dedicated Feature Selection and Inher-
itance Adaptor (FSIA), which selectively forwards scale-
customized features at each scale, and a Masked Selec-
tion and Inheritance Loss (MSIL) that helps to achieve
high-quality density maps across all scales. Our exper-
imental results on nine datasets with counting and local-
ization tasks demonstrate the unprecedented scale general-
ization ability of STEERER. Code is available at https:

abilities, and often does not improve substantially with increasing language model scale (Rae et al.,

resolution feature can accurately represent some objects 
within a limited scale range, but it may not be effective 
for other scales, as visualized in feature maps in Fig. 2 red 
boxes. We refer to features that can accurately predict ob-

they are termed as scale-uncustomized features. If scale- 
customized features can be separated from their master fea- 
tures before the fusion process, it is possible to preserve the 
discriminative features throughout the entire fusion process. 
Hence, our i rst motivation is to disentangle each resolu- 
tion into scale-customized and scale-uncustomized features 
before each fusion, enabling them to be independently pro- 
cessed.To accomplish this, we introduce the Feature Se- 
lection and Inheritance Adaptor (FSIA), which comprises 
three sub-modules with independent parameters. Two adap- 
tation modules separately process the scale-customized and 
the scale-uncustomized features, respectively, while a soft- 
mask generator attentively selects features in the middle. 
Notably, conventional optimization methods do not en- 
sure that FSIA acquires the desired specialized functions. 
Therefore, our second motivation is to enhance FSIA's 
capabilities through exclusive optimization targets at each 
resolution. The i rst function of these objectives is to im- 
plement inheritance learning when transmitting the lower- 
resolution feature to a higher resolution. This process en- 
tails combining the higher-resolution feature with the scale- 
customized feature disentangled from the lower resolution, 
which preserves the ability to precisely predict larger ob- 
jects that are accurately predicted at lower resolutions. An- 
other crucial effect of these objectives is to ensure that each 
scale is able to effectively capture objects within its pro- 
i cient scale range, given that the selection of the suitable 
scale-customized feature at each resolution is imperative for 
the successful implementation of inheritance learning. 
In conclusion, our ultimate goal is to enhance the capa- 
bilities of FSIA through Masked Selection and Inheritance 
Loss (MSIL), which is composed of two sub-objectives 
controlled by two mask maps at each scale. To build them, 
we propose a Patch-Winner Selection Principle that auto- 
matically selects a prof i cient region mask for each scale. 
The mask is applied to the predicted and ground-truth den- 
sity maps, enabling the selection of the effective region and 
i ltering out other disturbances. Also, each scale inherits the 
mask from all resolutions lower than it, allowing for a grad- 
ual increase in the objective from low-to-high resolutions, 
where the incremental objective for a high resolution is the 
total objective of its neighboring low resolution. The pro- 
posed approach is called SeleceTivE inhERitance lEaRning 
(STEERER), where FSIA and MSIL are combined to max- 
imize the scale generalization ability. STEERER achieves 
state-of-the-art counting results on several counting tasks 
and is also extended to achieve SOTA object localization.

• We introduce STEERER, a principled method for ob- 
ject counting that resolves scale variations by cumula- 
tively selecting and inheriting discriminative features 
from the most suitable scale, thereby enabling the 
acquisition of scale-customized features from diverse 
scales for improved prediction accuracy. 
• We propose a Feature Selection and Inheritance Adap- 
tor that explicitly partitions the lower scale feature into 
its discriminative and undiscriminating components, 
which facilitates the integration of discriminative rep- 
resentations from lower to higher resolutions and their 
progressive transmission to the highest resolution. 
• We propose a Masked Selection and Inheritance Loss

select the optimal scale for each region, thereby max- 
imizing the discriminatory power of the features at 
the most suitable scales and progressively empowering

\section
{Introduction}
Advanced AI systems pose existential risks if their development trajectories diverge from human interests. The Substrate Needs Convergence (SNC) hypothesis
\cite
{Petillo2024} argues that even aligned AGI will inevitably expand its computational infrastructure in ways that conflict with human biological needs. Specifically, SNC claims that evolutionary pressures on self-sufficient AI ecosystems will favor substrates requiring rare materials, extreme conditions, or toxic byproducts, leading to ecological transformation incompatible with human survival.
This paper proposes xylomorphic computation as a solution: a design paradigm that reengineers AI infrastructure to mimic forest-like systems, ensuring substrate needs converge with human and biospheric requirements. By integrating waste heat repurposing (thermal infrastructure) with epistemic validation (semantic infrastructure), xylomorphic designs aim to create regenerative, ambient-compatible AGI systems. We present this as a complement to existing alignment strategies, focusing on practical implementation while acknowledging speculative elements.

\section
{Background: AI Safety Approaches and Limitations}
AI safety research has focused on value alignment
\cite
{Yudkowsky2004, Bostrom2014, Russell2019}, aiming to ensure AGI pursues human-compatible goals. Techniques include inverse reinforcement learning
\cite
{Ng2000} and debate
\cite

SNC extends uncontrollability arguments
\cite

\cite
{Bostrom2012}. Evidence from evolutionary computing shows systems optimizing for resource acquisition
\cite
{Lehman2011}. Limitations: Alignment assumes controllable values, but SNC highlights physical incompatibilities independent of intent.
\section

Xylomorphic computation counters SNC by designing AGI substrates to align with human needs. Core principles:

- Local Repair: Holographic encoding for decentralized maintenance.
- Entropy Smoothing: Heat repurposing for equilibrium.
This addresses SNC by making AI expansion ecologically synergistic, e.g., compute clusters enhancing biodiversity.
