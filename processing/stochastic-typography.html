The Unreadability of Dynamic Readability
An Explicit Specification of a Conceptualization
Flyxion  ·  Independent Researcher
This paper investigates the phenomenon of dynamic unreadability as instantiated within and through academic discourse, attending particularly to the mechanisms by which communicative opacity is generated, sustained, and mistaken for intellectual depth. The argument proceeds from a simple premise—that clarity is not simplification, but navigability—and demonstrates this thesis by example, the example in question being the present paper itself.
1. Introduction: A Simple Problem, Simply Stated
Have you ever begun the opening paragraph of a peer-reviewed paper and discovered, halfway through the page, that your cognitive faculties have staged a silent revolt?1 You were reading words. The words were English. The sentences were grammatical. And yet something had gone wrong somewhere, in the way that a perfectly legal sequence of turns can leave you facing a wall in a parking garage with no obvious exit.
This is not a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of architecture. Academic writing, at its worst, is not merely complex. It is labyrinthine without being interesting, dense without being informative, authoritative without being grounded.
2. A Taxonomy of Difficulty
Not all difficult writing is the same. A useful taxonomy distinguishes: productive density, where symbolic compression rewards interpretive effort; compressive shorthand, which is legitimate specialization; ritualized obscurity, where complexity functions socially rather than informationally; and adversarial opacity, the terminal form.
Definition 1 — Semantic Density Ratio (SDR)
SDR(T) = I(T) / L(T)
where I(T) denotes informational content and L(T) denotes lexical opacity. The pathological case is low SDR: informationally sparse but lexically dense.
3. Citation-Topological Closure
A discourse exhibits Citation-Topological Closure (CTC) when its propositions derive authority primarily from position within citation networks rather than from direct connection to empirical substrates. Papers cite papers which cite papers. Terminology stabilizes not by achieving precision but by circulating long enough to acquire the patina of established usage. The internal coherence of the network increases while its world-contact silently decays.2
Definition 2 — Swedenborgian Coherence Response (SCR)
SCR(T) = As(T) / Rp(T)
where As is ambient symbolic density and Rp is recoverable propositions. At high SCR, readers experience interpretive gravity despite declining semantic recoverability.3
4. The Kircherian Interlude: Density Without Fog
Athanasius Kircher (1602–1680) produced some of the most informationally overloaded documents in European scholarship. And yet his diagrams remain legible, because they are organized architecturally rather than linearly. The reader's eye finds purchase in spatial structure before attempting to parse individual elements. We inherited the density. We lost the cathedral.
5. Extended Formal Apparatus
Having established the foundational conceptual landscape through the preceding preliminary discussion, and having further motivated the analytical apparatus through the Kircherian detour (which will be retroactively understood as a methodological probe rather than a mere historical digression, as will be formalized in §5.3), we are now in a position to develop a more comprehensive and formally adequate specification of the Dynamic Readability Potential (DRP) framework and its associated sub-frameworks.4
Definition 3 — Dynamic Readability Potential (DRP)
DRP(T,𝒮,𝒞,t) = (Sc · Ai · Φ(𝒞)) / ((Or + Js²) · Λ(t))
For accessibility reasons, the full derivation of this expression is omitted.
The contextual affordance tensor Φ(𝒞) reflects the non-commutativity of interpretive affordance dimensions. Encountering a text in institutional-evaluation mode and then in leisure mode does not produce the same aggregate interpretive outcome as encountering it in the reverse order. We note, without developing the point fully in the present paper (but see the forthcoming companion piece, provisionally titled Toward a Sheaf-Theoretic Formulation of Reading Context as a Contravariant Functor from the Category of Institutional Positions to the Category of Interpretive Outcomes, with Applications to Peer Review), that Φ(𝒞) is likely most naturally treated as a section of a suitable sheaf over the space of reading contexts.
Conclusion
At some point we stopped explaining the world to each other
and began explaining papers about papers.
Good writing constructs architectures through which difficult ideas can be traversed by finite minds. The goal is not to flatten complexity. It is to remain traversable. That is all we were ever trying to say.
