\begin{abstract}
This text presents the ancient park as a system of flows. Each chamber,
path, and threshold is treated as a transformation that carries visitors
from one state to another. The structures do not stand as objects; they
arise as continuities in motion. The patterns discovered in the surviving
remnants reveal the geometry by which the parks shaped perception.
\end{abstract}

% ===========================================================
\section{Origin of the Flow}
% ===========================================================

At the first entrance, the visitor crossed from ordinary pace into the
park’s internal rhythm. This crossing is the fundamental transformation:

\[
E : \text{outer state} \longrightarrow \text{inner state}.
\]

Its effect was not to create a boundary but to reweight attention. The
world outside receded. The world inside gathered curvature. The entrance
was not a gate; it was a redirection.

The structures that followed did not impose a path. They induced one.
Movement thickened in some regions, thinned in others. Visitors discovered
their trajectories by feeling the gradients around them. Much of the park’s
logic can be reconstructed by tracing these gradients.

% ===========================================================
\section{Curvatures of Passage}
% ===========================================================

A path is defined not by its length but by the changes it induces. The
ancient designers shaped paths through controlled variation of curvature.
Let a segment of passage be represented as:

\[
\gamma(t), \qquad t \in [0,1],
\]

with curvature field $k(t)$ governing attention.

When $k(t)$ increased, the visitor leaned inward, anticipating. When $k(t)$
softened, attention spread outward, releasing. The alternation of these
states formed the basic pulse of movement.

Two structures dominated these passages:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Contractions}, where curvature tightened and motion aligned.
\item \textbf{Expansions}, where curvature relaxed and the field opened.
\end{itemize}

These alternated with deliberate regularity, creating a rhythm more felt
than seen.

\section{Chambers as Transformations}
% ===========================================================

Each chamber enacted a transformation:

\[
C_i : S_{i-1} \longrightarrow S_i
\]

where $S_i$ denotes the visitor’s internal state upon exiting.

The chamber did not convey a message. It performed an operation. Light
shifted; sound reoriented; space widened or contracted. These were not
decorations but conditions that guided transition.

Ancient notes show that designers tuned chambers to produce clean
transitions, meaning:

\[
C_{i+1} \circ C_i
\]

aligned in slope, so that no chamber contradicted the momentum of the one
before. The sequence of chambers thus formed a continuous arc.

\section{Thresholds}
% ===========================================================

The threshold was the moment when all prior gradients converged. Its form
can be expressed as:

\[
T : \lim_{\epsilon \to 0^+} S(t-\epsilon) \to S(t+\epsilon).
\]

The threshold magnified this change. A corridor narrowed slightly to focus
attention; a ceiling lifted to expand it; a sound rose or fell. Each
threshold held a precise ratio between contraction and release.

Visitors rarely spoke of thresholds directly. They described instead the
feeling of “entering something larger.” The structure that produced this
feeling was the threshold’s true geometry.

\section{The Field of Wonder}
% ===========================================================

Wonder arose not through surprise but through alignment of gradients. When
spatial curvature, emotional pressure, and narrative tension converged, the
visitor experienced a sudden expansion in the field of perception.

Formally:

\[
W = \{ x \mid \nabla k(x) + \nabla e(x) + \nabla n(x) = 0 \},
\]

where $k$ is spatial curvature, $e$ emotional density, and $n$ narrative
slope. At such points, the system’s total gradient vanished, leaving the
visitor momentarily unweighted.

This vanishing produced the spaciousness described in ancient accounts: the
sensation of stepping into a world larger than one’s own.

\section{Nightfall and Reweighting}
% ===========================================================

When darkness came, the park’s logic shifted. Illumination redrew the
fields. New curvatures appeared; old ones softened.

Let $L_d$ denote daylight illumination and $L_n$ denote night illumination.
Then the transformation at dusk can be written:

\[
N : L_d \mapsto L_n,
\]

with the effect that boundaries inverted. What had been structural by day
became atmospheric by night. What had guided movement now guided attention.

Reflections doubled the field, creating symmetric extensions. The visitor
moved not toward objects but toward surfaces where light slowed.

Night was not a separate experience. It was the park’s second form.
