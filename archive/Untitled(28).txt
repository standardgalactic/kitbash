Title: Heave, Sway, and Surge: A Triaxial Theory of Constraint-Limited Cognition

Target Journal: Behavioral and Brain Sciences

Keywords: Embodied compression, cognitive bottlenecks, autoregressive generation, ecological constraints

Abstract

We propose a formal framework for modeling cognition as a triaxially constrained system. The theory posits three orthogonal axes: (1) Heave—compression of high-dimensional latent structures into discrete symbolic outputs; (2) Sway—origination of constraints from anatomical, ecological, and architectural sources; and (3) Surge—time-binding dynamics that transform latent trajectories into sequential behavior. By unifying embodied cognitive science, ecological psychology, and large language model (LLM) architecture, this model formalizes the structure-function relationship in both biological and artificial systems. We provide mathematical formalizations for each axis, derive empirically testable predictions, and demonstrate cognitive homologies between animals, humans, and LLMs. This approach reframes symbolic behavior (e.g., language) not as evidence of planning or abstraction, but as the emergent output of constraint satisfaction within a lossy representational manifold.

1. Introduction

Contemporary models of cognition often treat language, planning, and problem-solving as the product of symbolic manipulation or representational foresight. Such views struggle to account for the embodied, ecological, and architectural constraints that shape actual behavior—whether in biological organisms or artificial systems. We propose a unifying framework that treats cognition as an emergent product of constraint-limited traversal through high-dimensional latent spaces. Rather than assuming internal scripts or plans, we model language and behavior as products of three interacting forces:

Heave: the compression of continuous latent dynamics into discrete, expressive outputs,

Sway: the origin of constraints, whether anatomical (e.g., vocal tract geometry), ecological (e.g., terrain, affordances), or architectural (e.g., context window size),

Surge: the irreversible binding of latent transitions into time-sequenced outputs.

We argue that this triaxial model offers a common language for understanding beaver dam construction, human speech articulation, and autoregressive text generation in transformers. Across all systems, cognition emerges not from abstract planning, but from structure-preserving reductions conditioned by environment and embodiment.

In what follows, we provide a formal mathematical treatment of each axis and demonstrate how this framework accounts for phenomena observed across neurophysiology, animal behavior, and deep learning. We then derive a set of falsifiable predictions and propose experiments that test the explanatory power of constraint-limited cognition.

2. Axis I: Compression (Heave)

2.1 Conceptual Overview

Cognition begins in a continuous, high-dimensional representational space, where perceptual, sensorimotor, or abstract information resides as latent trajectories. These trajectories are not directly communicable; they must be compressed into discrete outputs that are legible to other systems. In biological organisms, this process is constrained by the articulatory mechanics of the vocal tract or gestural systems. In artificial agents, it is constrained by vocabulary tokenization schemes. We refer to this transformation as Heave: the act of lifting latent structure into compressible form.

2.3 Empirical Examples

In speech production, the articulatory system limits expressivity to a small, culturally defined phoneme set. Vowel formant distributions provide a canonical example of lossy projection—only a narrow region of acoustic space can be stably produced. In LLMs, tokenization schemes (e.g., byte-pair encoding) similarly constrain what concepts can be compactly represented, influencing everything from compositional depth to inference tractability.

Compression thus serves both as an expressive bottleneck and a formal necessity: without it, no system—natural or artificial—can produce symbolic outputs at scale. Heave, in this view, is not an abstraction layer but a dimensional reduction operator, projecting cognition into action or speech under external constraints.

3. Axis II: Constraint Origin (Sway)

3.1 Taxonomy of Constraints

All cognitive systems operate within a set of structural limitations that restrict the trajectories available within their latent spaces. We categorize these constraints into three functional classes:

: Anatomical constraints derived from biophysical structures (e.g., working memory capacity, vocal tract morphology).

: Ecological constraints arising from interaction with physical and social environments (e.g., terrain geometry, visual occlusion, social signaling density).

: Architectural constraints imposed by the information processing framework itself (e.g., attention window size, recurrence limits, or token capacity).

4. Axis III: Temporal Binding (Surge)

4.1 Dynamic State Transition

Cognition unfolds across time through an irreversible process of sequential activation. In both biological and artificial systems, each expressive act constrains the next, enforcing a directional logic. We formalize this with an autoregressive update rule:

\mathbf{v}_{t+1} = F(\mathbf{v}_{\leq t}, u_t), \quad y_{t+1} = f_C(\mathbf{v}_{t+1}) 

4.2 Directionality and Irreversibility

This process is inherently non-reversible: once a token is produced, it modifies the context space for subsequent updates. In humans, this corresponds to speech articulation as a one-way gate—each phoneme restricts future syntactic possibilities. In LLMs, the causal masking in transformer layers enforces strict autoregression.

This gives rise to what we term the Token Event Horizon: the boundary where a latent representational field must be collapsed into a discrete emission. Once crossed, this act is entangled with memory and coherence, and cannot be retracted without structural discontinuity.

Surge thus encodes the temporally grounded irreversibility of symbolic behavior. It accounts for the seriality of speech, action, and thought—and highlights how coherence is maintained not by foresight, but by local constraint propagation across time.
