\begin{abstract}
This monograph unifies three long-separated scientific lineages: (1) curvature-induced mass and symmetry breaking without fundamental Higgs fields, (2) global constrained optimization laws governing living systems, and (3) evolutionary disappearance dynamics driven by asymmetric, constrained admixture. We historicize each stream from its earliest conceptual roots, formalize their mathematical correspondences, and synthesize them into a non-expansion cosmology consistent with constraint-dominated relaxation. The results support an interpretation in which mass, life, and lineage extinction arise through globally bounded optimization on curved constraint manifolds rather than micro-local symmetry breaking alone. This structure mirrors core principles of the RSVP paradigm, where fields evolve by global entropic descent rather than local inflationary forcing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Scope}
% ===========================

Modern physics, biology, and evolutionary theory were independently shaped by an assumption of \textit{local} causal sufficiency. Mass emerged from pointwise symmetry breaking \citep{Higgs1964}. Growth laws emerged from nutrient conditions \citep{Monod1949}. Population outcomes emerged from local fitness landscapes \citep{Fisher1930, Wright1931}.  

Only recently have all three domains exhibited signs of a paradigm shift:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Mass may be \textit{geometric}, not local, emerging from global curvature constraints \citep{Pincak2025, Joyce2000, Bryant2006}.
    \item Growth laws appear to be consequences of \textit{global constraint duality}, not local metabolic kinetics \citep{Yamagishi2025}.
    \item Lineage replacement can be driven by \textit{persistent directional admixture under constraints}, not competitive fitness \citep{Amadei2025}.
\end{enumerate}

Each domain is revealing a world ruled less by local rules and more by global feasibility boundaries. This document traces the roots of this shift and shows their mathematical convergence.

\section{Historical Precursors to Global Constraint Thinking}
% ===========================

\subsection{Early thermodynamic and variational views}
The deepest origin lies in variational physics. Hamilton framed mechanics as extremization of action \citep{Hamilton1834}, giving the first indication that dynamics belong to extremal global objects rather than local rules. Later, Boltzmann \citep{Boltzmann1877} interpreted physical law as a statistical drift toward entropy maxima, and Gibbs \citep{Gibbs1902} formalized constrained ensembles, laying the groundwork for modern Lagrange multiplier interpretations of physical observables.

\subsection{Constraints in biology}
Biology adopted constraints much later. Liebig’s principle \citep{Liebig1840} established that growth is limited by the scarcest resource, but did not treat it as a formal dual variable. Monod \citep{Monod1949} introduced saturating growth curves but still assumed local kinetics. Only in the 21st century did constraint duality return via optimization theory \citep{Yamagishi2025}.

\subsection{Evolutionary constraint mathematics}
Evolutionary theory briefly flirted with global constraints via Sewall Wright’s adaptive landscapes \citep{Wright1931}, then reverted to gene-local selection. The formal mathematics of branching processes \citep{Kolmogorov1938} and later neutral theory \citep{Kimura1968} began to show how large-scale outcomes could be constraint-dominated rather than fitness-dominated.

\subsection{Constraint geometry in physics}
General relativity introduced curvature as dynamics \citep{Einstein1915}, but the idea of curvature as \textit{mass generator} rather than mass as curvature source only appeared much later in G$_2$ geometry \citep{Joyce2000, Bryant2006} and more recently in Ricci-flow mass mechanisms \citep{Pincak2025}.

\section{Evolution Under Constraint-Driven Replacement}
% ===========================

Amadei et al. \citeyearpar{Amadei2025} prove that Neanderthal replacement requires no fitness deficit, provided:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Continuous low-rate admixture from a larger source population
    \item Reproductive compatibility
    \item Demographic asymmetry
\end{enumerate}

Let $p_t$ be the fraction of indigenous ancestry. Then even under neutrality:

\begin{equation}
p_{t+1} = p_t(1 - \epsilon) + \delta
\end{equation}

with $\epsilon$ exogenous influx and $\delta$ symmetric reproduction, leads to extinction for any $\epsilon > 0$:

\begin{equation}
\lim_{t \to \infty} p_t = 0
\end{equation}

Thus:

\begin{quote}
Extinction can be caused by boundary conditions rather than competitive disadvantage.
\end{quote}

Again, the outcome is constraint-dominated, not interaction-dominated.

\section{Appendix A — Mathematical Foundations and Derivations}
% ==========================================================

This appendix provides complete derivations of the core mathematical claims used in the RSVP framework. We formalize well-posedness of the constrained gradient flow, the spectral properties of linearized dynamics, the mass–curvature dual mapping, and the asymptotics of constraint-dilution systems.

% ==========================================================
\subsection{A.1 Functional Setting and Well-Posedness}
% ==========================================================

We take the field space to be

\[
X = H^1(\mathcal{M}) \times H(\mathrm{curl}, \mathcal{M}) \times L^1(\mathcal{M}) \times \mathbb{R}^k
\]

where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \( \Phi \in H^1(\mathcal{M}) \)
    \item \( \mathbf{v} \in H(\mathrm{curl}) = \{\mathbf{u} \in L^2: \nabla \times \mathbf{u} \in L^2\}\)
    \item \( S \in L^1(\mathcal{M}), S \ge 0\) a.e.
    \item \( \boldsymbol{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^k_{\ge 0} \)
\end{itemize}

The energy functional is

\[
\mathcal{E}[X] = \int_{\mathcal{M}} \left(
\frac{1}{2} \lVert \nabla \Phi \rVert^2 +
\frac{1}{4} \lVert F_{\mu\nu} \rVert^2 +
\beta S \ln S
- \gamma \Phi \nabla_\mu v^\mu
\right) dV
+ \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k \mathcal{C}_k(X)
\]

\subsubsection{Variational derivatives}

Using standard Euler–Lagrange calculus on Sobolev spaces \citep{Evans1998}, we compute:

\[
\frac{\delta \mathcal{E}}{\delta \Phi} = -\nabla^2 \Phi - \gamma \nabla_\mu v^\mu + \sum_k \lambda_k \frac{\delta \mathcal{C}_k}{\delta \Phi}
\]

\[
\frac{\delta \mathcal{E}}{\delta v^\mu} = -\nabla_\nu F^{\nu\mu} + \gamma \nabla^\mu \Phi + \sum_k \lambda_k \frac{\delta \mathcal{C}_k}{\delta v^\mu}
\]

\[
\frac{\delta \mathcal{E}}{\delta S} = \beta(1 + \ln S) + \sum_k \lambda_k \frac{\delta \mathcal{C}_k}{\delta S}
\]

\subsubsection{Descent dynamics}

The primal–dual gradient flow is

\[
\partial_t X = - \nabla_X \mathcal{E}, \qquad
\partial_t \lambda_k = \alpha_k \mathcal{C}_k(X), \quad
\lambda_k \ge 0
\]

\subsubsection{Existence and boundedness}

\begin{theorem}[Global existence]
If each \( \mathcal{C}_k(X) \) is convex and weakly lower semicontinuous, then the constrained gradient flow admits a unique global solution \( X(t) \in X \) for all \( t \ge 0 \).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Energy coercivity follows from

\[
\mathcal{E} \ge c_1 \|\nabla \Phi\|_2^2 + c_2 \|F\|_2^2 + c_3 \int S \ln S \, dV - C
\]

which prevents blowup of the \(H^1 \times H(\mathrm{curl}) \times L^1\) norm. Lipschitz continuity of the gradient map on bounded sets ensures well-posedness via standard monotone operator theory \citep{Brezis1973}.
\end{proof}

% ==========================================================
\subsection{A.2 Linear Stability and Spectrum}
% ==========================================================

Linearize around a fixed point \(X^\ast\):

\[
\partial_t \delta X = -H \, \delta X, \qquad
H = D^2 \mathcal{E}|_{X^\ast}
\]

The Hessian has block structure:

\[
H = \begin{bmatrix}
-\nabla^2 + C_{\Phi} & -\gamma \nabla & 0 \\
\gamma \nabla & -\nabla \times \nabla \times + C_v & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \beta S^{-1} + C_S
\end{bmatrix} + J_\lambda
\]

where \(J_\lambda\) encodes dual constraint curvature.

\begin{theorem}[Stability criterion]
A fixed point is Lyapunov stable if and only if all eigenvalues of \(H\) satisfy
\[
\mathrm{Re}(\sigma(H)) > 0
\]
after quotienting zero modes associated with gauge symmetries.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The flow is a gradient system; thus the spectrum of \(H\) determines monotone energy descent. The only allowable zero eigenvalues correspond to divergence-free vector fields and constant shifts in \(\Phi\), both factored out by symmetry.
\end{proof}

% ==========================================================
\subsection{A.3 Mass as Curvature Dual Variable}
% ==========================================================

In differential geometry, the second variation of total scalar curvature yields:

\[
\delta^2 \int R \, dV = \int \langle h, \Delta_L h \rangle \, dV, \qquad \Delta_L = \nabla^2 + 2\,\mathrm{Rm} * (\cdot)
\]

On a \(G_2\) manifold, contraction with the symmetry-breaking vector \(v^\mu\) yields the mass operator:

\[
m^2 = \alpha \frac{\delta}{\delta \Phi} \int R \sqrt{-g} \, dV = \alpha\, R_{\mu\nu} v^\mu v^\nu
\]

recovering the geometric mass relation seen in G$_2$ Ricci-flow mass generation \citep{Pincak2025}.

\begin{corollary}
Mass eigenvalues correspond to discrete Ricci spectra when \(\mathcal{M}\) is compact with G$_2$ holonomy.
\end{corollary}

% ==========================================================
\subsection{A.4 Entropic Redshift from Mode Relaxation}
% ==========================================================

Let momentum modes decay by entropy gradient descent:

\[
\partial_t k = -\frac{\partial}{\partial k}\left( S(k) + \sum_k \lambda_k \mathcal{C}_k \right)
\]

Near equilibrium \(S(k) \approx a k^2\), giving

\[
\partial_t k = -2 a k + O(k^3)
\]

\[
k(t) = k_0 e^{-2 a t} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \nu(t) = \nu_0 e^{-2 a t}
\]

This produces exponential spectral softening rather than metric dilation, distinguishing RSVP cosmology from FLRW expansion \citep{Turok2012}.

% ==========================================================
\subsection{A.5 Constraint Dilution and Extinction}
% ==========================================================

Let ancestry fraction \(p(t)\) obey dual descent:

\[
\partial_t p = -\frac{\partial}{\partial p} \left[ D(p) + \lambda \mathcal{C}(p) \right]
\]

For dilute biased influx, take

\[
D(p) = 0, \qquad \mathcal{C}(p) = (p - p_\infty)^2, \quad \lambda = \epsilon
\]

yielding

\[
\partial_t p = -2 \epsilon (p - p_\infty)
\]

with solution

\[
p(t) = p_\infty + (p_0 - p_\infty)e^{-2\epsilon t}
\]

Under unidirectional replacement \(p_\infty=0\),

\[
\boxed{p(t) = p_0 e^{-2\epsilon t}}
\]

matching the neutral extinction tail predicted in \citet{Amadei2025}.

\subsection{A.6 Convergence of the Primal–Dual Solver}
% ==========================================================

The update rule:

\[
X^{n+1} = X^n - \eta \nabla_X \mathcal{E}(X^n), \qquad
\lambda^{n+1} = [\lambda^n + \alpha \mathcal{C}(X^{n+1})]_+
\]

is a projected gradient method. Convergence follows if:

\[
\eta < \frac{2}{L_H}, \qquad \alpha < \frac{1}{\|\nabla \mathcal{C}\|^2}
\]

where \(L_H\) is the Hessian Lipschitz constant \citep{Boyd2004}. Under these conditions:

\[
\|X^n - X^\ast\| \le O(e^{-ct})
\]

for some \(c>0\).

\subsection{B.2 Pseudocode — Primal–Dual Constrained Relaxation}
% ==========================================================

\begin{verbatim}
# Pseudocode: primal-dual constrained relaxation (single time step)
# Inputs: state X = (Phi, v, S), duals lambda, dt, hyperparams eta, alpha
# Outputs: updated (X, lambda)

def primal_dual_step(X, lambda, dt, eta, alpha):
    # 1. Compute local gradient of energy
    grad_Phi, grad_v, grad_S = compute_local_gradients(X)

    # 2. Explicit descent (predictor)
    Phi_tilde = X.Phi - eta * grad_Phi
    v_tilde   = X.v   - eta * grad_v
    S_tilde   = X.S   - eta * grad_S

    # 3. Project duals (ascent step)
    for k, C in enumerate(constraints):
        val = C.value(Phi_tilde, v_tilde, S_tilde)
        lambda[k] = max(0, lambda[k] + alpha * val)

    # 4. Correct primal via constraint gradients (proximal correction)
    correction_Phi = sum(lambda[k] * C.grad_Phi(Phi_tilde, v_tilde, S_tilde) for k in constraints)
    correction_v   = sum(lambda[k] * C.grad_v(      ...)                             for k in constraints)
    correction_S   = sum(lambda[k] * C.grad_S(      ...)                             for k in constraints)

    Phi_next = Phi_tilde - eta * correction_Phi
    v_next   = v_tilde   - eta * correction_v
    S_next   = prox_entropy(S_tilde)  # e.g., ensure positivity, mass conservation

    return FieldState(Phi_next, v_next, S_next), lambda
\end{verbatim}

Notes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use line-search or adaptive \(\eta\) if energy increases.
  \item For stiff diffusion terms, treat diffusion implicitly (see Section B.4).
  \item \texttt{prox\_entropy} implements the proximal map for the \(\beta S\ln S\) term, ensuring \(S>0\).
\end{itemize}

% ==========================================================
\subsection{B.3 Finite-Difference Schemes (FD)}
% ==========================================================

Grid: uniform Cartesian grid in 1D/2D/3D. Indexing \(i,j,k\). Grid spacing \(h\). Time-step \(dt\).

Operators (second-order centered):

\[
(\nabla \Phi)_{i,j,k} \approx \frac{\Phi_{i+1}-\Phi_{i-1}}{2h}
\]
\[
(\Delta \Phi)_{i,j,k} \approx \frac{\Phi_{i+1}+ \Phi_{i-1} + \Phi_{j+1} + \Phi_{j-1} + \Phi_{k+1} + \Phi_{k-1} - 6\Phi_{i,j,k}}{h^2}
\]

Boundary conditions: periodic for cosmology, Neumann or Dirichlet for biological domains.

Time integrators:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Explicit Euler for non-stiff terms (small dt).
  \item Crank–Nicolson / implicit for diffusion: solve \((I - \frac{\nu dt}{2} L)\Phi^{n+1} = (I + \frac{\nu dt}{2} L)\Phi^n + \text{RHS}\).
  \item IMEX (implicit-explicit) splitting: implicit on linear diffusion, explicit on nonlinear nonlinearities and constraint corrections.
\end{itemize}

Stability condition (explicit diffusion):

\[
dt \le \frac{h^2}{2d \nu}
\]

where \(d\) is spatial dimension.

% ==========================================================
\subsection{B.4 Spectral Methods and FFT-Based Solvers}
% ==========================================================

For periodic domains, represent fields in Fourier basis. Advantages: spectral accuracy and cheap inverse Laplacian.

Key operations (pseudo):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item compute gradients in k-space: \(\widehat{\nabla \Phi} = i \mathbf{k} \, \widehat{\Phi}\).
  \item solve Poisson: \(\widehat{\Psi} = -\widehat{f} / |\mathbf{k}|^2\), handle \(\mathbf{k}=0\) mode separately (mean constraint).
  \item implicit diffusion step via multiplication by \(\exp(-\nu |\mathbf{k}|^2 dt)\).
\end{enumerate}

Spectral integrator (semi-implicit):

\[
\widehat{\Phi}^{n+1} = \frac{\widehat{\Phi}^n + dt \, \widehat{\text{nonlinear}}^n}{1 + dt\, \nu |\mathbf{k}|^2}
\]

Aliasing: use 2/3 dealiasing rule for nonlinear products.

% ==========================================================
\subsection{B.5 Finite Element Methods (FEM)}
% ==========================================================

Use FEM for curved domains and variable coefficients. Recommended stack:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{FEniCS} for Python-based FEM,
  \item choose \(\mathbb{P}_1\) basis for speed, \(\mathbb{P}_2\) for higher accuracy,
  \item implement weak forms: multiply Euler–Lagrange equation by test function, integrate by parts.
\end{itemize}

Example weak form for \(\Phi\):

Find \(\Phi\in V\) such that for all test functions \(w\in V\),

\[
\int \nabla \Phi \cdot \nabla w \, dV + \int \gamma (\nabla\cdot v) w \, dV + \sum_k \lambda_k \langle \delta \mathcal{C}_k/\delta \Phi, w \rangle = \int f w \, dV
\]

Temporal integration: backward Euler or BDF-2 for stiff terms.

% ==========================================================
\subsection{B.6 Implicit–Explicit (IMEX) and Operator Splitting}
% ==========================================================

Recommended IMEX pattern:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Implicit solve for viscous / diffusive linear operators (L) using conjugate gradient or sparse direct solvers.
  \item Explicit evaluation for non-linearities and constraints.
  \item Update duals with projected gradient ascent.
\end{enumerate}

Operator-splitting variant:

\begin{verbatim}
for each time step:
    # Step A: diffusion & geometry implicit solve
    solve (I - dt*L) X_mid = X_old + dt*source_terms

    # Step B: constraint correction (explicit)
    X_next = X_mid - dt * sum_k lambda_k grad_Ck(X_mid)

    # Step C: dual update
    lambda = project_positive(lambda + alpha * C(X_next))
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{B.7 JAX (NumPy-like) Implementation — Example}
% ==========================================================

Below is a minimal JAX-based solver skeleton that demonstrates spectral diffusion and primal–dual updates. Save as \texttt{rsvp/jax\_solver.py}.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={JAX spectral primal–dual solver skeleton (rsvp/jax_solver.py)}]
import jax
import jax.numpy as jnp
from jax import jit, vmap
from functools import partial

# utilities
def fftn(x): return jnp.fft.rfftn(x)
def ifftn(x): return jnp.fft.irfftn(x, s=shape)

# initialize grid and k-vectors (example 2D)
shape = (256,256)
L = 1.0
kx = jnp.fft.rfftfreq(shape[0], d=L/shape[0]) * 2*jnp.pi
ky = jnp.fft.fftfreq(shape[1], d=L/shape[1]) * 2*jnp.pi
KX, KY = jnp.meshgrid(kx, ky, indexing='ij')
K2 = KX**2 + KY**2
K2 = jnp.where(K2==0, 1.0, K2)

@jit
def spectral_diffuse(field_hat, nu, dt):
    # implicit-like spectral damping
    factor = 1.0 / (1.0 + nu * dt * K2)
    return field_hat * factor

@jit
def compute_local_gradients(Phi, v, S):
    # Placeholder: compute gradients in physical space
    grad_Phi = jnp.gradient(Phi)
    grad_v = jnp.gradient(v)  # vectorized
    grad_S = jnp.log(S + 1e-12)  # entropy term derivative
    return grad_Phi, grad_v, grad_S

@jit
def primal_dual_step(Phi, v, S, lambdas, dt, eta, alpha, nu):
    # 1. spectral diffusion step (predictor)
    Phihat = fftn(Phi)
    Phihat = spectral_diffuse(Phihat, nu, dt)
    Phi_tilde = ifftn(Phihat)

    # 2. local gradients
    gPhi, gv, gS = compute_local_gradients(Phi_tilde, v, S)

    # explicit descent
    Phi_tilde = Phi_tilde - eta * gPhi
    v_tilde = v - eta * gv
    S_tilde = S - eta * gS

    # 3. dual ascents (example: one constraint)
    # C = total_entropy - S0
    Cval = jnp.sum(S_tilde) - S0
    lambdas = jnp.maximum(0.0, lambdas + alpha * Cval)

    # 4. constraint correction (approx)
    gradC_Phi = 0.0  # define as needed
    Phi_next = Phi_tilde - eta * lambdas * gradC_Phi
    v_next = v_tilde
    S_next = jnp.maximum(1e-12, S_tilde)  # prox positivity

    return Phi_next, v_next, S_next, lambdas
\end{lstlisting}

Notes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item This skeleton shows the spectral diffusion idea and a simple primal–dual step; extend gradients and constraint functions to match full RSVP Lagrangian.
  \item Keep arrays as \texttt{float32} for GPU memory efficiency, or \texttt{float64} for high-precision tests.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{B.8 PyTorch (GPU) Implementation — Example}
% ==========================================================

PyTorch is convenient for automatic differentiation, batching, and GPU acceleration. Save as \texttt{rsvp/torch\_solver.py}.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={PyTorch IMEX primal–dual solver skeleton (rsvp/torch_solver.py)}]
import torch
import torch.fft as fft

device = torch.device('cuda' if torch.cuda.is_available() else 'cpu')

def spectral_diffuse_torch(field, nu, dt, k2):
    # field: torch tensor [nx,ny]
    fhat = fft.rfft2(field)
    factor = 1.0 / (1.0 + nu * dt * k2)
    fhat = fhat * factor.unsqueeze(-1)  # align dims
    return fft.irfft2(fhat, s=field.shape)

def primal_dual_step_torch(Phi, v, S, lambdas, dt, eta, alpha, nu, constraints):
    Phi = Phi.to(device); v = v.to(device); S = S.to(device)
    # implicit diffusion step
    Phi_mid = spectral_diffuse_torch(Phi, nu, dt, k2)
    # compute gradients via autograd or finite differences
    Phi_mid.requires_grad_(True)
    # Example local gradient (entropy)
    gradS = torch.log(S.clamp(min=1e-12))
    # explicit step
    Phi_tilde = Phi_mid - eta * (torch.autograd.grad(Phi_mid.sum(), Phi_mid, retain_graph=True)[0])
    # ... implement v and S similarly
    # dual update (example)
    Cval = constraints['entropy'].value(S)  # user-defined
    lambdas = torch.clamp(lambdas + alpha * Cval, min=0.0)
    # proximal corrections omitted for brevity
    return Phi_tilde.detach(), v, S.clamp(min=1e-12), lambdas
\end{lstlisting}

Tips:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use \texttt{torch.autograd} for gradients of complex constraint functions.
  \item Keep gradient graphs small; detach after each step to avoid memory blowup.
  \item Use mixed precision (AMP) to accelerate large 3D runs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{B.16 Final Notes and Extensibility}
% ==========================================================

\begin{itemize}
  \item The provided code skeletons prioritize clarity over raw performance. Production runs should replace Python loops with vectorized kernels and exploit library FFTs and sparse solvers.
  \item Integrate checkpointing and restart logic early for long simulations.
  \item Unit tests should cover: operator accuracy, constraint projections, and energy monotonicity.
  \item For collaboration, include a JSON manifest listing exact git hash, container image tag, and parameter sets for any published run.
\end{itemize}
