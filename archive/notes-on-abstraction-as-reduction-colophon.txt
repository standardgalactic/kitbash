This essay, titled "Abstraction as Reduction: A Proof-Theoretic and Computational Perspective," argues that abstraction is not simply hiding or ignoring details, but is fundamentally a form of evaluation and reduction—a computational process that makes systems composable and intelligible.

Core Argument: Abstraction is structurally identical to concepts like execution, normalization, and proof reduction across computational and mathematical frameworks.

Key Points:

1. Abstraction as Innermost Reduction: Like β-reduction in lambda calculus—which resolves innermost expressions first—abstraction conceptually "reduces" a component to a stable, substitutable unit, turning it into a "deliberate normal form."
2. Interfaces as Contracts: In software, interfaces (like type signatures in Haskell) do not just hide details; they create a theory of concern. They define a contract allowing other components to treat an implementation as a "black box" whose internal computation is already resolved.
3. Hardware Parallel - Null Convention Logic: In asynchronous circuits (e.g., dual-rail encoding), abstraction occurs when an incomplete, propagating signal stabilizes into a determinate value. This stable output becomes a composable black box for the next stage, independent of the microscopic timing that produced it.
4. Mereological Ascent: Abstraction involves moving from parts to wholes (e.g., elements to sets, objects to morphisms in category theory). It preserves essential relational structure while discarding irrelevant granularity, choosing the "correct level" of structure.
5. Curry-Howard Correspondence: Under this principle (proofs as programs, programs as proofs), abstraction corresponds to proof normalization. Using a function's type relies on trusting that its internal "proof" has been reduced to a concise certificate of behavior.

Conclusion: The essay synthesizes these perspectives to assert that abstraction is computation. Whether in lambda calculus, type systems, hardware design, or mathematical hierarchies, abstraction marks the successful completion of an evaluation process. It is the moment when internal workings are resolved and reduced into a stable object ready for use in a larger system—not a retreat from implementation, but the sign of its fulfillment.
