\begin{abstract}
Contemporary machine learning, logical inference, and symbolic AI
operate on representational scaffolding---tensors, typed lambda
calculi, graphs, and attention mechanisms---that approximate
structural relations only extrinsically, through indices and
coordinate charts. These encodings treat computation as manipulation
of formal tokens rather than as geometric evolution. In this work we
introduce \emph{Spherepop Calculus}, a primitive computational
formalism whose core operations are given by embeddings, collapses,
and flows on a plenum manifold. We show that many existing AI
formalisms arise as linear or discrete shadows of this calculus, and
that Tensor Logic is a special case corresponding to a flattened
boundary theory.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

There is, at present, no intrinsic geometry underlying the
representational systems of artificial intelligence. Neural networks
propagate vectors through layers whose indices bear no semantic
relation to the phenomena they describe; logical programs manipulate
symbols standing in for entities not embedded in any computational
space; and probabilistic models represent dependencies as graphical
structures imposed \emph{upon} variables rather than arising from
the topology of the world. These systems function, often remarkably
well, but they inherit a fundamental limitation: computation is
defined \emph{over} geometric space instead of \emph{within} it.

By contrast, human cognition, physical processes, and biological
systems perform information processing by exploiting geometry at
every scale. Computation in nature is never disembodied. This
suggests that artificial computation may require a primitive
geometric formalism, not merely a geometric interpretation applied
after the fact.

Spherepop Calculus, or {\sc spc}, constructs computation from
first principles of geometry rather than from symbolic or algebraic
primitive. A computation is an evolving configuration of
\emph{spheres}---region-like scopes---under \emph{pop} operations
that collapse and propagate boundary effects through a surrounding
plenum. This calculus admits a rigorous formulation through derived
geometry and shifted symplectic structure, while providing an
operational semantics akin to lambda calculus. We treat
computation as geometry, rather than geometry as an interpretation
of computation.
