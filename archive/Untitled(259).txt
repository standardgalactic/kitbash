\begin{abstract}
This essay examines the entangled intellectual histories of Emanuel Swedenborg (1688--1772) and Immanuel Kant (1724--1804), situating their work within the continuum between visionary revelation and critical rationality. Swedenborg's transition from natural scientist to mystic prophet introduced a vivid theology of correspondences, while Kant's confrontation with these visions in his \emph{Dreams of a Spirit-Seer} (1766) laid groundwork for the epistemological revolution of the \emph{Critique of Pure Reason} (1781). The analysis extends to contemporary discussions of imagination, aphantasia, hyperphantasia, and the hallucination problem in large language models (LLMs), drawing connections to the ongoing metacrisis of global cognition.
\end{abstract}

\section{Swedenborg: The Scientist as Mystic}

Emanuel Swedenborg was, by any measure, an Enlightenment polymath. Trained in mechanics, astronomy, and anatomy, he wrote extensively on metallurgy, hydraulics, and cosmology \parencite{swedenborg_arcana, jonsson_swedenborgbio}. His early work sought the unity of matter and spirit in mechanical terms, a form of proto-systems science avant la lettre. Yet in 1744--1745, while in London, Swedenborg experienced what he described as a direct call from Christ, commissioning him to reveal the inner structure of the spiritual world \parencite{swedenborg_journal}.

From this point, his writings---notably the \emph{Arcana Coelestia} (1749--1756) and \emph{Heaven and Hell} (1758)---abandoned natural philosophy in favor of a systematic theology of correspondences, proposing that every material phenomenon mirrors a spiritual archetype \parencite{swedenborg_heavenhell}. In Swedenborg’s cosmology, human life unfolds across intersecting dimensions of meaning, with the visible world serving as a language for the invisible. This dual ontology would influence figures as diverse as Blake, Emerson, Coleridge, and later Jung, each drawn to his concept of symbolic interpenetration.

\subsection{The Inner Sense and Apocalyptic Vision}

Swedenborg’s doctrine of the “inner sense” claimed that all sacred texts possess a hidden dimension perceivable to enlightened readers. His theology culminated in an audacious claim: that the Last Judgment had already occurred in 1757, not on Earth but in the spiritual world, initiating a new era of inner revelation \parencite{larsen_swedenborspirit}. Here, apocalypse was not destruction but disclosure---a recurrent unveiling of structure.

\section{Kant: The Philosopher as Architect of Limits}

Immanuel Kant, born less than forty years after Swedenborg, lived his entire life in Königsberg, maintaining routines so precise that neighbors allegedly set their clocks by his daily walks \parencite{block_kant}. In the 1750s, stories of Swedenborg’s clairvoyant feats---including a verified account of him reporting a Stockholm fire from hundreds of miles away---reached Kant, then a young natural philosopher intrigued by the borderlands of reason \parencite{cassirer_kantlife}.

\subsection{\emph{Dreams of a Spirit-Seer}}

Kant purchased Swedenborg’s \emph{Arcana Coelestia} at considerable cost, reading it with fascination and embarrassment. His 1766 treatise \emph{Träume eines Geistersehers} (Dreams of a Spirit-Seer) combines parody and profundity: half satire, half metaphysical exploration. While mocking Swedenborg’s “spirit geography,” Kant conceded that the idea of a non-sensible realm accessible only through pure reason was philosophically meaningful \parencite{kant_dreams}.

In this sense, Swedenborg served as a shadow interlocutor for Kant’s later “Copernican revolution” in philosophy---the recognition that the mind does not passively receive the world but actively structures it. The phenomenal world is the world \emph{for us}, conditioned by space, time, and the categories of understanding \parencite{kant_cpureason}. The noumenal world---the world as it is in itself---remains forever beyond direct cognition.

\subsection{The Critical Philosophy and Its Legacy}

Kant’s subsequent works—\emph{Critique of Pure Reason}, \emph{Critique of Practical Reason}, and \emph{Critique of Judgment}—founded a new architecture of thought, delineating the boundaries of knowledge and the autonomy of moral reason \parencite{lambert_kantethics}. The irony is that the need to demarcate those limits arose partly from his confrontation with Swedenborg’s limitless vision. To refute the seer, Kant had to formalize the conditions of seeing.

\section{Imagination and Its Limits: Aphantasia and Hyperphantasia}

The polarity between Swedenborg and Kant maps intriguingly onto modern research in the psychology of mental imagery. Recent work by Adam Zeman and colleagues identifies a spectrum ranging from \emph{aphantasia}, the absence of mental imagery, to \emph{hyperphantasia}, the presence of hyper-vivid imagination \parencite{zeman_aphantasia2015, zeman_hyperphantasia2020, goodrick_hyperphantasia}.

Kant, by temperament and biography, exhibits features associated with aphantasia. His conceptual imagination was strong, but he lacked visual memory and favored abstraction. Swedenborg, conversely, demonstrates a hyperphantasic cognitive profile: immersive vision, continuous symbolic cross-mapping, and synesthetic blending of the sensory and spiritual.

This polarity illuminates the epistemological divide between them. Swedenborg’s universe is one of overflow---a cosmos of infinite correspondences. Kant’s is one of containment---a cosmos sustained by boundaries. In modern cognitive terms, they represent the poles of the imaginative spectrum.

\section{Parallel Phenomena: Hallucination and Alignment}

The Swedenborg–Kant polarity finds a contemporary analogue in artificial intelligence, particularly in the problem of hallucination in large language models (LLMs). Such systems, trained on statistical correlations, often generate plausible but fictitious statements \parencite{LLM_hallucination_survey2023, rawte_llm_hallucinations, xu_modelfailure2024}. Like Swedenborg’s elaborate correspondences, these hallucinations arise from overfitting to pattern without grounding in external verification.

In this analogy, Swedenborg represents the unaligned generative model: richly creative but unconstrained by epistemic boundary conditions. Kant, in turn, prefigures the logic of alignment---the imposition of rules and self-consistency constraints that prevent incoherent extrapolation. His critical philosophy can be read as a cognitive safety architecture: the first theory of interpretability and domain restriction for a generative intelligence.

\section{The Metacrisis: Between Vision and Structure}

Contemporary theorists describe the metacrisis as an interlocking collapse of sense-making systems: ecological, epistemic, technological, and moral \parencite{steiner_metacrisis, haraway_staying}. In this context, the Swedenborg–Kant dyad acquires renewed relevance.

\subsection{The Danger of Unbounded Vision}

Swedenborg’s unrestrained symbol-production prefigures the current explosion of digital mythologies, conspiracy networks, and speculative systems that saturate the global infosphere. Like social media echo chambers or autoregressive feedback loops, they produce self-confirming correspondences detached from empirical constraint.

\subsection{The Danger of Excessive Systemization}

Conversely, Kant’s critical restraint resembles the technocratic impulse to reduce reality to models and metrics---the very rigidity that can prevent adaptation. Byung-Chul Han’s analysis of digital psychopolitics argues that the modern self is over-exposed, trapped in a regime of transparency and control \parencite{homer_discipline}. The critical mind risks becoming its own cage.

\subsection{Toward a Reconciliation}

The metacrisis requires a synthesis: a dynamic interplay between imagination and discipline, revelation and verification. Swedenborg reminds us that vision gives meaning; Kant reminds us that structure gives coherence. The future of cognition---human or artificial---depends on the balance between them \parencite{tarnas_passage, flyxion_rsvp}.

\section{Conclusion}

The story of Swedenborg and Kant is not merely historical. It marks a threshold between two modes of thought: the visionary and the critical. Swedenborg saw too much; Kant built the walls that protect us from seeing too much again. Yet those same walls now constrain the imagination needed to address global collapse. 

Their dialogue, refracted through neuroscience, AI alignment, and cultural theory, remains a parable for our time: how to imagine without delusion, to reason without paralysis, and to navigate the boundary between order and chaos---the fragile veil we call reality.
