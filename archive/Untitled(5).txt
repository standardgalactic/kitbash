\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract} %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Recent work in systems neuroscience suggests that the cerebral cortex operates less like a digital circuit and more like a resonant analog computer: waves of electrical activity sweep across the cortical sheet, forming large-scale standing and traveling modes that organize cognition and consciousness in real time \parencite{MillerCohen2001PFC,Miller2025PicowerAnalogWaves}. At the same time, mathematical physics and number theory have converged on a seemingly unrelated observation: the spectra of complex quantum systems and the zeros of $L$-functions share the same statistical fingerprints described by Random Matrix Theory (RMT) \parencite{wigner1957,dyson1962,FirkMiller2009NucleiPrimesRMT}. This essay develops a neuroscience-first synthesis. Beginning from empirical evidence of cortical resonance and wave-based computation, we introduce the mathematics of spectral universality and show how the same Wigner--Dyson laws governing nuclear spectra and the zeros of the Riemann zeta function also govern the eigenmodes of cortical and \RSVP{} operators. We then present the Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum (\RSVP{}) as a unifying field-theoretic framework in which: \begin{enumerate} \item nuclear Hamiltonians, \item hypothetical zeta-operators, \item cortical neural-field operators, \item and \RSVP{} field operators \end{enumerate} arise as symmetry-reduced or perturbed instances of a common operator class. The central claim is that \emph{spectral universality} is not an accident of complex systems but an expression of deeper field-theoretic invariants: the same plenum-like substrate that governs primes and nuclei also governs cortical waves and conscious dynamics. %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \chapter{Introduction: From Brain Waves to Spectral Universality} %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% The traditional story about the brain begins with neurons and synapses. The brain is a graph; synaptic strengths encode memories; computation is carried out by changing connectivity. This narrative has been extraordinarily successful in grounding connectionist models and statistical learning. Yet it has proven increasingly inadequate for explaining the large-scale, fast, and flexible self-organization that underlies perception, working memory, and conscious control. A different picture has emerged from electrophysiology and ultrafast imaging. Earl K.~Miller and collaborators have argued that cortical computation is fundamentally \emph{wave-based} and \emph{analog}: oscillating electric fields, not synaptic updates, coordinate vast populations of neurons in real time \parencite{MillerCohen2001PFC,Miller2025PicowerAnalogWaves}. Standing and traveling waves serve as `traffic signals' for information flow, sculpting temporary task-specific networks on the timescale of tens of milliseconds. Concurrently, Cabral, Shemesh and colleagues have provided direct fMRI evidence for macroscale standing waves in the rodent brain, operating as resonant modes that organize long-range functional connectivity \parencite{cabral2023intrinsic}. Their work supports the view of the brain as a resonance chamber whose geometry and connectivity define an intrinsic spectral repertoire. These neuroscientific developments invite a broader question: are there \emph{universal mathematical structures} that underlie not only cortical dynamics but also other complex systems? Random Matrix Theory (RMT) and analytic number theory provide a striking answer. The same eigenvalue statistics that describe energy levels in heavy nuclei also describe the distribution of zeros of the Riemann zeta function and related $L$-functions \parencite{wigner1957,dyson1962,berry1986,FirkMiller2009NucleiPrimesRMT}. Firk and Miller's expository survey \parencite{FirkMiller2009NucleiPrimesRMT} demonstrates that nuclei and primes share a common spectral universality class. This essay develops a unified operator-theoretic framework in which: \begin{itemize} \item cortical standing waves, \item nuclear spectra, \item zeta zeros, \item and \RSVP{} fields \end{itemize} are all instances of the same underlying phenomenon: spectral universality in complex systems. We proceed as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:neuro} reviews the neuroscience of cortical waves and analog computation. Chapter~\ref{chap:cabral} introduces ultrafast fMRI evidence for intrinsic resonant modes. Chapter~\ref{chap:rmt} reviews RMT and its number-theoretic connections, emphasizing the Firk–Miller exposition. Chapter~\ref{chap:bridge} articulates the bridge from primes to cortex to \RSVP{} via operator hierarchies. Chapter~\ref{chap:rsvp} develops the \RSVP{} field operator and shows how it contains the known operator classes as symmetry reductions. The appendices provide mathematical foundations, operator derivations, symmetry hierarchies, numerical methods, and empirical evidence. %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \chapter{Cortex as a Resonant Analog Computer} \label{chap:neuro} %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \section{Prefrontal Cortex as Model Builder} Miller and Cohen's integrative theory of prefrontal cortex (PFC) function \parencite{MillerCohen2001PFC} characterizes the PFC as a model-building and control system rather than a static memory store. The PFC maintains patterns of activity that represent task goals, rules, and contextual constraints; these patterns exert top-down bias on other cortical and subcortical areas, guiding behavior in a flexible, context-sensitive manner. Formally, we can think of these PFC states as dynamical attractors in a high dimensional neural state space. The PFC must: \begin{enumerate} \item integrate diverse sensory and mnemonic inputs; \item maintain goal-relevant information over delays; \item bias processing toward action plans that satisfy those goals. \end{enumerate} In early models, these functions were implemented as patterns of persistent firing in recurrent networks. However, this picture left open the question of how such networks are dynamically configured and reconfigured in real time. \section{Mixed Selectivity and Dimensionality Reduction} Electrophysiological recordings from PFC demonstrate that neurons are not highly specialized detectors for single variables. Instead, many exhibit \emph{mixed selectivity}: their responses are nonlinear functions of multiple task variables that change with context. This property provides two advantages: \begin{itemize} \item enormous representational capacity, as combinations of inputs can be encoded sparsely across populations; \item flexible participation in multiple functional networks, depending on the larger-scale wave patterns and phase relationships. \end{itemize} Despite this complexity at the level of individual neurons, population activity often lies close to a low-dimensional manifold. Principal component analysis and related techniques reveal that a small number of latent dimensions can explain most of the variance in large ensembles of PFC neurons during cognitive tasks. Miller likens this to birds flocking: each bird follows simple local rules, yet the flock behaves like a coherent whole. The natural mathematical language for such behavior is spectral: latent dimensions correspond to modes of an effective operator, and neural trajectories are constrained by the eigenstructure of that operator. \section{Brain Waves as Computational Substrate} Recent work from Miller's lab and the Picower Institute proposes that oscillatory brain waves --- traveling, rotating, and standing patterns of electrical activity --- provide the primary computational substrate for cortex \parencite{Miller2025PicowerAnalogWaves,Miller2025DebriefInterview}. Slower beta and alpha rhythms (8–35 Hz) carry top-down signals reflecting goals and rules; faster gamma rhythms (35–60 Hz) encode bottom-up sensory details. Wave interference and phase relationships enable: \begin{itemize} \item routing of information between regions; \item dynamic gating of sensory input by top-down expectations; \item flexible formation and dissolution of functional networks. \end{itemize} In this view, synaptic connectivity sets the boundary conditions and coupling strengths for the wave equation, but \emph{the computation itself is performed by wave dynamics}. The brain acts as an analog computer whose `processor' is the geometry of its resonant modes. %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \chapter{Intrinsic Macroscale Oscillatory Modes} \label{chap:cabral} %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \section{Ultrafast fMRI and Standing Waves} Cabral, Fernandes, and Shemesh used ultrafast ultrahigh-field fMRI in rats to visualize the spontaneous activity of the brain at temporal resolutions of $\sim 38$ ms \parencite{cabral2023intrinsic}. This is an order of magnitude faster than conventional fMRI, which typically acquires 1--2 images per second. Their key findings are: \begin{itemize} \item power at frequencies up to 0.3 Hz in resting-state fMRI signals; \item spatially extended modes with fixed phase relationships across distant cortical and subcortical regions; \item clear standing-wave patterns resembling vibrational modes of a physical resonator; \item systematic changes in mode number, frequency, and persistence with level of anesthesia. \end{itemize} These modes are well-described as eigenfunctions of an effective operator on the cortical domain, analogous to the eigenmodes of a vibrating membrane. \section{Resonance, Stochastic Resonance, and Anesthesia} Cabral et al.\ show that as anesthetic concentration increases, the number of modes decreases, their frequency content shifts to lower values, and their lifetimes shorten \parencite{cabral2023intrinsic}. At deep anesthesia, the standing-wave repertoire collapses into slow, global fluctuations. This behavior is consistent with models in which the cortex is an underdamped oscillator driven by noise: stochastic resonance in a structured medium. The operator spectrum determines which modes can be excited, and the noise level determines which are actually populated. In parallel, Miller and collaborators have shown that general anesthesia alters the balance and coherence of cortical rhythms, pushing the system into regimes where wave patterns cannot sustain the long-range synchronization necessary for consciousness \parencite{Miller2025PicowerAnalogWaves}. Consciousness thus appears as a spectral phase of the cortex, characterized by a rich but coherent multi-mode structure. \section{Traveling Waves and Working Memory} Trongnetrpunya et al.\ report traveling waves in prefrontal cortex during working memory tasks \parencite{Trongnetrpunya2022TravelingWavesPFC}. These waves sweep across electrode arrays, carrying content-specific information and coordinating local assemblies. Together, the ultrafast fMRI and electrophysiological evidence supports a model in which: \begin{itemize} \item the cortex expresses a structured family of standing, traveling, and rotating modes; \item these modes are eigenfunctions of an underlying neural-field operator; \item cognitive states correspond to specific spectral patterns within this operator's spectrum. \end{itemize} The next step is to connect this spectral picture to the broader theory of spectral universality developed in physics and number theory. %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \chapter{Random Matrices, Zeta Zeros, and Nuclei} \label{chap:rmt} %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \section{Wigner’s Semicircle Law and Nuclear Spectra} The origins of Random Matrix Theory lie in Wigner's attempt to model the highly complicated spectra of heavy nuclei \parencite{wigner1957}. Instead of trying to calculate the exact Hamiltonian for a many-body nucleus, Wigner proposed treating it as a large random symmetric matrix, constrained only by symmetry and variance. Under broad conditions, the eigenvalue density of such random matrices converges to the semicircle distribution (Theorem~A.2.1 in Appendix~A). The striking success of this model in reproducing the gross features of nuclear level statistics established RMT as a powerful tool for complex quantum systems. \section{Wigner–Dyson Statistics and Universality} Dyson extended Wigner's ideas to classify random matrices according to symmetry classes and to derive universal spacing laws for their eigenvalues \parencite{dyson1962}. The Wigner–Dyson spacing distributions exhibit level repulsion: the probability of finding two eigenvalues arbitrarily close to each other goes to zero as a power of the spacing (Theorem~A.1.1). The key insight is that these spacing laws are \emph{universal}: they hold not only for Gaussian ensembles but also for a wide variety of random matrix models and even for deterministic chaotic systems. Only the symmetry class matters. \section{Zeta Zeros and the Berry–Montgomery–Odlyzko Conjectures} Montgomery's pair correlation conjecture \parencite{montgomery1973}, supported by Odlyzko's extensive numerical computations \parencite{odlyzko1987}, suggests that the local spacing statistics of the nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function coincide with those of the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE). Berry interpreted this as evidence that the zeta zeros behave like the spectrum of a quantum chaotic system \parencite{berry1986}. Firk and Miller provide a detailed exposition of these developments, showing how random matrices, nuclear spectra, and zeta zeros occupy the same spectral universality class \parencite{FirkMiller2009NucleiPrimesRMT}. Their paper illustrates: \begin{itemize} \item how semicircle law and spacing distributions arise from RMT; \item how explicit formulas link primes to zeta zeros; \item how 1-level densities and n-level correlations for families of $L$-functions match RMT predictions. \end{itemize} The conclusion is that complex quantum systems and the arithmetic of primes share a deep spectral structure. %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \chapter{From Primes to Cortex to RSVP} \label{chap:bridge} %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% \section{The Firk–Miller Exposition} The Firk–Miller paper \parencite{FirkMiller2009NucleiPrimesRMT} serves as a conceptual bridge between nuclear physics and number theory. It shows that: \begin{enumerate} \item highly excited nuclei, modeled via random Hamiltonians, exhibit Wigner–Dyson spacing; \item zeta zeros and zeros of other $L$-functions exhibit the same local statistics; \item these coincidences are explained naturally by RMT universality. \end{enumerate} In both cases, the microscopic details are intractable; what remains visible at the spectral level is determined by symmetry and large-scale structure. This perspective suggests that any sufficiently complex system with appropriate symmetry will exhibit the same spectral fingerprints. \section{Spectral Universality from Primes to Cortex to RSVP} We now extend the Firk–Miller perspective to cortical operators and the \RSVP{} field operator. Let $H_N$ be an $N\times N$ Hermitian random matrix drawn from a Gaussian ensemble (GOE, GUE, GSE). Denote its ordered eigenvalues by $\lambda_1^{(N)} \le \cdots \le \lambda_N^{(N)}$ and define normalized spacings: \[ s_k^{(N)} = \frac{\lambda_{k+1}^{(N)} - \lambda_k^{(N)}}{\mathbb{E}[\lambda_{k+1}^{(N)} - \lambda_k^{(N)}]}. \] As $N\to\infty$, the distributions of $s_k^{(N)}$ converge to universal forms, depending only on the Dyson index $\beta$ associated with the symmetry class \parencite{dyson1962}. These distributions exhibit level repulsion: $P_\beta(s) \sim s^\beta$ as $s\to0$, in stark contrast to Poisson statistics. \section{Wigner’s Semicircle Law} Under broad assumptions on the entries of $H_N$, the empirical spectral distribution (ESD) converges to the semicircle distribution \parencite{wigner1957}. A standard proof uses the moment method, showing that the moments of the ESD converge to the Catalan numbers, which are the moments of the semicircle density. This underpins the interpretation of RMT as describing the `background' spectral density of complex systems, upon which finer structures (e.g.\ arithmetic or geometric) may be superimposed. \section{Pair Correlation of Zeta Zeros} Montgomery's pair correlation conjecture, supported by Odlyzko's numerical results \parencite{montgomery1973,odlyzko1987}, states that the pair correlation of the nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function matches that of the GUE ensemble. Berry's work connects this to quantum chaos \parencite{berry1986}, suggesting that zeta zeros behave like the spectrum of a chaotic quantum Hamiltonian. \section{Standing Waves as Eigenfunctions of Cortical Operators} Modeling the cortex as a 2D domain $\Omega$, we introduce a neural-field operator: \[ (\Lop_{\mathrm{cortex}} u)(x) = -D\nabla^2 u(x) + \int_\Omega W(x,y) u(y)\,dy + \Phi'(u_0(x)) u(x). \] Under symmetry and regularity assumptions on $W$, $\Lop_{\mathrm{cortex}}$ is self-adjoint and has a discrete spectrum. Its eigenfunctions correspond to standing waves, and its eigenvalues define mode frequencies. This provides a rigorous foundation for interpreting ultrafast fMRI modes and traveling waves as spectral phenomena. \section{Linearized RSVP Operator} The derivation of the linearized \RSVP{} operator and its spectral decomposition follows the block-matrix form given in Chapter~\ref{chap:rsvp} and is worked out in detail in the proofs of Appendix~B.
