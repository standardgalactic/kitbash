\chapter{Mathematical Structure of RSVP}

With the ontological foundations of RSVP firmly established, we now turn to the formal mathematical framework. The central goal is to define a consistent set of partial differential equations governing the scalar, vector, and entropy fields of the plenum in a manner that respects constraint-first principles. Unlike conventional field theories, RSVP does not treat the equations as generators of state evolution from initial conditions. Rather, they define admissibility: only solutions satisfying these coupled equations correspond to physically possible histories.

Let the domain of the plenum be denoted by (\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}), representing spatial positions and global relational time. We assume sufficient smoothness to admit partial derivatives, but allow for singular structures in scalar or vector gradients, reflecting concentrated constraint accumulation. Boundary conditions are typically taken to be asymptotic, encoding global plenum saturation, or periodic in finite-volume simulations to simplify numerical implementation. Regularity assumptions are otherwise minimal; admissibility is the guiding principle.

The scalar field (\Phi(x,t)) represents local availability of the plenum. Formally, (\Phi: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^+), with the positivity condition ensuring that no region is unconstrained or nonexistent. Coupling to the vector and entropy fields ensures that scalar relaxation is directed and moderated: (\Phi) cannot change arbitrarily, but only along admissible directions encoded by (\mathbf{v}) and in accordance with local entropy density (S).

The vector field (\mathbf{v}(x,t)) encodes relaxation direction. Unlike conventional velocity fields, (\mathbf{v}) is a guide for the redistribution of scalar availability, not a description of particle motion. Its divergence, curl, and higher-order differential operators control admissibility conditions for directional flows, torsion suppression, and vorticity regulation. The formal structure ensures that (\mathbf{v}) aligns locally with the gradients of (\Phi) while respecting global entropy constraints.

Entropy is represented by the scalar field (S(x,t)), which quantifies local saturation of irreversible constraint. Its evolution is governed by production, transport, and reintegration terms that reflect the accumulation of past histories. In formal terms, (S) acts as a Lagrange multiplier restricting admissible variations in (\Phi) and (\mathbf{v}). High entropy regions inhibit further local relaxation, producing structural plateaus and aging regimes in the plenum. Formally, (S: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^+) and obeys inequalities ensuring non-decreasing local constraint accumulation.

Coupling among these three fields is expressed via a system of partial differential inequalities and equations. A minimal illustrative form is
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\Phi \mathbf{v}) &= - f(S, \Phi), \
\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{v} &= - \nabla g(\Phi, S) + \mathbf{T}(\Phi, \mathbf{v}, S), \
\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} &= h(\Phi, \mathbf{v}, S),
\end{align}
where (f, g, h) are admissibility functions reflecting local constraint interactions, and (\mathbf{T}) encodes torsion and vorticity regulation. These functions are chosen to satisfy the overarching principle: no evolution is allowed that violates the monotonic accumulation of constraint, as encoded in (S), and all directional relaxations proceed along (\mathbf{v}).

Several features distinguish these equations from conventional field theory. First, they are generally irreversible: the formal structure enforces a monotonic increase in (S), precluding time-reversed solutions as admissible. Second, they are nonlocal in the sense that the admissibility of a local change depends on the surrounding distribution of (S) and (\Phi), reflecting the global coupling of histories. Third, they integrate naturally with discrete and lattice formulations for simulation, allowing one to explore emergent structures even in the absence of analytic solutions.

Historically, aspects of this formalism echo classical continuum mechanics and thermodynamics. The divergence and curl terms in (\mathbf{v}) recall Eulerian fluid dynamics \cite{batchelor1967}, while the coupling to (S) parallels the entropy production terms in non-equilibrium thermodynamics \cite{degroot1962}. However, RSVP generalizes these ideas by embedding them within a constraint-first ontology: the equations define what is possible, not what must occur given initial data.

Finally, boundary and asymptotic conditions serve an interpretive as well as computational role. Regions of maximal plenum saturation correspond to high (S), constraining relaxation and producing structural plateaus. These conditions also define global admissibility domains: no field configuration outside these bounds is physically meaningful. In this way, the mathematical structure of RSVP embodies the principle that the universe is defined not by what it contains at a given instant, but by the totality of histories that are admissible within the coupled scalar–vector–entropy framework.

In the following chapters, each field is examined in detail. The scalar field is analyzed in terms of local availability and coupling to historical constraint. The vector field is examined for its role in directional relaxation and torsion suppression. The entropy field is formalized as a physical measure of irreversible constraint accumulation. Together, these analyses form the backbone for the simulation and predictive frameworks presented in subsequent sections.

Long before the mathematical formalism of modern field theory, the notion that reality could contain true voids was regarded as incoherent. Aristotle argued that motion itself would be unintelligible in a vacuum, since nothing would distinguish one place from another \cite{aristotle_physics}. Although his specific arguments were later superseded, the intuition that physical processes require a substrate has resurfaced repeatedly across the history of physics. In Aristotle’s conception, the plenum was not a material substance in the modern sense but a continuously occupied medium whose properties determined the possibilities of motion and change. This view was transformed rather than abandoned in early modern philosophy. Descartes rejected action at a distance and identified space with extension itself, asserting that wherever there is extension, there is matter \cite{descartes1644}. Motion, on this view, consists of the rearrangement of contiguous parts of a filled continuum. While Cartesian mechanics ultimately failed as a predictive framework, its insistence that spatial structure and physical content are inseparable anticipated later developments in field theory. The nineteenth century witnessed a revival of plenary thinking in the form of the electromagnetic ether. Faraday’s introduction of field lines as real physical entities marked a decisive break from purely particle-based ontology \cite{faraday1852}. Maxwell’s synthesis of electricity and magnetism formalized this break by treating electromagnetic fields as dynamical objects in their own right, capable of storing energy and propagating disturbances through space \cite{maxwell1865}. The ether, in this context, functioned as a conceptual placeholder for the medium that carried these fields. The subsequent rejection of the ether is often portrayed as a triumph of empirical reasoning over metaphysical speculation. Einstein’s special theory of relativity eliminated the need for a preferred rest frame, and with it the mechanical ether of nineteenth-century physics \cite{einstein1905}. However, this rejection is frequently misunderstood as an endorsement of empty space. In reality, relativity replaces the ether not with nothingness, but with a richly structured spacetime endowed with metric properties and dynamical degrees of freedom \cite{janssen2006}. The vacuum of modern physics is not empty; it is defined by fields, symmetries, and constraints. Quantum field theory makes this point unavoidable. What is colloquially called the vacuum is a highly nontrivial state characterized by zero-point fluctuations, condensates, and symmetry-breaking structures \cite{weinberg1995}. Particle excitations are understood as localized disturbances of underlying fields, not as occupants of an otherwise featureless void. The language of emptiness persists largely as a relic of classical intuition rather than as a faithful description of the formalism. RSVP builds on this historical trajectory while rejecting the residual assumptions that tether modern field theories to state-first ontology. The plenum, in RSVP, is not a mechanical medium composed of parts, nor a geometric manifold whose properties evolve according to reversible equations. It is a structured substrate defined by the coexistence of scalar, vector, and entropy fields whose coupled dynamics constrain admissible histories. The plenum is everywhere not as a metaphysical assertion, but as a consequence of denying the coherence of a truly empty region in which no constraints apply. Crucially, the RSVP plenum is not reintroduced to explain the propagation of forces or signals alone. Its primary role is to encode irreversibility. Constraint accumulation cannot occur in nothingness; it requires a substrate in which constraints can be stored, transported, and saturated. The plenum is thus the physical bearer of history. Without it, the concept of an irreversible past would be unintelligible. This reconceptualization distinguishes RSVP sharply from both classical ether theories and modern spacetime realism. The plenum is not defined by a rest frame, nor does it privilege any coordinate system. Its structure is relational and dynamical, yet not reducible to geometry alone. Geometry, in RSVP, emerges as an effective description of large-scale regularities in plenum structure, not as a fundamental ingredient. The return to a plenary ontology is therefore not a regression but a progression. It reflects the recognition that the elimination of substance in favor of abstract state spaces has obscured the physical reality of constraint and history. By reinstating a structured substrate, RSVP provides a home for irreversibility that neither classical mechanics nor relativistic field theory can supply on their own. The next chapter articulates the minimal ontological content of this plenum. It argues that three field types—scalar, vector, and entropy—are sufficient and necessary to encode density, directed relaxation, and constraint pressure. This scalar–vector ontology is not an arbitrary choice but the simplest structure capable of supporting irreversible histories without collapsing back into state-first dynamics. \chapter{The Crisis of Expansion} Modern cosmology is widely presented as an empirical triumph. Observations of galactic redshift, the cosmic microwave background, and large-scale structure are taken to support a coherent narrative in which the universe expands from an initially hot, dense state according to relativistic field equations. Yet this narrative rests on a subtle but consequential inference: that redshift necessarily implies metric expansion of space itself. This chapter argues that this inference is not an observation but a theoretical commitment inherited from state-first physics, and that once irreversibility and constraint are taken as primitive, the interpretation of cosmological data must be reconsidered. The association between redshift and expansion originates in Edwin Hubble’s discovery of a linear relationship between the redshift of galaxies and their inferred distance \cite{hubble1929}. Within the framework of general relativity, this relationship is naturally modeled by solutions to Einstein’s field equations in which the spacetime metric evolves in time, stretching wavelengths as space itself expands \cite{friedmann1922,lemaitre1931}. The success of these models in fitting observational data has led to the widespread identification of expansion as a directly observed phenomenon. However, the inference from redshift to expansion is not logically forced. Redshift is an observed change in wavelength between emission and detection; expansion is one possible mechanism by which such a change might arise. Alternative mechanisms, including gravitational redshift and cumulative interaction with an intervening medium, have historically been considered and often dismissed on model-dependent grounds \cite{peebles1993}. The dismissal of non-expansion interpretations reflects not a decisive empirical refutation, but a preference for explanations that preserve the state-first ontology of relativistic cosmology. The crisis becomes apparent when the expansion paradigm is examined in light of irreversibility. In standard cosmology, the universe evolves from a highly ordered initial state through a sequence of increasingly complex structures, all while the underlying equations remain time-reversal invariant. The arrow of time is imposed through boundary conditions rather than derived from dynamics. Inflationary theory intensifies this reliance on special initial conditions by positing an early epoch of exponential expansion designed to explain homogeneity, isotropy, and flatness \cite{guth1981}. Inflation functions as a patch that restores consistency within the expansion framework, but it does so by introducing new fields and parameters whose physical origin remains obscure. From the perspective developed in the preceding chapters, this situation is deeply unsatisfactory. A cosmological model that requires exquisitely tuned initial conditions in order to reproduce the observed arrow of time signals a mismatch between ontology and phenomenon. If irreversibility is primitive, then cosmological evolution should be governed by monotonic processes of relaxation and constraint accumulation, not by reversible metric dynamics supplemented by improbable starting points. RSVP proposes that what is interpreted as cosmic expansion is more accurately described as large-scale relaxation within a structured plenum. In this picture, the universe does not grow by creating new space; rather, existing structure redistributes and smooths over time as entropy gradients relax. Redshift arises from cumulative interaction with the scalar and entropy fields of the plenum, which drain energy from propagating radiation in a manner analogous to, but more general than, gravitational redshift. The effect is global, monotonic, and irreversible, aligning naturally with the observed arrow of time. This reinterpretation resolves several persistent tensions in cosmology. The apparent acceleration of expansion inferred from supernova observations \cite{riess1998} no longer requires a distinct dark energy component. Instead, it reflects the late-time dominance of entropy-driven relaxation as large-scale gradients diminish. Similarly, the coincidence problem—the question of why cosmic acceleration becomes significant only in the recent universe—loses its force when acceleration is replaced by saturation-driven smoothing rather than exponential growth \cite{weinberg2008}. Moreover, the expansion paradigm struggles with the ultimate fate of the universe. Heat death scenarios, big crunches, and eternal inflation each extrapolate reversible dynamics into regimes where constraint accumulation should dominate. RSVP replaces these speculative endpoints with a picture of cosmic aging. As entropy gradients are exhausted, structure formation slows, degrees of freedom freeze out, and the universe approaches a state of maximal admissibility saturation. This state is not a singularity, nor a thermodynamic equilibrium in the classical sense, but a structurally inert plenum in which further irreversible change is impossible. The crisis of expansion is thus not merely empirical but conceptual. It arises from the attempt to interpret fundamentally irreversible phenomena within a framework that treats reversibility as basic. By relinquishing the assumption that space itself must expand, RSVP opens the door to a cosmology grounded in constraint, history, and entropy rather than in dynamically evolving geometry. The next part of this monograph turns from critique to construction. Having argued that state-first physics, reversible dynamics, and metric expansion are inadequate foundations, we now develop the ontological basis of RSVP itself. The following chapter revisits the concept of the plenum, tracing its historical evolution and articulating why a structured substrate is not a regression to discarded ether theories but a necessary consequence of taking irreversibility seriously. \chapter{Irreversibility as a Primitive} The apparent conflict between the time-reversal symmetry of fundamental laws and the irreversible character of observed phenomena has long been regarded as a problem to be solved within an otherwise state-first framework. The prevailing strategy has been to preserve reversible microdynamics while explaining irreversibility as an emergent, statistical, or epistemic phenomenon. This chapter argues that this strategy fails in principle. Irreversibility is not a derivative feature of physical law but a primitive constraint that must be built into the ontology of any adequate theory. The modern concept of irreversibility enters physics through thermodynamics, most clearly in the work of Sadi Carnot. Carnot’s analysis of heat engines did not presuppose microscopic models of matter; instead, it identified absolute limits on the conversion of heat into work that depended only on temperature differences \cite{carnot1824}. These limits were not contingent on ignorance or coarse-graining but expressed a structural asymmetry between processes that could occur and those that could not. Carnot’s work already suggested that physical law constrains histories directly, forbidding certain transformations regardless of dynamical detail. Rudolf Clausius sharpened this insight by introducing entropy as a state function whose increase characterizes irreversible processes \cite{clausius1865}. Although entropy was later reinterpreted statistically, Clausius’ original formulation treated it as an objective quantity tied to physical transformations. The second law of thermodynamics, in Clausius’ hands, was not a statement about probability but a prohibition: heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot bodies. Such prohibitions are fundamentally different in character from equations of motion, as they delimit the space of admissible processes rather than generating trajectories from initial data. Ludwig Boltzmann’s statistical program sought to reconcile this prohibition with the reversibility of Newtonian mechanics by identifying entropy with the logarithm of phase-space volume \cite{boltzmann1877}. While enormously influential, this move came at a conceptual cost. By grounding entropy in microstate counting, Boltzmann transformed irreversibility into a matter of overwhelming likelihood rather than necessity. The second law became true “for all practical purposes,” but not strictly. This left open the possibility, however remote, of spontaneous entropy decrease, a possibility that sits uneasily with the absolute character of thermodynamic prohibitions. The tension is crystallized in Loschmidt’s paradox, which observes that any entropy-increasing trajectory generated by reversible dynamics has a time-reversed counterpart that decreases entropy \cite{loschmidt1876}. Appeals to typicality or probabilistic reasoning do not resolve the paradox; they merely restate it in different terms. If entropy increase is not enforced by the laws themselves, then its universality remains unexplained. The invocation of special initial conditions, such as a low-entropy early universe, merely displaces the problem to the boundary of the theory \cite{price1996}. Twentieth-century developments did not dissolve this difficulty. In quantum mechanics, unitary time evolution preserves von Neumann entropy and is exactly reversible \cite{dirac1930}. The apparent irreversibility of measurement is typically attributed to decoherence and environmental entanglement, yet the combined system-plus-environment still evolves reversibly according to the Schrödinger equation \cite{zurek2003}. Decoherence explains why interference becomes unobservable, but it does not explain why one outcome occurs rather than another, nor why outcomes are permanently recorded. The irreversibility of measurement is thus presupposed, not derived. Ilya Prigogine argued forcefully that this situation reflects a fundamental mistake in the foundations of physics. According to Prigogine, irreversibility must be incorporated at the most basic level of description, rather than emerging from reversible dynamics through probabilistic arguments \cite{prigogine1980,prigogine1997}. His work on non-equilibrium thermodynamics demonstrated that far-from-equilibrium systems exhibit stable, irreversible structures whose existence cannot be understood as mere perturbations of equilibrium behavior. These structures embody a temporal asymmetry that is intrinsic, not accidental. RSVP adopts this lesson as an ontological commitment. Irreversibility is treated as a primitive constraint on admissible histories, not as an emergent regularity. Entropy, within this framework, is not a measure of ignorance but a physical field encoding the accumulation of constraints that forbid certain reversals. Once a constraint has been accumulated, it cannot be undone without violating the admissibility conditions of the theory. Time, correspondingly, is identified with the monotonic accumulation of such constraints. This perspective dissolves the traditional puzzles associated with the arrow of time. There is no need to explain why entropy increases rather than decreases, because decrease is simply inadmissible. There is no need to posit a special initial condition of extraordinarily low entropy, because the theory does not define itself by evolution from an initial state at all. Histories are not generated; they are selected by constraint. The implications of this move are far-reaching. If irreversibility is primitive, then any theory that treats time-reversal invariance as fundamental is, at best, an approximation valid within restricted regimes. Reversible laws may emerge as effective descriptions when constraint accumulation is negligible, just as frictionless motion emerges as an idealization in mechanics. But such reversibility cannot be elevated to an ontological principle. The next chapter applies this insight to cosmology. It argues that the prevailing interpretation of redshift and large-scale structure as evidence for an expanding universe is itself a consequence of state-first reasoning. By reinterpreting cosmological data through the lens of irreversible relaxation rather than metric expansion, RSVP offers a radically different account of cosmic history, one in which the universe ages rather than grows. \chapter{The State Illusion} Modern physics is organized around a deceptively simple premise: that the world can be exhaustively described by specifying its state at an instant and applying dynamical laws to evolve that state in time. This premise is so deeply ingrained that it is rarely articulated explicitly, yet it structures the mathematical formalism of classical mechanics, statistical mechanics, quantum theory, and cosmology alike. The present chapter argues that this premise is not merely a technical convenience but a substantive metaphysical commitment, one that obscures the fundamentally historical and irreversible character of physical reality. The state-first worldview finds its clearest classical expression in the deterministic ideal articulated by Pierre-Simon Laplace. In Laplace’s formulation, an intellect that knows the positions and velocities of all particles at a single moment, together with the laws of motion, could reconstruct the entire past and future of the universe \cite{laplace1814}. This vision does not merely assume determinism; it assumes that instantaneous state descriptions are ontologically complete. Time, within this picture, is a parameter indexing reversible transformations of a timeless configuration. Classical mechanics operationalizes this assumption through phase space. A point in phase space is taken to represent the full physical reality of a system at a given instant, and Hamiltonian flow guarantees that this point traces a unique trajectory forward and backward in time. The success of this formalism in celestial mechanics and rigid-body dynamics reinforced the intuition that physical completeness coincides with instantaneous specification. Yet even within classical physics, this intuition was already in tension with thermodynamics. The laws of motion are time-reversal invariant, while macroscopic phenomena manifest an unmistakable arrow of time \cite{boltzmann1896}. Statistical mechanics attempted to reconcile this tension by relegating irreversibility to epistemic limitation. In the Gibbsian formulation, entropy is defined as a functional over ensembles in phase space, encoding ignorance about microstates rather than objective physical structure \cite{gibbs1902}. The second law of thermodynamics is thus interpreted as overwhelmingly probable behavior, not as a fundamental constraint. While mathematically powerful, this move preserves the state-first ontology at the cost of introducing a sharp divide between microscopic reversibility and macroscopic irreversibility, a divide that remains conceptually unstable. The instability is sharpened by Loschmidt’s paradox, which points out that if the microscopic laws are truly reversible, then entropy-increasing and entropy-decreasing trajectories must be equally admissible \cite{loschmidt1876}. Attempts to resolve this paradox by appeal to special initial conditions implicitly smuggle irreversibility back in through the boundary conditions, rather than confronting it as a primitive feature of the theory. The invocation of a low-entropy initial state of the universe functions as a metaphysical placeholder, not an explanation. Quantum mechanics inherits and amplifies these difficulties. The quantum state, represented by a vector in Hilbert space, is commonly interpreted as a complete description of a system at an instant \cite{vonneumann1932}. Unitary time evolution preserves information and is exactly reversible, reinforcing the state-first picture at the formal level. Yet the measurement problem exposes a deep inconsistency: the apparent irreversibility of measurement outcomes is not captured by the underlying dynamics. Interpretations that treat collapse as epistemic or subjective merely rephrase the problem without dissolving it \cite{maudlin2007}. The persistence of the state illusion is not confined to physics. It reflects a broader metaphysical tendency to privilege being over becoming, a tendency already visible in ancient philosophy. Parmenides’ conception of reality as static and timeless stands in stark contrast to Heraclitus’ emphasis on flux and transformation \cite{kirk1983}. Modern physics, despite its mathematical sophistication, largely sides with Parmenides by encoding reality in instantaneous structures and treating change as secondary. Literature offers a striking parallel critique. In Jorge Luis Borges’ “The Garden of Forking Paths,” time is not a single linear sequence but a branching structure of histories, each real in its own right \cite{borges1941}. Borges’ narrative anticipates a conception of reality in which history cannot be reduced to a sequence of static snapshots. Meaning arises from the structure of paths taken and not taken, rather than from any isolated moment. The RSVP framework takes this lesson seriously. It rejects the assumption that an instantaneous state can ever be ontologically complete. Instead, it posits that physical reality is constituted by admissible histories constrained by irreversible processes. What matters is not where a system is at a moment, but what it has undergone and what constraints it has accumulated. The apparent success of state-based descriptions is reinterpreted as a regime-limited approximation, valid only when history-dependence is weak or can be neglected. This shift has immediate consequences. If histories are primary, then the notion of an initial condition loses its foundational status. There is no privileged starting point from which everything unfolds; rather, there are admissible and inadmissible histories determined by constraint structure. The arrow of time is no longer an emergent statistical tendency but an ontological asymmetry encoded directly into the fabric of physical law. The remainder of this monograph develops the consequences of abandoning the state illusion. The next chapter argues that irreversibility must be taken as a primitive, not derived from reversible laws supplemented by probabilistic reasoning. Only by making this move can physics hope to reconcile its formal success with the manifest asymmetry of the world we observe. 

\chapter*{Preface}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface}

Modern physics is extraordinarily successful at prediction and technological control, yet persistently unsettled about what, precisely, it claims to describe. The dominant frameworks of classical mechanics, quantum theory, and relativistic cosmology all rely on the assumption that reality is exhausted by the specification of an instantaneous state together with dynamical laws that propagate that state forward and backward in time. This assumption, inherited from classical determinism and formalized most clearly in the Laplacian ideal of complete knowledge \cite{laplace1814}, has proven so productive that it is rarely questioned, even as it generates unresolved conceptual tensions concerning irreversibility, cosmological initial conditions, quantum measurement, and the ontological status of time itself \cite{maudlin2007,price1996}.

The present work proposes that these tensions are not peripheral anomalies but symptoms of a deeper misidentification of the fundamental objects of physical theory. The Relativistic Scalar--Vector Plenum (RSVP) is developed here as a constraint-first physical ontology, in which admissible histories, rather than instantaneous states, constitute the primary domain of physical law. Within this framework, entropy is not a statistical artifact derived from coarse-graining \cite{gibbs1902}, nor merely an emergent bookkeeping device, but a real structural field encoding the accumulation of irreversible constraints. Time is not a neutral parameter labeling reversible evolution, but an index of constraint accretion that cannot, even in principle, be undone \cite{prigogine1980,reichenbach1956}.

This monograph is written in the conviction that foundational physics has reached a point analogous to the late nineteenth century, when the internal coherence of classical mechanics masked a growing inability to accommodate thermodynamics and electromagnetism within a unified conceptual scheme \cite{maxwell1865}. RSVP is not presented as a minor modification of existing theories, nor as one speculative alternative among many, but as a systematic attempt to reverse the explanatory order that has governed physics since the nineteenth century. Instead of asking how the universe evolves from an initial condition, we ask what histories are admissible at all, and how structure emerges as the progressive saturation of those admissibility constraints.

\chapter*{Reader's Guide}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Reader's Guide}

This book is addressed to readers with graduate-level familiarity with classical field theory, partial differential equations, thermodynamics, and the mathematical structure of quantum mechanics. However, the conceptual arc is intentionally broader than any single discipline. Historical, philosophical, and literary materials are not included as ornamentation, but as diagnostic instruments, revealing how deeply the state-first picture has shaped not only physics but Western metaphysics more generally \cite{kuhn1962,heidegger1927}.

Readers primarily interested in the formal RSVP field equations may proceed directly to Part III, returning to the earlier conceptual chapters as needed. Those concerned with cosmology and empirical consequences may focus on Parts V and VI, while readers interested in implications for quantum theory, biology, or cognition will find the later chapters largely self-contained. Extensive appendices provide mathematical background, derivations, and numerical considerations so that the main text can maintain conceptual continuity without sacrificing rigor \cite{evans2010,trefethen2000}.

\chapter*{Notational Conventions and Ontological Commitments}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Notational Conventions and Ontological Commitments}

Throughout this work, symbols are chosen to reflect ontological role rather than historical convention wherever possible. Scalar quantities represent densities or availabilities within the plenum, vector quantities represent directed relaxation tendencies rather than velocities in the Newtonian sense \cite{landau1987}, and entropy is treated as a field with dynamical and transport properties, not merely as a functional of microstates \cite{degroot1962}. Spacetime indices are employed sparingly, and only where relativistic covariance is genuinely at issue, in order to avoid importing geometric assumptions that RSVP explicitly rejects \cite{choquet2009}.

Most importantly, RSVP does not assume the existence of a global configuration space, phase space, or Hilbert space as a fundamental arena of reality. These structures will appear later as effective or derived representations, valid only within restricted dynamical regimes \cite{dirac1930,weinberg1995}. The central ontological commitment of this work is that histories themselves are physically real objects, constrained by irreversible processes whose cumulative effects define both time and structure.

\chapter*{Executive Summary of Claims and Predictions}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Executive Summary of Claims and Predictions}

The central claim of this monograph is that the universe does not expand in the sense posited by standard cosmological models \cite{weinberg2008}. Instead, what is observed as expansion is a large-scale relaxation and smoothing of a structured plenum governed by coupled scalar, vector, and entropy fields whose dynamics enforce irreversibility at the most fundamental level. Gravity is reinterpreted as a manifestation of entropy gradients and relaxation flows rather than spacetime curvature, eliminating physical singularities and reframing dark matter and dark energy as effective descriptions of constraint-driven dynamics rather than independent substances \cite{verlinde2011,clifton2012}.

RSVP predicts observable deviations from $\Lambda$CDM cosmology in the late-time behavior of large-scale structure \cite{planck2018}, novel limits on quantum coherence arising from history accumulation rather than environmental decoherence \cite{zurek2003}, and measurable signatures of entropy saturation in both physical and biological systems \cite{landauer1961,friston2010}. These predictions are not appended as speculative afterthoughts but arise directly from the core ontological commitments of the theory.

What follows is an attempt to show that these commitments are not arbitrary, but forced upon us once irreversibility, history, and constraint are taken seriously as physical primitives rather than emergent conveniences.
