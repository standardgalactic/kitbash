
Problem Statement

The Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, 1999 (as amended), vests the control and regulation of security institutions exclusively in the Federal Government. Matters relating to defence, internal security, and policing fall within the Exclusive Legislative List, thereby limiting the constitutional authority of state and local governments to participate meaningfully in security administration.

Despite this centralized security framework, Nigeria continues to face serious and persistent security challenges. Incidents of banditry, kidnapping, insurgency, and communal violence remain prevalent, particularly in the northern regions of the country. These challenges have exposed the limitations of a security system that is highly centralized and often detached from local realities.

Security threats in Nigeria are largely localized in nature, arising within specific communities and regions. However, federal security agencies often lack sufficient local intelligence, proximity, and rapid-response capacity to effectively address these threats at the grassroots level. As a result, responses to insecurity are frequently delayed, inadequate, or unsustainable.

The continued reliance on an exclusively federal security structure has therefore failed to provide an effective solution to Nigeria’s security problems. This situation raises critical questions about the adequacy of the current constitutional framework and highlights the need for alternative approaches to security governance that are better suited to Nigeria’s diverse and complex socio-political environment.

Proposed Solution

This study proposes the decentralization of security responsibilities to the lowest tier of government as a means of addressing Nigeria’s persistent security challenges. Bringing security administration closer to the local government level would enable communities to play a more active role in safeguarding their environments and responding to emerging threats.

Local governments are better positioned to gather grassroots intelligence, understand local dynamics, and respond promptly to security challenges within their jurisdictions. Decentralizing security would also enhance accountability, as security agencies would operate closer to the communities they serve.

Such decentralization should be carried out within a clearly defined constitutional and legal framework to prevent abuse and ensure coordination with federal and state security agencies. Proper oversight mechanisms, training standards, and funding structures would be necessary to ensure that local security institutions operate professionally and in accordance with the rule of law.

By empowering local governments to participate in security administration, Nigeria can develop a more responsive, inclusive, and effective security framework capable of addressing both national and local security challenges.
