🧠 Summary of Key Themes in Ortega y Gasset’s Thought

1. Biographical Context

Born: 1883, Madrid, into a liberal, educated family connected to journalism.

Education: Studied in Spain and Germany, deeply influenced by Neo-Kantianism (Cohen, Natorp) and phenomenology (Husserl).

Career: Professor, essayist, editor of Revista de Occidente, political figure (Agrupación al Servicio de la República), and later founder of the Institute of Humanities in Madrid.

Exile: Spent time in Argentina and Portugal during and after the Spanish Civil War; returned to Spain in 1948.

2. Philosophical Orientation

Ortega y Gasset is aligned with several traditions:

Perspectivism: Truth is approached through a plurality of viewpoints.

Vitalism: But with rational structure—thus "ratiovitalism."

Historicism: Human beings are embedded in a historical context.

Existentialism: A proto-existentialist emphasis on life, choice, and responsibility.

Phenomenology: Influenced by Husserl but with a realist/historicist inflection.

3. Major Concepts and Contributions

🌀 “I am I and my circumstance”

Yo soy yo y mi circunstancia; si no la salvo a ella no me salvo yo
("I am I and my circumstance; if I do not save it, I do not save myself.")

Human identity is inseparable from context.

Neither the subject nor the object alone is real; it is the entwinement of both—life as experienced.

🧬 Ratiovitalism (Razón Vital)

Ortega proposes "vital reason", a post-Cartesian foundation: 

Not "I think, therefore I am" but "I live, therefore I think".

Rejects both pure rationalism and irrationalism.

Knowledge arises from life, and reason must be in service of life.

Related to Lebensphilosophie (philosophy of life) but rationally structured.

📜 Historical Reason

Every person is embedded in history—no ahistorical "self" exists.

Philosophical systems must include the temporal embeddedness of thought and life.

Influenced by Wilhelm Dilthey’s emphasis on historical understanding.

📚 The Revolt of the Masses (1930)

Warns against massification, mediocrity, and the "tyranny of the majority".

Argues for meritocratic liberalism, in line with J.S. Mill, yet critiques both capitalism and authoritarianism.

Describes modern life as dangerously dominated by technocracy without culture and citizens without responsibility.

🧭 Influences and Interactions

Philosophical Influences: William James, Husserl, Nietzsche, Kant, Cohen, Natorp, Dilthey.

Contemporaries Engaged With: Spengler, Huizinga, Simmel, Uexküll, Brentano, Russell.

Political Ambivalence: Though liberal in aspiration, his writings have been appropriated by conservatives; his own distrust of mass democracy and populism complicates his alignment.

🔁 Cross-Comparisons

Free Energy Principle (Friston): Like FEP, Ortega sees life as adaptive regulation within constraints ("fate and freedom"). Both highlight homeostasis through interaction with the environment.

Integrated Information Theory (Tononi): Ortega’s perspectivism and emphasis on subject-object interplay can be loosely analogized to IIT’s emphasis on the structure of conscious integration.

Relevance Activation Theory (RAT): His maxim "I am I and my circumstance" fits RAT’s idea that salience and relevance are circumstantially modulated, and that cognition involves constant contextual activation.
