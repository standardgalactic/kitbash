\begin{abstract}
Computation in machine learning is almost universally described using
vectors, matrices, and tensors. These form the conceptual backbone of
differentiable programming and deep learning systems. In this document
we explore a different approach: \emph{Spherepop}, a geometric
computational framework based on collapse and merge operations on
spatial regions. Instead of storing information in arrays, Spherepop
represents information geometrically and expresses computation through
spatial interaction.

Spherepop provides an unusually visual and intuitive way of
understanding computation. While it is fully expressive in the sense
of classical computation, its real value comes from its geometric way
of constructing functions, programs, and inference procedures. We
develop Spherepop using ideas familiar to machine learning
practitioners and programmers, emphasizing visualization and spatial
intuition. Later sections show how classical formalisms such as tensor
computation and functional programming arise as restricted projections
of the same geometric model.
\end{abstract}

1. Why Geometry Matters in ML

\section{Why Geometry Matters in Machine Learning}

Machine learning is deeply geometric, but the geometry is often
obscured by algebraic notation. Neural networks operate on vector
spaces, but the computations correspond to geometric transformations:
linear maps, projections, manifold contraction, and non-linear
deformation. In practice, these geometric structures are explained
using algebra, because algebra provides clean notation. But the
geometric structure remains implicit.

\subsection{From Algebra to Geometry}

Consider a neural layer:
\[
x \mapsto \sigma(Wx+b)
\]
which is technically a linear transformation followed by a non-linear
map. Algebraically, it is a matrix-vector computation. Geometrically,
it is a deformation of the input space: stretching, rotating,
compressing, and then collapsing regions along a non-linear map. The
algebra forgets this geometric intuition, which can make neural
computation feel abstract or opaque.

\subsection{A Different Starting Point}

Spherepop inverts the order of explanation: rather than deriving
geometry from algebra, we derive algebra from geometry. Instead of
starting from vectors and matrices, we start from geometric regions.
Instead of treating activation functions as symbolic maps, we treat
them as collapse rules. Matrices become projections of spatial
interactions; neural layers become constrained geometric merges.

2. What is Spherepop?

(first gentle introduction)

\section{What is Spherepop?}

The basic idea is simple: objects of computation are geometric regions
(typically balls or spheres) in some space. Computation is performed by
two primitive operations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{collapse} --- remove internal detail,
\item \textbf{merge} --- combine two regions and then collapse them.
\end{enumerate}

These two operations are surprisingly expressive. By constraining how
regions collapse and merge, we encode logic, arithmetic, program
composition, and differentiable computation.

5. Intuition: Collapse and Merge

\section{Intuition: Collapse and Merge}

To understand Spherepop, it helps to imagine a simple spatial domain:
a 3-dimensional box in which we draw balls or spheres. These spheres
represent pieces of information. Spherepop operations modify these
objects directly, rather than applying symbolic functions to them.

The first operation is \emph{collapse}. Given a sphere, we compress it—
removing internal detail—until only its essential boundary information
remains. Collapse can be visualized as shrinking or simplifying the
interior of a region until a smaller, more canonical region remains.

The second operation is \emph{merge}. Given two spheres, we combine
them, then collapse the result. Merge therefore consists of two steps:
a spatial union, and a subsequent collapse. Merge destroys distinctions
inside the combined region while keeping global boundary interaction
intact.


---

5.1 Everyday Analogies

(This is important for the ML educator audience.)

\subsection{Everyday Analogies}

Consider two images. When we blend them (merge), we may lose
fine-grained local detail but preserve the overall shape or structure.
The collapsed result encodes a new, simplified object containing
information from both inputs.

Or consider two clusters in a dataset. If we merge them and then apply
a coarse clustering step (collapse), we obtain a new cluster
representing them as a single entity. Spherepop performs this
operation geometrically, not statistically.


---

5.2 Why collapse is essential

\subsection{Why Collapse Matters}

Without collapse, merge would be only set union: stacking shapes on
top of each other. Collapse removes high-resolution internal structure,
simplifying the shape into a more canonical form. In computation, this
acts like a function mapping many interior states onto a smaller space
of equivalence classes. In practice, this implements abstraction.

6. First Visual Thought Experiments

(3D visualization here, described textually.)

\section{First Visual Thought Experiments}

Imagine placing two spheres in space. If they barely touch, the merged
region will have two lobes; collapse simplifies this into a single
object. Slide the spheres together until they overlap more, and the
collapsed region becomes increasingly compact. If they fully overlap,
collapse yields essentially the same result as the original sphere.

This process is dynamic: as objects move and merge, collapse continually
updates global shape, acting as a form of geometric simplification.
Computation thus unfolds visually in space rather than symbolically in
expressions.

7. Why Spherepop is actually a programming language

(critical for CS-theory flavor)

\section{Spherepop as a Programming Language}

At first glance, Spherepop may look like a visualization gimmick, but
it is in fact a computational language. Collapse is analogous to
function application: it reduces an intermediate object to a simpler
object. Merge is analogous to composition: combining objects into a
new value.

The key point is that collapse and merge generate program structure.
Every machine learning model, neural layer, or logical function can be
described purely in terms of these two operations.

\subsection{Primitive Values}

A single sphere corresponds to a primitive value. We can think of it as
a basic piece of data. Collapse reduces a value; merge combines values.
A program is a shape built by repeated merge and collapse operations.

8. A Simple Example: Logical OR

(This shows that collapse implements abstraction.)

\section{Example: Logical OR}

Let us attempt to compute a logical OR using Spherepop. Suppose we start
with two spheres $A$ and $B$. We merge them and then collapse the
result:
\[
A \diamond B = \mathrm{collapse}(A \cup B).
\]

If either $A$ or $B$ exists (is ``present'' in the space), then the
merge produces a non-empty region; collapse produces a canonical region
representing “true.” If none exists, there is no region to collapse,
representing “false.”

Thus, the OR operation emerges from geometric merge followed by
collapse. Logical operations naturally arise from spatial interaction
rules.

\section{Values and Regions}

We define a \emph{region} as any connected subset of a spatial domain~$P$.
In illustrations we typically use spheres in three dimensions, but any

11. Collapse and Merge

(formal but minimal)

\section{Collapse and Merge}

Let $A$ and $B$ be regions. Define

\[
\mathrm{pop}(A)
\]

as an abstract operation that reduces $A$ to a canonical representative
of its equivalence class. This collapse removes internal detail
(generalizing ``simplification'').

Define merge as
\[
A\diamond B := \mathrm{pop}(A\cup B).
\]

Merge therefore consists of a geometric union followed by collapse.

The key observation is that collapse identifies values: many different internal configurations are equivalent after collapse. Computation becomes the manipulation of these equivalence classes.

12. Spherepop Programs

(minimal syntax)

\section{Spherepop Programs}

A Spherepop expression $t$ is generated by the grammar

\[
t ::= A \ \mid\ \mathrm{pop}(t)\ \mid\ t_1\diamond t_2,
\]

where $A$ is a primitive value.

This definition resembles programming language syntax, but note what is missing: there are no variables, lambdas, types, or algebraic operators. Everything emerges through geometry.

13. Operational Semantics

(very recognizable by PL people, but approachable)

\section{Operational Semantics}

Let $\llbracket t\rrbracket$ denote the geometric region represented by
the term $t$. Evaluation is given by:

\[
\llbracket A\rrbracket = A,
\qquad
\llbracket \mathrm{pop}(t)\rrbracket = \mathrm{pop}(\llbracket t\rrbracket),
\]

\[
\llbracket t_1\diamond t_2\rrbracket =
\mathrm{pop}(\llbracket t_1\rrbracket\cup\llbracket t_2\rrbracket).
\]

This means evaluation is straightforward: we always compute a shape by applying collapse operations on unioned regions.

14. Normal Forms

(This section hints at computability power)

\section{Normal Forms}

A term $t$ is in \emph{normal form} if $\mathrm{pop}(t)=t$. Intuitively,
a normal form is a region that cannot be simplified further. Every
evaluation sequence in Spherepop eventually produces a normal form (up
to geometric equivalence), making Spherepop a deterministic, terminating
computation model for most practical cases.

In later sections we show that general computation can be represented
in Spherepop, including non-terminating structures, but these require
explicit constructions rather than accidental loops.

16. Encoding Information Geometrically

(very intuitive)

\section{Encoding Information Geometrically}

A region $A$ encodes information through its spatial properties.
The size, position, and relation to other regions all represent
computational state. Collapse destroys some of this information,
leaving only global structure, while merge creates new global structure
from local interactions.

By controlling how and when collapse occurs, we can encode logical
conditions, composition rules, and control flow. Importantly,
Spherepop does not require explicit symbolic values to represent
information—the geometry itself is the information.

17. Boolean Values

(more examples)

\section{Boolean Values}

Boolean values can be encoded spatially using the presence or absence
of a region. A region $T$ denotes ``true'' and its absence denotes
``false.'' Collapse maps any non-empty region into a canonical region
representing $T$. Logical operations arise by combining regions and
collapsing them.

\[
A \lor B := \mathrm{pop}(A\cup B).
\]

Logical AND is more subtle: if collapse precedes union, AND may require
encoding absence or negation by controlled collapse rules. We return to
formal boolean encoding after introducing conditional collapse patterns.

18. Controlled Collapse

(this resembles functions and conditionals)

\section{Controlled Collapse}

To represent conditionals, we use collapse as a controlled abstraction.
Given a region $A$, collapse produces a canonical shape $C(A)$, losing
interior structure but preserving global presence. If collapse is
delayed or forced at specific stages, we implement control flow:
collapse only after certain interactions occur.

An intuitive reading:

if A then B

means: merge with $A$, then collapse only after $A$ influences the combined region. Conditionals arise from when collapse is applied, not from explicit syntax.

\section{Composition}

A useful way to think about Spherepop is that collapse and merge

\[
A \diamond B = \mathrm{pop}(A\cup B).

23. Syntax

\section{Syntax}

Spherepop expressions (``programs'') are built from three constructs:

\begin{itemize}
\item a primitive region $A$,
\item collapse: $\mathrm{pop}(t)$,
\item merge: $t_1 \diamond t_2$.
\end{itemize}

Formally,
\[
t ::= A\mid\mathrm{pop}(t)\mid t_1\diamond t_2.
\]
This grammar resembles the inductive syntax used in traditional programming languages, but its semantics will be geometric rather than symbolic.

24. Semantics

(Gentle but rigorous.)

\section{Semantics}

Let $\llbracket t\rrbracket$ denote the geometric region that $t$ represents.

Evaluation rules are:

\[
\llbracket A\rrbracket = A,
\qquad
\llbracket\mathrm{pop}(t)\rrbracket
    =\mathrm{pop}(\llbracket t\rrbracket),
\qquad
\llbracket t_1\diamond t_2\rrbracket
   =\mathrm{pop}(\llbracket t_1\rrbracket\cup\llbracket t_2\rrbracket).
\]

The semantics define computation as a transformation of regions rather than transformation of symbols or numerical arrays. The evaluation process involves actual geometry (or an equivalent simulation of geometry).

25. Pop as Function Application

(Bridging to programmers)

\section{Pop as Function Application}

In ordinary programming languages, we apply a function $f$ to a value $x$
to produce $f(x)$. In Spherepop, collapse $\,\mathrm{pop}\,$ plays a similar
role: it transforms a region into a simpler one.

The analogy is not literal—collapse is not a function evaluated symbolically—but operationally, collapse reduces an input to an output that participates in later computation. Function application is replaced by geometric abstraction.

26. Merge as Composition

(Again: gentle analogy)

\section{Merge as Composition}

When we combine two computations in a classical language, we compose
their functions. In Spherepop, we merge the regions and collapse the
result. Merge therefore encodes composition.

\[
A\diamond B = \mathrm{pop}(A\cup B).
\]

All higher composition patterns (including pipelines, chains, and functional composition) can be constructed recursively from merge.

Part VI — Spherepop and Machine Learning

\section{Spherepop and Machine Learning}

Machine learning is usually described as the transformation of vectors
by linear maps and nonlinearities. Spherepop reformulates these
operations geometrically: instead of arrays of numbers, we manipulate
regions in space. Instead of linear transformations, we merge and
collapse shapes. Instead of activations, we perform controlled collapse
patterns. This geometric perspective makes certain aspects of ML
intuitive and visually interpretable.

41. Neural Layers as Merge–Collapse Pipelines

\section{Neural Layers as Merge--Collapse Pipelines}

A typical neural layer applies a transformation $x\mapsto \sigma(Wx+b)$.
In Spherepop, we replace this with a pattern of merges (combining
regions representing input information) followed by collapse (projecting
the combined region into a canonical representation). The sequence

\[
x \Rightarrow (x\diamond A_1) \diamond A_2 \diamond \cdots
\]

combined with collapse steps, implements a functional transformation
analogous to a neural layer. Collapse reduces detail (nonlinearity)
while merge aggregates information across the input.

Intuition: linear maps mix information; merge mixes geometry.

42. Nonlinearities as Collapse Functions

\section{Nonlinearities as Collapse Functions}

Neural networks introduce nonlinearity via activation functions such as
ReLU or sigmoid. Spherepop does not require explicit activation
functions, because collapse already discards interior detail and
projects values to a simpler representation. Collapse therefore plays
a role similar to nonlinear activation, preventing the composition from
reducing to a linear transformation.

Collapse is the nonlinearity.

43. Pooling as Geometric Abstraction

\section{Pooling as Geometric Abstraction}

Pooling operations aggregate multiple values while discarding local
information. Spherepop's merge-and-collapse naturally behaves like a
pooling layer: multiple regions are merged into a single region and
collapsed, abstracting fine detail. Max-pooling corresponds to selecting
tighter collapse; average-pooling corresponds to smooth contraction.

Graphically, pooling = merge + collapse.

44. Convolution as Structured Interaction

\section{Convolution as Structured Interaction}

In convolutional networks, the receptive field controls how local
patches interact. In Spherepop, receptive fields correspond to spatial
adjacency: regions merge only when they overlap. Collapse abstracts the
merged region into a simpler representation. The convolution kernel
becomes geometric overlap; the kernel size corresponds to interaction
radius.

Instead of sliding DOT-products, we apply spatial union + collapse.

45. Attention as Spatial Weighting

(this is key—educators love this)

\section{Attention as Spatial Weighting}

Attention mechanisms weight interactions between values. In Spherepop,
attention can be viewed as controlling which regions merge. Regions that
spatially ``attend'' to each other merge and collapse; regions that do
not interact remain separate. This yields a geometric interpretation of
attention as selective spatial interaction rather than tensor weighting.

No dot-products required for intuition.

46. Aggregators and Message Passing

\section{Aggregators and Message Passing}

Graph neural networks aggregate neighbor information:
\[
h_v^{(k+1)}
  = \mathrm{AGG}\{h_u^{(k)} : u\in N(v)\}.
\]

Spherepop performs analogous aggregation by merging spatially adjacent
regions and collapsing the result. Message passing corresponds to
iterated merge-collapse sequences in local neighborhoods.

Part VII — Spherepop and Tensor Logic

\section{Spherepop and Tensor Logic}

Tensor Logic proposes that reasoning in AI can be described using
tensor algebra, where logical inference corresponds to tensor
operations (addition, multiplication, contraction) over embedding
spaces. In this formalism, computation is expressed as transformations
of high-dimensional arrays. Spherepop approaches computation
differently: values are geometric regions, and computation corresponds
to collapse and merge of these regions.

50. Shared Motivation

(both want “geometry beneath AI”)

\section{Shared Motivation}

Both Spherepop and Tensor Logic attempt to describe computation in a
way that unifies symbolic and neural methods. Tensor Logic does this by
encoding logic in tensor operations. Spherepop does this by encoding
logic in geometry.

In this sense, the two are aligned: each recognizes that traditional
symbolic logic is insufficient for modern AI and machine learning, and
each seeks a foundation that includes both symbolic and continuous
computation.

51. Tensor Algebra vs. Spatial Interaction

\section{Tensor Algebra vs.\ Spatial Interaction}

Tensor Logic describes computation using algebra on vectors and
tensors. Spherepop describes computation using spatial interaction.
Where Tensor Logic uses matrix multiplication, Spherepop uses merge.
Where Tensor Logic uses non-linear activation, Spherepop uses collapse.

In principle,

W x \quad\text{(tensor logic)}

\mathrm{pop}(A\cup B) \quad\text{(spherepop)}.

One abstracts algebra from geometry; the other abstracts geometry from algebra.

52. Locality vs. Abstraction

\section{Locality vs.\ Abstraction}

Tensor operations combine all components of a vector simultaneously,
regardless of spatial adjacency. Spherepop interactions are determined
by geometric adjacency: regions merge only when they physically
overlap. This spatial locality may be exploited for interpretability and
visualization purposes.


---

53. Collapse and Tensor Nonlinearity

\section{Collapse and Tensor Nonlinearity}

Nonlinear activation in Tensor Logic is introduced symbolically through
activation functions. In Spherepop, collapse already plays this role:
collapse discards interior detail, preventing merge from being purely
linear. Collapse therefore behaves like a built-in nonlinearity.


---

54. Tensor Composition vs. Merge Composition

\section{Tensor Composition vs.\ Merge Composition}

Tensor networks construct complex operations by composing tensor
contractions. Spherepop constructs complex operations by composing
merge and collapse. Both mechanisms support arbitrarily complex
computations, but the underlying mathematical substrate differs
(algebraic vs.\ geometric).

(the respectful core idea)

\section{Projection Viewpoint}

A useful way to compare the two approaches is as follows:

Tensor Logic may be seen as a \emph{linear projection} of Spherepop in

vectors. When we forget spatial structure and retain only algebraic
information, Spherepop computations become tensor computations.

Conversely, we can view Spherepop as a geometric lifting of Tensor

representations.

This projection language is crucial: it frames Spherepop as a geometric generalization, not a competitor.

\section{Why Both Views Are Valuable}

Spherepop provides interpretability, visualization, and intuitive

Together, they offer complementary insights into computation in
machine learning systems.

58. Merge Implementation

\section{Merge Implementation}

To merge two spheres $A$ and $B$, compute their union. For simple
spherical representations, merge can be approximated by computing a new
sphere enclosing both:

\[
\mathrm{merge}(A,B)
  = \text{minimal enclosing sphere}(A,B),
\]

followed by collapse, which may further shrink or regularize the shape.

For more complex shapes, use voxel union (bitwise OR), mesh union (Constructive Solid Geometry), or implicit-level-set blending.

59. Collapse Implementation

\section{Collapse Implementation}

Collapse maps a region to a canonical form. For spheres, this can be
implemented by shrinking to a fixed radius or scaling to a standard size
or simplifying the mesh. For voxel representations, collapse might be
threshold-based erosion or morphological simplification.

The exact collapse rule depends on the domain, but the key idea remains: collapse reduces internal detail while preserving global presence.

60. Evaluator Loop

\section{Evaluator Loop}

The simplest Spherepop interpreter uses a repeat-until-stable loop:

\begin{enumerate}
\item apply merge operations,
\item apply collapse operations,
\item update all regions,
\item stop when no changes occur.
\end{enumerate}

This loop simulates computation until a fixed point (normal form) is
reached.

61. Example: Two-Sphere Merge

\section{Example: Two-Sphere Merge}

Given two spheres with centers $c_A$ and $c_B$, merged radius could be:

\[
r = \|c_A-c_B\|/2 + \max(r_A,r_B),
\]

and merged center could interpolate between them. Then collapse shrinks
the result slightly or replaces it with a canonical sphere of fixed
radius. This simple mechanism implements merge-and-collapse visually.
