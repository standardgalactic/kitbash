Although Doctorow’s Toronto represented only one of many human aggregation nodes in the Late Plasticocene, it serves as a clear miniature of the period’s wider informational practices. The city’s dense structure of concrete towers and fossil-fueled circulation resembled what we now identify as “proto-network formations”—not in the biological sense, as with kelp pediments, but culturally: millions of individual agents exchanging symbolic signals through airwaves, fiber conduits, and localized computation boxes.

Doctorow’s early environment therefore exposed him to a contradiction typical of the era: individuals were densely interconnected at the symbolic level while remaining materially separated by private property boundaries, combustion transit mechanisms, and rigid architectural geometry. These contradictions, visible in fossilized building fragments found beneath the current root mats, help explain Doctorow’s lifelong fascination with freeing information from confinement—he lived in a world that connected minds while enclosing everything else.
