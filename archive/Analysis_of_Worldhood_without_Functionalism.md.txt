# An Analysis of Worldhood, Irreversibility, and Intelligence

## Executive Summary

This briefing document synthesizes a novel anti-functionalist framework for understanding intelligence, which argues that the prevailing computational and behavioral criteria are insufficient. The central thesis is that genuine intelligence requires worldhood, a condition defined as the possession of a non-recoverable past that irreversibly constrains future possibilities. Systems designed to preserve full reversibility, regardless of their performance, are deemed structurally incapable of inhabiting a world and thus lack a necessary component of intelligence.

This perspective directly challenges functionalist assumptions, which equate intelligence with behavioral equivalence or representational accuracy. The framework formalizes this critique with the No-World Theorem, which states that any system whose future option space is preserved across all transitions is necessarily world-less. Consequently, functional equivalence, which permits internal reversibility, cannot guarantee intelligence in this deeper sense.

To operationalize these principles, the Spherepop calculus is introduced as an event-driven formalism. It distinguishes between world-constituting operations like pop (irreversible commitment) and bind (long-term constraint) and world-erasing operations like collapse (history erasure), which characterizes the architecture of many contemporary AI systems.

The framework is generalized beyond cognition to physical ontology, treating objects as processes that persist by surviving irreversible constraint tests. This model is then elevated to a continuous field-theoretic description—the Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum (RSVP)—which describes the formation and erosion of worlds through fields of optionality, commitment, and accumulated constraint. Finally, the concept of care is refined for cognitive systems as a form of attentional compression that, unlike the collapse-based summarization in AI, preserves global coherence. The overarching conclusion is that intelligence is not the capacity for limitless computation, but the capacity to live with the consequences of having acted.

## The Central Argument: Beyond Functionalism to Worldhood

The analysis begins by addressing a perceived distinctive shallowness in contemporary AI systems. Despite their fluency and adaptability, they generate meaning-like artifacts without sustaining meaning and respond without responsibility. The core argument posits this is not a contingent limitation to be solved by scale, but a structural deficit rooted in the absence of worldhood.

## Critique of Functionalism

Prevailing functionalist theories of intelligence define intelligence by what a system does—its input-output behavior or representational performance. This framework argues this is insufficient because it ignores reversibility. Systems designed to be reset, cloned, or rolled back without loss cannot accumulate a meaningful history. Flexibility is gained at the cost of depth.

## Defining Worldhood

Worldhood is not an external environment. It is an ontological condition: to inhabit a world is to be bound by one’s own history in a way that cannot be undone without loss. A world is a structured field of significance within which action has consequences. Care is formalized as self-binding through irreversible foreclosure of future possibilities.

## Formalizing the Critique: The No-World Theorem

Key definitions include actional possibility space, optionality, event, event cost, and world as the intersection of historical constraints. The No-World Theorem states that if a system preserves optionality across all transitions, it is world-less. Functional equivalence therefore cannot guarantee intelligence.

## An Architecture of Commitment: The Spherepop Calculus

Spherepop distinguishes four operators: pop, bind, refuse, and collapse. Pop and refuse irreversibly reduce optionality. Bind structures future commitments. Collapse erases history while preserving flexibility. World-bearing systems accumulate irreversible history; collapse-dominated systems do not.

## Generalizing the Framework: From Cognition to Physical Ontology

Optionality is refined into a possibility lattice. Events are non-invertible descents in this lattice. Physical objects are treated as processes that persist by surviving irreversible constraint tests. Identity is the residue of constraint survival.

## Macroscopic Dynamics: The Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum

The RSVP model introduces scalar optionality density, vector commitment flow, and entropy fields. World-bearing regimes maintain gradients and coherent flow. Optimization-dominated regimes flatten gradients and erase meaning.

## Cognitive Refinement: Care as Attentional Compression

Using sheaf theory, care is modeled as coherent compression of possibilities that preserves global consistency. Collapse-based summarization, by contrast, sacrifices coherence for efficiency.

## Implications for Artificial Intelligence and Technology

Modern AI systems externalize irreversibility and rely on collapse. They simulate intelligence without accumulating internal worldhood. True intelligence requires accepting irreversible loss of optionality and responsibility for history. The central claim is that intelligence is not the capacity to compute indefinitely, but the capacity to live with the consequences of having acted.
