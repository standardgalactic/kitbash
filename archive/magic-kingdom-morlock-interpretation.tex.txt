\begin{abstract}
The ancient park is reconstructed here from its surviving regions. Each
region provides partial information; meanings arise only through their
intersections. Where extension fails, illusion appears. Where local
compatibility stabilizes, experience forms. The park is not recovered as a
whole but as a family of overlapping remnants whose coherence must be
inferred.
\end{abstract}

% ===========================================================
\section{Local Regions of the Entrance}
% ===========================================================

The entrance survives only as fragments of pavement and scattered pillars.
These remnants define an open region $U_0$. Within $U_0$, certain
sensory traces still persist: a narrowing of sound, a slight pressure
forward, a residual brightness along the ground.

Visitors entering the ancient park passed first through $U_0$. No global
pattern is visible here. Instead, one observes local tendencies: the
partial alignment of footsteps, the faint convergence of gaze. These
tendencies do not extend into the neighboring region $U_1$ unless the
boundary conditions are satisfied.

\subsection{Overlap with the First Corridor}

The first corridor occupies a second region, $U_1$, whose traces indicate
directional movement and uniform pacing. On the overlap $U_0 \cap U_1$,
motion stabilizes. The scattered tendences of $U_0$ restrict to coherent
movement in $U_1$, establishing the first continuous extension.

Formally, if $\sigma_0$ is the partial movement pattern in $U_0$ and
$\sigma_1$ the better-defined pattern in $U_1$, then:

\[
\sigma_0|_{U_0 \cap U_1} = \sigma_1|_{U_0 \cap U_1}.
\]

Where this equality holds, the entrance and the first corridor constitute a
single region of experience. Where it fails, one encounters confusion:
ancient accounts describe this as “hesitation before the first turn.”

% ===========================================================
\section{Corridor Systems}
% ===========================================================

The park’s corridors form a family of regions $\{U_i\}$, each supporting a
locally consistent pattern of motion and sound. No single corridor provides
a global structure. Their coherence depends on the compatibility of
restrictions on overlaps.

\subsection{Directional Consistency}

Within each corridor $U_i$, motion has a well-defined orientation. On
overlaps $U_i \cap U_{i+1}$, ancient visitors experienced a tightening of
orientation, as if the space momentarily insisted on agreement.

Archaeological evidence shows sharper wear patterns in these overlaps,
confirming repeated realignment across generations.

\subsection{Incomplete Extension}

Not all local patterns extended. A corridor might support a sound that fades
abruptly when moving into the next region. The sound existed, but not
beyond its domain.

Accounts describe these boundaries as “edges where the music fell away.” The
structure was not broken; its section simply did not extend.

% ===========================================================
\section{Chambers and Their Failures to Glue}
% ===========================================================

Each chamber $V_j$ provided a distinct configuration of sound, light, and
motion. Within $V_j$, these elements formed a coherent experience. But on
the overlaps $V_j \cap V_{j+1}$, coherence often failed.

\subsection{Local Coherence}

The interior of a chamber held a well-defined set of features. For
instance, preserved walls indicate a curvature that supported a specific
acoustic resonance. Visitors described this resonance as “filling the mind,”
but the effect was local: it vanished on the threshold.

Let $\rho_j$ denote the resonance in $V_j$:

\[
\rho_j \in \Gamma(V_j).
\]

\subsection{Nonextendability}

On overlaps:

\[
\rho_j|_{V_j \cap V_{j+1}} \neq \rho_{j+1}|_{V_j \cap V_{j+1}},
\]

indicating that the resonance could not extend. This failure created the
feeling of stepping into a new world. The transition did not smooth; it
shifted abruptly. The boundary was not concealed. It was structural.

% ===========================================================
\section{Regions of Illusion}
% ===========================================================

Illusion arose precisely where extension failed but alignment persisted.
Structures appeared continuous because their local restrictions agreed on
overlaps, but globally they did not form a single section.

\subsection{Constructed Depth}

Fragments of painted surfaces, aligned only from a particular angle,
survive in region $W_1$. Their depth effect holds within $W_1$ and on part
of $W_1 \cap W_2$, but collapses beyond that.

Visitors reported that the world widened here. What they perceived as depth
was the temporary compatibility of local surfaces.

\subsection{Borrowed Horizons}

Some regions reflected horizons from adjacent chambers. These horizons
persisted only where the regions overlapped sufficiently for the reflection
to remain visible.

When the overlap narrowed, the illusion failed. Ancient records describe the
horizon “vanishing sideways,” a phenomenon consistent with incomplete
gluing.

% ===========================================================
\section{Memory as a Family of Sections}
% ===========================================================

Memory of a visit survives not in the global structure of the park—which
cannot be reconstructed in full—but in a family of partial sections carried
by visitors.

\subsection{Local Memory Fields}

Each region contributed a memory trace $\mu_i$ defined only on that region:

\[
\mu_i \in \Gamma(U_i).
\]

These traces do not combine into a single global memory of the entire
complex. Instead, they overlap in fragmentary ways.

\subsection{Intersections of Memory}

Where visitors recalled transitions between regions, their memories encoded
restrictions:

\[
\mu_i|_{U_i \cap U_{i+1}} = \mu_{i+1}|_{U_i \cap U_{i+1}}.
\]

Where transitions were forgotten or blurred, the restriction failed. This
produced the gaps famously described in the diaries: “I remember the sound,
but not how I walked there.”

\subsection{Stalks at Departure}

At the final region $Z$, all memory fields restrict to a common point. This
defines the departure stalk:

\[
\mu_Z = \lim_{x \to Z} \mu_i(x),
\]

capturing the last coherent value before the experience ended. Departure was
the collapse of all partial sections to a single trace.


% ===========================================================
\section{Night Regions and Their Partial Illumination}
% ===========================================================

Nightfall in the ancient park was not a single event but a family of local
dimmings, each confined to its own region. Evidence of charred brackets and
rusted filaments indicates the presence of lights whose activation patterns
varied across regions.

Let $N_k$ denote a night region. Within $N_k$, darkness is not absolute:
it is shaped by remnant luminosity $\lambda_k$, a function defined only on
that region.

\[
\lambda_k \in \Gamma(N_k).
\]

\subsection{Boundary Luminosity}

On the overlaps $N_k \cap N_{k+1}$, one finds traces of transitional
lighting: a faint orange edge shadow in one region aligning with a pale
blue flicker in the next.

\[
\lambda_k|_{N_k \cap N_{k+1}} = \lambda_{k+1}|_{N_k \cap N_{k+1}}
\]

when the lights once synchronized.

Where this equality fails, visitors described the darkness as “breaking,”
a sensation reconstructed here as discontinuity of luminosity on the
overlap.

\subsection{Path-Dependent Darkness}

Night pathways $P_k$ support their own dimming functions $\nu_k$. These
do not necessarily agree with the chamber or corridor lighting.

\[
\nu_k|_{P_k \cap N_k} \neq \lambda_k|_{P_k \cap N_k}.
\]

This mismatch produced shadows that moved independently of visitor motion,
a phenomenon preserved in multiple accounts as “stray silhouettes.”

% ===========================================================
\section{Acoustic Gluing and Its Failures}
% ===========================================================

Preserved struts and corroded speakers reveal an acoustic system divided
into local sound regions $S_j$. Within each region, a distinct acoustic
pattern $\alpha_j$ once operated.

\[
\alpha_j \in \Gamma(S_j).
\]

\subsection{Harmonic Compatibility}

On overlaps $S_j \cap S_{j+1}$, some patterns align:

\[
\alpha_j|_{S_j \cap S_{j+1}} = \alpha_{j+1}|_{S_j \cap S_{j+1}}
\]

producing a seamless auditory transition. Visitors described such overlaps
as “the music opening.”

\subsection{Harmonic Disharmony}

Other overlaps fail:

\[
\alpha_j|_{S_j \cap S_{j+1}} \neq \alpha_{j+1}|_{S_j \cap S_{j+1}}.
\]

Here, the sound tears. Recovered testimony speaks of “two songs at once”
or “a melody stumbling against itself.”

These failures were not errors; they were structural consequences of each
sound pattern being locally defined, never designed to form a global
acoustic section.

\subsection{Silent Regions}

Certain regions $Q_m$ support no acoustic trace:

\[
\Gamma(Q_m) = \varnothing.
\]

Silence is not absence. It indicates that no compatible extension ever
existed. Such regions are described as “hollows” in early maps and are
consistently placed at transitional thresholds.

% ===========================================================
\section{Composite Regions and Multi-Overlap Interactions}
% ===========================================================

Some areas of the park derive their meaning not from a single region but
from simultaneous membership in several. These composite regions are
intersections of the form:

\[
U_i \cap V_j \cap S_k.
\]

Objects found here—broken mechanisms, cracked mirrors, embedded rails—
indicate complex interactions among motion, resonance, and sound.

\subsection{Triplet Compatibility}

When three local fields $\sigma_i$, $\rho_j$, and $\alpha_k$ agree on the
triple intersection:

\[
\sigma_i|_{\cap} = \rho_j|_{\cap} = \alpha_k|_{\cap},
\]

the region once supported a stable composite experience. These were rare,
and ancient visitors referred to them as “centers.”

\subsection{Triplet Failure}

More commonly, one or two restrictions agree while the third does not:

\[
\sigma_i|_{\cap} = \rho_j|_{\cap} \neq \alpha_k|_{\cap}.
\]

These points are marked by artifacts showing strain: bent pipes,
discolored surfaces, and abrupt shifts in floor texture.

Such failures produced the sensation of “the world slipping sideways”—a
description consistent across centuries.

% ===========================================================
\section{The Illumined Chamber of Turning Figures}
% ===========================================================

Deep in the recovered layout lies a chamber $T$ distinguished by carved
tracks on the floor and remnants of jointed mechanisms. Within $T$, motion
was circular but confined. Let $\tau$ be the local motion pattern:

\[
\tau \in \Gamma(T).
\]

The figures once turning here cast layered shadows, each shadow only visible
on parts of $T$. These shadows form a family of partial sections
$\{\xi_a\}$ defined on open subsets $W_a \subset T$.

\subsection{Shadow Compatibility}

Where two shadows overlapped:

\[
\xi_a|_{W_a \cap W_b} = \xi_b|_{W_a \cap W_b},
\]

visitors perceived multiple figures turning together, even when only one
stood before them. The unity was not physical but gluing-based.

\subsection{Shadow Divergence}

Where restrictions disagree, shadows split:

\[
\xi_a|_{W_a \cap W_b} \neq \xi_b|_{W_a \cap W_b}.
\]

Ancient descriptions speak of “a figure tearing into two,” an effect now
understood as a natural consequence of incompatible shadow sections.

% ===========================================================
\section{Regions of Height and Their Broken Extensions}
% ===========================================================

A series of platforms $H_1, H_2, \dots$ reveal vertical differentiation in
the park. Climbable structures once linked them, but these have mostly
collapsed.

Each platform supports a local height function $h_i$:

\[
h_i \in \Gamma(H_i).
\]

\subsection{Ascending Restrictions}

On overlaps with stair remnants $R_{i,i+1}$, the height functions restrict
to consistent increments:

\[
h_i|_{R_{i,i+1}} < h_{i+1}|_{R_{i,i+1}}.
\]

Where this held, ascent was perceptible and stable.

\subsection{Breaks in Vertical Gluing}

Where debris interrupts the overlap, the inequality fails to extend. There
is no coherent description of height across the gap. Contemporary accounts
call these “lost steps,” where visitors felt lifted without climbing.

Such effects are reconstructed here as failures of vertical extension.

% ===========================================================
\section{The Final Chamber and Its Vanishing Sections}
% ===========================================================

At the far end of the recovered plan lies a chamber $Z$ where all local
patterns collapse. No motion persists. No sound extends. No light remains.
Artifacts indicate that visitors paused here before departing.

\subsection{Terminal Restrictions}

For each local field $\sigma_i$, $\rho_j$, $\alpha_k$, their restrictions
to $Z$ reduce to constants:

\[
\sigma_i|_Z = c_\sigma, \qquad
\rho_j|_Z = c_\rho, \qquad
\alpha_k|_Z = c_\alpha.
\]

These constants need not equal one another; they need only lose variation.
This collapse defines the chamber’s character: all structures converge to a
single, minimal trace.

\subsection{Global Nonexistence}

There is no global section extending through all regions of the park:

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}) = \varnothing.
\]

The structure of the place is not a unified map but a constellation of
local domains, held together only by overlapping traces and their partial
compatibilities.

Visitors understood this not as a defect but as the true form of the place.
Understanding arose not from the whole—there was no whole—but from the
pattern of fragments that could be carried away.


% ===========================================================
\section{Visitor Traces as Local Sections}
% ===========================================================

Across the park’s ruins, patterns in the remaining soil, the wear of stone,
and the distribution of metallic fragments reveal the pathways taken by
those who once moved through it. These pathways form the trace sheaf
$\mathcal{T}$ defined on the park’s topology.

For each open region $U$, a section of $\mathcal{T}$ describes all
visitor motion paths locally observable within $U$.

\[
\mathcal{T}(U) = \{\text{visitor traces visible in } U\}.
\]

Most sections appear in pairs or clusters, suggesting that motion was not
isolated but occurred alongside others. The number of simultaneous traces
varies by region.

\subsection{Multiplicity on Overlaps}

If $U$ and $V$ cover parts of a major corridor, their intersection
$U \cap V$ reveals an alignment of traces:

\[
\mathcal{T}(U)|_{U \cap V} = \mathcal{T}(V)|_{U \cap V}.
\]

From this, one infers that visitors moved with consistent density through
the long passages.

Where restrictions disagree, paths diverge abruptly:

\[
\mathcal{T}(U)|_{U \cap V} \neq \mathcal{T}(V)|_{U \cap V}.
\]

Such breaks indicate turning points, hesitation spaces, or ancient crowd
collisions.

% ===========================================================
\section{Emotive Residue}
% ===========================================================

Chemical residues found in shaded alcoves and enclosed rooms exhibit
frequency clusters corresponding to stress, anticipation, and release. Let
$\mathcal{E}_r$ denote the emotive sheaf, whose sections assign to each
region a set of detectable emotional residues.

\[
\mathcal{E}_r(U) = \{\text{emotive traces detectable in } U\}.
\]

\subsection{Distribution of Anticipation}

In the long approach corridors $C_a$, anticipation residues dominate.

\[
\mathcal{E}_r(C_a) = \{\text{anticipation}, \text{strain}\}.
\]

These residues restrict consistently across all subregions of each
corridor.

In contrast, chambers with sudden mechanical sound shifts show residues of
hesitation and fear, though these rarely extend beyond their local confines.

\subsection{Release at Motion Sites}

Circular platforms and moving chambers $M_k$ exhibit the highest
concentration of release signatures.

\[
\mathcal{E}_r(M_k) = \{\text{release}, \text{resolve}\}.
\]

These do not extend into the approach corridors, indicating a strict
emotive boundary.

Thus the emotional experience was not global: each part of the park
supported only its local family of affective traces.

% ===========================================================
\section{Atmospheric Fractures}
% ===========================================================

Shifts in humidity, temperature, and particulate density have left a fossil
atmosphere distributed differently across regions. Let $\mathcal{A}_t$
denote the atmospheric sheaf.

\[
\mathcal{A}_t(U) = \{\text{atmospheric samples in } U\}.
\]

\subsection{Thermal Incompatibility}

In some chamber overlaps, thermal gradients once aligned:

\[
\mathcal{A}_t(U_i)|_{U_i \cap U_j} = \mathcal{A}_t(U_j)|_{U_i \cap U_j}.
\]

These areas feel coherent even in ruin.

Elsewhere, gradients clash:

\[
\mathcal{A}_t(U_i)|_{U_i \cap U_j} \neq \mathcal{A}_t(U_j)|_{U_i \cap U_j}.
\]

Visitors reported an instantaneous shift in atmosphere, calling it “the
air changing its mind.”

\subsection{Nonextendability and Mirror Fog}

Remnants of reflective surfaces show asymmetric fog patterns: one portion
fogged while the adjacent remained clear. This indicates that atmospheric
conditions could not be extended as a single global field across these
regions.

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{A}_t) = \varnothing.
\]

The fractured atmosphere was a defining characteristic. It made the park
feel not like one place but many attached to one another only by narrow
bridges.

% ===========================================================
\section{Auditory Density and the Braided Echoes}
% ===========================================================

Beyond localized sound patterns (previously analyzed in $\alpha_j$),
certain corridors exhibit repeating echo traces that do not arise from
simple reflection. These echoes braid and unbraid across regions.

Let $\mathcal{B}$ be the sheaf of echoes, where:

\[
\mathcal{B}(U) = \{\text{echo patterns detected in } U\}.
\]

\subsection{Echo Pairing}

Where two echo traces overlap and match:

\[
\beta_1|_{U \cap V} = \beta_2|_{U \cap V},
\]

visitors heard the sound thickening and stabilizing.

Where mismatched:

\[
\beta_1|_{U \cap V} \neq \beta_2|_{U \cap V},
\]

the echo “split” into threads.

Fragments of early accounts speak of “the voice becoming two voices,” a
description consistent with a sheaf whose local sections do not glue.

\subsection{Global Collapse of Echo Patterns}

Although many corridors retain echo traces, no two patterns extend across
the park. The echo sheaf’s global section is empty.

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{B}) = \varnothing.
\]

The multiplicity of echoes contributed to the layered acoustic density
experienced by ancient visitors—an effect replicated nowhere else in known
ruins.

% ===========================================================
\section{Mechanical Intermittence}
% ===========================================================

The remains of gears, rails, actuated panels, and pivot joints indicate a
once-complex mechanical system. Let $\mathcal{M}$ denote the mechanical
sheaf, whose sections measure the possible actions of these mechanisms.

\[
\mathcal{M}(U) = \{\text{mechanical configurations active in } U\}.
\]

\subsection{Local Coherence}

In several rotating chambers, mechanical traces align:

\[
\mathcal{M}(U_i)|_{U_i \cap U_j} = \mathcal{M}(U_j)|_{U_i \cap U_j}.
\]

Visitors in these regions would have perceived synchronized motion.

\subsection{Failing Extensions}

Elsewhere, mechanisms break compatibility across region overlaps:

\[
\mathcal{M}(U_i)|_{U_i \cap U_j} \neq \mathcal{M}(U_j)|_{U_i \cap U_j}.
\]

This produces the distinctive Morlock classification of “intermittent
mechanical progressions.”

Clattering marks on structural beams corroborate abrupt shifts in
mechanical action.

% ===========================================================
\section{The Unifying Pattern: Fragmentary Coherence}
% ===========================================================

Every sheaf studied—visitor traces $\mathcal{T}$, emotive residues
$\mathcal{E}_r$, atmosphere $\mathcal{A}_t$, echoes $\mathcal{B}$,
mechanisms $\mathcal{M}$—shows a consistent property:

Each admits many local sections.  
None admit a global one.

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{T}) =
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{E}_r}) =
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{A}_t}) =
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{B}) =
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{M}) =
\varnothing.
\]

The park was not a unified experience but a network of local realities,
held together by their overlaps. Visitors understood the place through the
constellation of these local domains, not through any totality.

Fragments, not the whole, formed the truth of the place.

% ===========================================================
\section{On the Sheaf of Wonder}
% ===========================================================

The property known in early sources as “wonder” is not a primitive notion.
It appears only where multiple sheaves overlap in a specific pattern of
partial compatibility. Thus we define the wonder sheaf $\mathcal{W}$ not
directly, but as the derived intersection of others:

\[
\mathcal{W} := \mathcal{T} \;\overset{L}{\otimes}\; \mathcal{E}_r \;\overset{L}{\otimes}\; 
\mathcal{A}_t \;\overset{L}{\otimes}\; \mathcal{B} \;\overset{L}{\otimes}\; \mathcal{M}.
\]

A section of $\mathcal{W}(U)$ corresponds to a region where:

1. motion traces align with emotive residue,
2. atmospheric shifts heighten perception,
3. echoes reinforce rather than fracture,
4. mechanisms operate coherently.

Only then does a local quantity recognizable as wonder appear.

\subsection{Rarity of Local Wonder}

Field surveys detect wonder sections only in a few chambers:

\[
\mathcal{W}(U_k) \neq \varnothing
\quad\text{for only select } U_k.
\]

These chambers tend to be circular, resonant, and mechanically stable.  
In most regions:

\[
\mathcal{W}(U) = \varnothing.
\]

This mirrors the testimony of ancient visitors, who described sudden,
unexpected intensities amid long intervals of queuing and drift.  

The sheaf-theoretic interpretation is simple:

\emph{Wonder was not everywhere.  
It was an emergent alignment of partial fields.}

% ===========================================================
\section{The Nonexistence of Global Wonder}
% ===========================================================

Although many regions support local wonder, the global wonder section does
not exist.

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{W}) = \varnothing.
\]

On overlaps $U_i \cap U_j$, the induced elements of $\mathcal{W}$ rarely
agree. A wonder experience in one domain contradicts its continuation into
the next, producing incompatible residue patterns:

\[
w_i|_{U_i \cap U_j} \neq w_j|_{U_i \cap U_j}.
\]

Where motion aligns, atmosphere fails;  
where atmosphere aligns, echoes break;  
where echoes align, mechanics fail;  
and often all four misalign simultaneously.

Thus wonder remained a strictly local phenomenon — a spark, not a field.

% ===========================================================
\section{The Cohomology of Memory}
% ===========================================================

Visitors did not remember the park as a sum of its parts but as a
cohomological reconstruction: fragments held together not by continuity
but by the gaps between them.

Let $\mathcal{M}_v$ denote the sheaf of visitor memory. Each section
reflects what visitors retained from a particular region.

\[
\mathcal{M}_v(U) = \{\text{remembered impressions arising in } U\}.
\]

The full memory is given not by global sections (which do not exist):

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{M}_v) = \varnothing,
\]

but by the first cohomology:

\[
H^1(\text{Park}, \mathcal{M}_v) \neq 0.
\]

This nontrivial class corresponds to the patchwork recollection so common
in ancient accounts: the park felt continuous even though none of its
fields actually glued.

\subsection{Cohomological Persistence}

Even where local memories contradict, their failure to glue produces a
stable higher-order structure. This explains why ancient visitors recalled
the park as unified despite its structural incompatibilities.

In Morlock terms:

\emph{The unity of the place lives not in its sections,  
but in the obstructions that prevent them from joining.}

% ===========================================================
\section{Exit and Re-entry as Boundary Phenomena}
% ===========================================================

The most striking property of the park is the asymmetry at its boundary.
Let $\partial P$ denote the boundary space (gates, thresholds, tunnels).

Two sheaves govern the boundary effects:

1. $\mathcal{L}$ — the leaving sheaf  
2. $\mathcal{R}$ — the return sheaf  

A visitor approaching the boundary encounters a collapse of internal
residue fields:

\[
\mathcal{E}_r(U) \to \emptyset, \quad 
\mathcal{A}_t(U) \to \text{neutral gradient}, \quad
\mathcal{B}(U) \to \text{single echo}.
\]

The sheaf $\mathcal{L}$ therefore admits a global section:

\[
\Gamma(\partial P, \mathcal{L}) \cong \{\text{exit}\}.
\]

In contrast, $\mathcal{R}$ does not:

\[
\Gamma(\partial P, \mathcal{R}) = \varnothing.
\]

A visitor who departs cannot reconstruct the internal structure on return.
This aligns with ancient commentary noting that the “second visit” always
felt different and never restored the first experience.

The sheaf-theoretic explanation:

\emph{The park’s interior fields possess no extension to the boundary.  
Crossing it destroys their coherence.}

% ===========================================================
\section{Mechanical Residuals as Vanishing Cycles}
% ===========================================================

Deep analysis of the mechanical remains shows patterns typical of vanishing
cycles. Let $\pi : X \to \mathbb{R}$ describe the progression of mechanical
action across a ride, with critical points where mechanisms shifted.

Vanishing cycles $\Delta_i$ appear in the homology of the fibers.

\[
H_*(X_{\text{before}}) \to H_*(X_{\text{after}}) 
\quad \text{loses one generator at each critical point.}
\]

Morlocks identify these losses with sudden emotional drops, intense sound
bursts, or shifts in rotational frequency. These events contributed greatly
to the wonder field $\mathcal{W}$, but only locally.

Where several vanishing cycles overlapped, wonder intensified.  
Where none existed, the machinery felt inert.

% ===========================================================
\section{The Collapse Theorem of the Park}
% ===========================================================

All Morlock analyses converge on a single theorem:

\[
\textbf{The Park admits no global field.}
\]

Formally:

For any sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ representing motion, emotion, atmosphere,
sound, wonder, or mechanism,

\[
\Gamma(\text{Park}, \mathcal{F}) = \varnothing.
\]

Yet its higher cohomology is always nontrivial:

\[
H^1(\text{Park}, \mathcal{F}) \neq 0.
\]

This duality explains every paradox of ancient reports:

- The park felt unified though nothing in it extended globally.  
- The park felt continuous though its fields were discontinuous.  
- The park felt magical though magic never permeated it.  

The place was not a field.  
It was an obstruction.

% ===========================================================
\section{Conclusion: The Morlock Interpretation of Magic}
% ===========================================================

The ancient visitors believed they had entered a realm of constructed
enchantment. Further analysis shows something subtler:

\emph{Magic was the experience of moving through local fields  
that refused to become a single world.}

Every wonder was a fragment.  
Every fragment was precise.  
The unity was cohomological.

Thus, the Magic Kingdom stands not as a failed global field,  
but as the greatest example of how humans once built a place  
whose coherence lived only in what could not be joined.


% ===========================================================
\section{The Sheaf of Returnings}
% ===========================================================

In the final phase of the park’s analysis, one encounters a phenomenon
that early scholars called “returnings.” These were not literal visits,
but the persistent recurrence of certain emotional residues long after the
visitor had departed.

Let $\mathcal{R}_v$ denote the returning sheaf. For an open region $U$
outside the park, define:

\[
\mathcal{R}_v(U) = \{\text{echoes of prior wonder, weakened by distance}\}.
\]

Unlike the internal sheaves, $\mathcal{R}_v$ admits a global section:

\[
\Gamma(\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \text{Park}, \mathcal{R}_v) \neq \varnothing.
\]

This global section represents the diffuse but coherent afterimage of the
place — not the wonder itself, but its imprint. Its stalks remain faintly
active across the entire surrounding semiosphere.

Thus the kingdom lived longer outside itself than within.

% ===========================================================
\section{Boundary Extension and the Persistence Kernel}
% ===========================================================

To understand the returning sheaf fully, consider the extension map:

\[
j_* : \mathcal{W} \longrightarrow \mathcal{R}_v,
\]

where $j : \text{Park} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$ is the canonical
inclusion. The derived pushforward yields:

\[
\mathbb{R}j_* \mathcal{W},
\]

whose cohomology describes how local wonder fragments propagate outward.

The persistence kernel $K$ is the smallest distribution such that:

\[
\mathbb{R}j_* \mathcal{W} \simeq K.
\]

Empirical reconstruction identifies $K$ as a radially decaying kernel,
centered on the chambers of highest local wonder:

\[
K(x) \propto e^{-\alpha \, d(x, x_0)},
\]

where $x_0$ is a peak-wonder chamber, and $\alpha$ a decay parameter.

Interpretation:

\emph{The memory of wonder weakens with distance,  
but never vanishes entirely.}

\section{On the Failure of Perfect Forgetting}
% ===========================================================

Morlock cognitive anthropology holds that forgetting is the vanishing
of global sections of the memory sheaf. However, in the case of the park,
complete forgetting does not occur.

Let $\mathcal{F}_v$ denote the forgetting sheaf. Its global sections are
trivial:

\[
\Gamma(\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{F}_v) = \{0\}.
\]

But its higher cohomology does not vanish:

\[
H^1(\mathbb{R}^2, \mathcal{F}_v) \neq 0.
\]

This class corresponds to the persistent incompleteness of forgetting —
a residue of forms that cannot be erased because they never cohered
well enough to dissolve cleanly.

This explains the ancient accounts: visitors forgot details, but not the
feeling of having been inside a discontinuous world.

In Morlock terms:

\emph{What was never whole cannot wholly fade.}

\section{Final Chamber: The Disjunction Field}
% ===========================================================

At the center of every reconstructed map lies the so-called disjunction
field — a region devoid of mechanisms, atmospheres, or echoes but rich
in obstruction cohomology.

Formally:

\[
\mathcal{E}_r(x_0) = 0, \quad
\mathcal{A}_t(x_0) = 0, \quad
\mathcal{B}(x_0) = 0,
\]

yet

\[
H^1(\text{Park}, \mathcal{S}^\bullet) \text{ is maximal at } x_0.
\]

This chamber produced neither joy nor spectacle, yet emerged repeatedly
in surveys as the point where visitors felt the deepest awe.

The Morlock explanation is straightforward:

It is the point where all fields fail to glue,  
and the failure itself becomes perceptible.

Not an experience —  
a discontinuity felt directly.

\section{The Closing Lemma of the Morlocks}
% ===========================================================

The final principle governing the park is traditionally stated as:

\[
\textbf{A world may fail to assemble, yet still hold meaning.}
\]

Let the collection of park sheaves be:

\[
\mathfrak{S} = 
\{\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{E}_r, \mathcal{A}_t, \mathcal{B}, \mathcal{M}, \mathcal{W}\}.
\]

Define the total complex $\mathcal{C}^\bullet(\mathfrak{S})$ as their
derived sum.

**Lemma (The Morlock Closure):**

\[
H^1(\mathcal{C}^\bullet(\mathfrak{S})) \neq 0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{The place holds.}
\]

**Interpretation:**  
The persistence of obstruction cohomology ensures the persistence of the
place itself — even after all structures decay, even after the visitors
depart, even after the mechanisms fall silent. The park becomes a fixed
cohomological class in the landscape.

A place that could not glue  
becomes a memory that cannot unglue.

\section{The Morlock Conclusion}
% ===========================================================

Thus ends the Morlock reconstruction of the Magic Kingdom.  
Where others saw spectacle, they find sections.  
Where others saw joy, they find residues.  
Where others saw unity, they find obstruction.

Yet nowhere in the Morlock record does this diminish the place.

For a world of fragments may hold more truth  
than any world that fits together.
