\title{Gradient-Regularized Scalar Fluid with Finite Spinodal Interval:\ Linear Stability and Scale-Dependent Structure Growth}

\begin{abstract}

We construct and analyze a conservative relativistic scalar-fluid model in which the free-energy functional of a conserved number density includes a positive gradient regulator. The regulator introduces a finite correlation length and produces a scale-dependent contribution to the pressure perturbation while preserving local energy--momentum conservation. Embedded in a flat Friedmann--Robertson--Walker background, the model yields a modified linear growth equation for density perturbations containing both $k^{2}$ and $k^{4}$ terms. We derive the resulting dispersion relation, demonstrate ultraviolet stability for positive gradient coefficient, and identify the conditions under which a finite instability band arises when the effective compressibility becomes transiently negative. The analysis remains within a conservative relativistic framework and does not assume modifications to the Einstein equations at background level. The resulting scale-dependent growth factor defines an observationally parameterizable deviation from standard pressureless matter evolution while maintaining mathematical closure and boundedness of the perturbation spectrum. \end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In standard cosmological perturbation theory, nonrelativistic matter is modeled either as a collisionless component or as a perfect fluid with negligible pressure. At linear order in scalar perturbations and on subhorizon scales, this approximation yields the familiar growth equation in which the density contrast evolves under the competition between gravitational attraction and Hubble friction. Extensions of this description are often formulated in terms of effective fluid corrections that encode small-scale physics through modified stress terms. Such approaches appear, for example, in effective treatments of large-scale structure and in continuum models where gradient stresses arise from finite correlation length effects.

The present work examines the minimal consequences of introducing a gradient-regularized free-energy functional for a conserved scalar density within an otherwise standard relativistic fluid framework. The model is conservative and local in the sense that no dissipative transport coefficients or explicit diffusion terms are introduced. The gradient term is positive definite and generates a Korteweg-type contribution to the stress--energy tensor, thereby modifying the pressure sector in a scale-dependent manner. The objective is not to replace the standard cosmological model but to analyze, in a controlled and mathematically closed setting, the linear perturbation spectrum of such a regulated scalar fluid and to determine the conditions under which a bounded instability band may arise.

We construct a conservative relativistic scalar-fluid model with a gradient regulator, embed it in a flat Friedmann--Robertson--Walker spacetime, and derive the linearized perturbation equations in longitudinal gauge. The resulting growth equation contains an additional $k^{4}$ term originating from the gradient stress. We demonstrate that the regulator guarantees ultraviolet stability and show that if the effective sound speed squared becomes negative over a finite interval in scale factor, the model exhibits a finite spinodal window with a calculable fastest-growing mode. The associated scale-dependent growth factor defines a concrete and parameterizable modification to linear structure growth.

\section{Conservative Scalar Fluid with Gradient Regularization}

\subsection{Conserved Number Current}

We consider a relativistic fluid characterized by a conserved number density $n$ and four-velocity $u^{\mu}$ satisfying $u^{\mu}u_{\mu}=-1$. The conserved number current is defined by \begin{equation} J^{\mu}=n u^{\mu}, \end{equation} with conservation law \begin{equation} \nabla_{\mu}J^{\mu}=0. \end{equation} This relation determines the kinematic evolution of the density in terms of the fluid flow and imposes particle number conservation at the level of the effective theory.

\subsection{Free-Energy Functional and Gradient Regulator}

To encode finite correlation length effects, we introduce a gradient-regularized free-energy density. On spatial hypersurfaces orthogonal to $u^{\mu}$ with induced metric $\gamma_{ij}$, we define the free-energy functional \begin{equation} \mathcal{F}[n]=\int_{\Sigma_t}\sqrt{\gamma}\left[f(n)+\frac{\kappa}{2}D_i n D^i n\right] d^3x, \end{equation} where $f(n)$ is a local free-energy density, $D_i$ denotes the spatial covariant derivative compatible with $\gamma_{ij}$, and $\kappa>0$ is a constant with dimensions of length squared. The local part $f(n)$ determines the equation of state in the homogeneous limit, while the gradient term penalizes spatial inhomogeneities and introduces a finite correlation length scale $\ell$ satisfying $\ell^2\sim\kappa$.

The inclusion of the gradient term is the sole modification to the minimal fluid sector. No dissipative transport coefficients or explicit diffusion operators are introduced in the present formulation. The theory is treated as an effective description valid below a cutoff scale set by $\ell^{-1}$.

\subsection{Stress--Energy Tensor}

We adopt a Korteweg-type stress--energy tensor consistent with the gradient-regularized free-energy density. The total stress--energy tensor is written as \begin{equation} T^{\mu\nu}=(\varepsilon+p)u^{\mu}u^{\nu}+p g^{\mu\nu} -\kappa\left(\nabla^{\mu} n \nabla^{\nu} n-\frac{1}{2} g^{\mu\nu}\nabla_{\alpha}n\nabla^{\alpha}n\right), \end{equation} where $\varepsilon(n)$ and $p(n)$ are the homogeneous energy density and pressure derived from $f(n)$ in the usual thermodynamic manner. The final term represents the gradient contribution and is quadratic in derivatives of $n$.

Given the conservation of the number current and the assumed functional dependence of $\varepsilon$ and $p$ on $n$, the divergence of $T^{\mu\nu}$ vanishes, \begin{equation} \nabla_{\mu}T^{\mu\nu}=0, \end{equation} provided the equations of motion associated with the free-energy functional are satisfied. The model is therefore conservative and local at the level of the effective stress--energy tensor.

\section{Background Cosmology}

We embed the scalar fluid in a spatially flat Friedmann--Robertson--Walker spacetime with metric \begin{equation} ds^{2}=-dt^{2}+a(t)^{2} d\vec{x}^{2}. \end{equation} We assume a homogeneous background number density $\bar n(t)$ and neglect spatial gradients at zeroth order. The gradient contribution to $T^{\mu\nu}$ therefore vanishes at background level.

The conservation equation $\nabla_{\mu}J^{\mu}=0$ reduces to \begin{equation} \dot{\bar n}+3H\bar n=0, \end{equation} where $H=\dot a/a$ is the Hubble parameter. Thus $\bar n\propto a^{-3}$. If the local equation of state corresponds to nonrelativistic matter in the homogeneous limit, then $\bar\rho\propto a^{-3}$ as well. The background Friedmann equations are assumed to take their standard form, and the gradient regulator does not modify the homogeneous dynamics.

\section{Linear Scalar Perturbations}

\subsection{Metric and Fluid Variables}

We consider scalar perturbations in longitudinal gauge, \begin{equation} ds^{2}=-(1+2\Phi)dt^{2}+a(t)^{2}(1-2\Psi)d\vec{x}^{2}. \end{equation} In the absence of significant anisotropic stress at leading order, we set $\Phi=\Psi$ for the purposes of the subhorizon analysis.

We decompose the number density as $n=\bar n+\delta n$ and define the density contrast \begin{equation} \delta=\frac{\delta n}{\bar n}. \end{equation} The peculiar velocity is described by a scalar potential $v$ such that the spatial components of the velocity perturbation satisfy $u_i=a,\partial_i v$ at linear order. In Fourier space, we define $\theta_k=-k^{2}v_k$.

\subsection{Linearized Continuity and Euler Equations}

Linearizing the conservation equations in the subhorizon regime and neglecting time derivatives of the metric potentials relative to spatial gradients, we obtain \begin{equation} \dot{\delta}_k=-\frac{1}{a}\theta_k, \end{equation} \begin{equation} \dot{\theta}_k+H\theta_k=-\frac{k^{2}}{a}\Phi_k -\frac{k^{2}}{a}\frac{\delta p_k}{\bar\rho}. \end{equation} These equations retain their standard form, and all model dependence enters through the pressure perturbation $\delta p_k$.

\subsection{Constitutive Closure}

Linearizing the gradient-regularized free-energy functional about a homogeneous background yields a scale-dependent correction to the pressure perturbation. In Fourier space we write \begin{equation} \delta p_k

c_s^{2},\bar\rho,\delta_k + \frac{\kappa_{\rm eff},\bar\rho}{a^{2}},k^{2}\delta_k, \end{equation} where $c_s^{2}=(\partial p/\partial\rho){\bar n}$ is the adiabatic sound speed squared associated with the local part $f(n)$, and $\kappa{\rm eff}$ is the effective gradient coefficient in the pressure sector. The factor $a^{-2}$ accounts for the conversion between comoving and physical gradients.

\subsection{Poisson Equation}

In the matter-dominated, subhorizon, quasi-static limit, the gravitational potential satisfies \begin{equation} \frac{k^{2}}{a^{2}}\Phi_k=4\pi G\bar\rho,\delta_k. \end{equation}

\section{Modified Growth Equation}

Combining the continuity and Euler equations and eliminating $\theta_k$ yields \begin{equation} \ddot{\delta}_k+2H\dot{\delta}_k

\frac{k^{2}}{a^{2}}\Phi_k + \frac{k^{2}}{a^{2}}\frac{\delta p_k}{\bar\rho}. \end{equation} Substituting the Poisson equation and the constitutive closure gives \begin{equation} \ddot{\delta}_k+2H\dot{\delta}k + \left( \frac{c_s^{2}k^{2}}{a^{2}} + \frac{\kappa{\rm eff}k^{4}}{a^{4}}

4\pi G\bar\rho \right)\delta_k =0. \end{equation} This equation governs linear density evolution in the regulated scalar fluid. The $k^{4}$ term arises solely from the gradient regulator and vanishes in the limit $\kappa_{\rm eff}\to 0$, recovering the standard result.

\section{Linear Stability and Dispersion Structure}

To analyze the stability properties of the regulated scalar fluid, it is instructive to consider the instantaneous mode structure at fixed scale factor. Neglecting the Hubble friction term temporarily in order to inspect the local dispersion relation, we define an effective squared frequency \begin{equation} \omega^{2}(k,a)

\frac{c_s^{2}k^{2}}{a^{2}} + \frac{\kappa_{\rm eff}k^{4}}{a^{4}}

4\pi G\bar\rho. \end{equation} The ultraviolet behavior is determined by the highest power of $k$. For $\kappa_{\rm eff}>0$, the $k^{4}$ term dominates at large $k$, implying $\omega^{2}(k,a)\to +\infty$ as $k\to\infty$. The perturbation spectrum is therefore ultraviolet stable, and arbitrarily short-wavelength modes do not exhibit runaway growth. This boundedness property distinguishes the present model from fluids with negative sound speed squared in the absence of gradient regularization.

The condition for instability at a given time is $\omega^{2}(k,a)<0$. Writing the dispersion relation in terms of the physical wavenumber $k_{\rm phys}=k/a$, we obtain \begin{equation} \omega^{2}(k_{\rm phys},a)

c_s^{2}k_{\rm phys}^{2} + \kappa_{\rm eff}k_{\rm phys}^{4}

4\pi G\bar\rho. \end{equation} The instability region, if it exists, is determined by the roots of the quartic polynomial in $k_{\rm phys}^{2}$. Solving for the marginal stability condition $\omega^{2}=0$ yields \begin{equation} k_{\rm phys}^{2}

\frac{-c_s^{2}\pm\sqrt{c_s^{4}+16\pi G\bar\rho,\kappa_{\rm eff}}}{2\kappa_{\rm eff}}. \end{equation} When $c_s^{2}\ge 0$, the right-hand side is nonnegative only for sufficiently small $k_{\rm phys}$, reproducing a modified Jeans criterion. When $c_s^{2}<0$, the structure changes qualitatively. Because $\kappa_{\rm eff}>0$, the quartic polynomial opens upward, and the instability region, if present, is confined to a finite band in $k_{\rm phys}$. The gradient term therefore guarantees that any negative compressibility does not lead to arbitrarily small-scale collapse.

\section{Finite Spinodal Interval}

We now consider the case in which the effective sound speed squared becomes negative over a finite interval in scale factor. Specifically, assume that there exist $a_{\rm on}$ and $a_{\rm off}$ such that \begin{equation} c_s^{2}(a)<0 \qquad \text{for} \qquad a_{\rm on}<a<a_{\rm off}, \end{equation} while $c_s^{2}(a)\ge 0$ outside this interval. The background evolution is assumed continuous across these transitions.

Within the interval of negative compressibility, the dispersion relation admits a bounded instability band. The maximally unstable physical wavenumber is obtained by minimizing $\omega^{2}$ with respect to $k_{\rm phys}$. Differentiating and setting the derivative equal to zero yields \begin{equation} 2c_s^{2}k_{\rm phys}+4\kappa_{\rm eff}k_{\rm phys}^{3}=0. \end{equation} For $c_s^{2}<0$ and $k_{\rm phys}\neq 0$, the extremum occurs at \begin{equation} k_{\rm phys,*}^{2}

\frac{|c_s^{2}|}{2\kappa_{\rm eff}}. \end{equation} Substituting this value back into the dispersion relation shows that the most negative $\omega^{2}$ is finite and proportional to $-|c_s^{4}|/(4\kappa_{\rm eff})-4\pi G\bar\rho$. The growth rate of the fastest mode is therefore controlled by both the magnitude of the negative compressibility and the strength of the gradient regulator. The instability window is finite in both time and scale, and modes outside the band either oscillate or grow only through the usual gravitational term.

\section{Scale-Dependent Growth Factor}

To connect the stability analysis to observable structure formation, we define a scale-dependent growth factor $D(k,a)$ through \begin{equation} \delta_k(a)=D(k,a),\delta_k(a_i), \end{equation} for some initial scale factor $a_i$ prior to the onset of any spinodal interval. In the absence of gradient effects and for pressureless matter, $D(a)\propto a$ during matter domination. In the regulated model, the evolution depends explicitly on $k$ through the modified growth equation.

For wavenumbers satisfying $k\ll k_J(a)$, where $k_J(a)$ is the modified Jeans scale determined by the condition $\omega^{2}=0$, the pressure terms are negligible compared to gravity, and the growth remains approximately proportional to $a$. For wavenumbers well above the Jeans scale, the $k^{4}$ term dominates and produces either oscillatory behavior or suppressed growth depending on the sign of $c_s^{2}$. During a finite spinodal interval, modes within the instability band experience enhanced growth relative to the standard case, with the maximal enhancement occurring near $k_{\rm phys,*}$.

The matter power spectrum at linear order may be written as \begin{equation} P(k,a)=P_{\rm prim}(k),|T(k)|^{2},D^{2}(k,a), \end{equation} where $P_{\rm prim}(k)$ is the primordial spectrum and $T(k)$ is the transfer function encoding early-time evolution. In the present framework, the scale dependence of $D(k,a)$ modifies the late-time transfer function in a manner determined by the parameters $c_s^{2}(a)$ and $\kappa_{\rm eff}$. The presence of the $k^{4}$ term produces a high-wavenumber suppression that differs parametrically from that of collisionless or warm dark matter models.

\section{Parameter Interpretation and Regime of Validity}

The gradient coefficient $\kappa_{\rm eff}$ has dimensions of length squared and may be interpreted as the square of a correlation length $\ell$ characterizing the coarse-grained fluid description, \begin{equation} \ell^{2}\sim \kappa_{\rm eff}. \end{equation} The effective theory is expected to remain valid only for physical wavenumbers $k_{\rm phys}\lesssim \ell^{-1}$. At shorter scales, additional microphysical degrees of freedom not captured by the scalar fluid may become relevant.

The function $c_s^{2}(a)$ encodes the effective compressibility of the homogeneous sector. A transient negative value may arise in effective descriptions of phase transitions or metastable regimes. The present analysis does not assume a specific microphysical origin but treats $c_s^{2}(a)$ as a phenomenological function subject to observational constraints.

\section{Conclusion}

We have constructed a conservative relativistic scalar-fluid model in which a positive gradient regulator modifies the linear perturbation spectrum without altering the homogeneous cosmological background. The resulting growth equation contains an additional $k^{4}$ term that guarantees ultraviolet stability and introduces scale dependence into linear structure growth. When the effective sound speed squared becomes negative over a finite interval in scale factor, the model exhibits a bounded instability band with a calculable fastest-growing mode.

The framework remains mathematically closed and conservative, with all modifications arising from a regulated free-energy functional for a conserved scalar density. The scale-dependent growth factor derived here defines a concrete observational wedge that may be constrained through measurements of the matter power spectrum. Further work may investigate nonlinear evolution and possible microphysical realizations of the effective compressibility, but the present analysis establishes the linear stability structure and perturbative consequences of the gradient-regularized scalar fluid sector.
