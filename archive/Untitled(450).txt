A Thermodynamically-Aware Resource Theory}

\author{Flyxion}

\begin{abstract}
Cymatic Yogurt Computing (CYC) is a speculative but mathematically concrete
bio-stochastic computing paradigm in which vibrated microbial cultures serve
as computational media. Patterns on yogurt cultures, shaped by cymatic
excitation and transferred through sieving operations, embody information
processing under explicit thermodynamic constraints. In this essay we
develop a categorical and resource-theoretic formalism for CYC. We define a
category of culture states, equip it with a symmetric monoidal and
comonoidal structure, introduce entropy and Fisher information as
functorial scalar invariants, and show how closed monoidal structure
enables higher-order ``control cultures'' that implement learning-like
adaptation. The resulting framework clarifies how bio-stochastic substrates
can be rigorously modeled as thermodynamically-aware resource theories and
connects naturally to broader work on scalar--vector field theories and
semantic infrastructure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: The Need for Categorical Rigor in Bio-Stochastic Systems}

\subsection{The computational crisis and the digital bottleneck}

Classical computing is dominated by the von Neumann architecture: a clean
separation between memory, processing, and control, coordinated through a
global clock and a narrow instruction pipeline. This architecture has been
astonishingly successful, but it carries structural liabilities that become
acute for highly parallel, noisy, and energy-constrained regimes.

First, the \emph{digital bottleneck}: even if the underlying hardware
contains vast numbers of degrees of freedom (transistors, photonic modes,
molecular states), the usable interface is compressed into a serial stream
of discrete instructions. Moving information across memory hierarchies and
interconnects often costs more energy and time than the logical operations
themselves. Second, the von Neumann model is largely indifferent to
thermodynamic structure. Landauer's principle is acknowledged in theory but
rarely appears as a first-class design constraint; energy is something to
minimize, not a quantity to be structurally modeled.

These limitations become visible in neuromorphic hardware, synthetic
biology, and other emerging domains where computation is massively parallel,
inherently stochastic, and embedded in non-equilibrium thermodynamic
flows. In such contexts, the usual abstractions---bit-level logic, Turing
machines, RAM models---are not so much wrong as insufficiently structured:
they do not track which transformations are physically cheap, which require
irreversible mixing, and which depend on fine-grained spatial coherence.

\subsection{Introducing Cymatic Yogurt Computing (CYC)}

Cymatic Yogurt Computing (CYC) pushes this situation to an intentionally
extreme and playful limit. Consider a shallow tray of yogurt seeded with
microbial cultures and subjected to controlled vibrational patterns. The
resulting standing-wave fields, diffusion gradients, and microbial
responses produce rich spatial patterns: nodal lines, clustering domains,
and traveling waves. These patterns are treated as \emph{computational
states}. 

Computation proceeds in three coupled steps:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cymatic pattern formation:} vibrational modes and boundary
  conditions induce spatial modes in the culture, shaping density and
  metabolic fields.
  \item \textbf{Sieving and transfer:} the patterned culture is passed
  through a physical sieve or stencil, producing a filtered pattern that is
  embedded onto a fresh culture.
  \item \textbf{Entropic descent and reorganization:} the newly seeded
  culture relaxes toward thermodynamic equilibrium, generating further
  structure along the way.
\end{enumerate}
The core mechanism is thus ``computation via vibration, pattern transfer,
and entropic descent.'' The substrate is biological and stochastic; the
state space is defined over spatial domains; and the cost of computation is
unavoidably thermodynamic.

\subsection{Thesis: CYC as a thermodynamically-aware resource theory}

The central claim of this essay is that Category Theory furnishes the
appropriate structural language for CYC. We will show that:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Culture states and their transformations form a well-defined
  category \(\catC\) whose objects encode spatially distributed patterns as
  sheaves over a domain, and whose morphisms model sieve--transfer
  operations.
  \item \(\catC\) admits a symmetric monoidal structure \((\catC, \otimes,
  I)\) corresponding to parallel juxtaposition of culture bins, with
  entropy acting as a monoidal functor that tracks thermodynamic cost.
  \item Duplication and erasure operations endow objects with comonoid
  structure, making CYC a \emph{resource theory}: objects are resources,
  morphisms are allowed processes, and comonoid axioms express the laws of
  resource budgeting under entropic constraints.
  \item A closed monoidal upgrade equips \(\catC\) with internal homs
  \([C,D]\) that represent ``higher-order yogurt functions,'' or control
  cultures that implement morphisms between other cultures. This supplies a
  structural basis for learning and adaptation in CYC.
\end{itemize}
In short, CYC becomes a thermodynamically-aware resource theory whose
categorical structure is rich enough to support higher-order computation,
yet grounded in physical operations on microbial media.

\section{Objects and Foundations: The Category of Culture States \texorpdfstring{\(\catC\)}{C}}

\subsection{Culture states as computational sheaves}

We begin by specifying the objects of our category.

\begin{definition}[Culture state]
A \emph{culture state} \(C\) is a pair \(C = (X,\mathcal{F})\) where
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(X \subset \mathbb{R}^2\) is a compact spatial domain representing
  the footprint of the culture tray;
  \item \(\mathcal{F} \in \Sh(X)\) is a \emph{pattern sheaf} assigning to
  each open set \(U \subseteq X\) a space \(\mathcal{F}(U)\) of admissible
  local configurations (e.g.\ microbial density, nutrient and metabolite
  concentrations), together with restriction maps satisfying the usual
  sheaf axioms.
\end{itemize}
We write \(\mathrm{Ob}(\catC)\) for the class of all such culture states.
\end{definition}

The sheaf \(\mathcal{F}\) encodes local-to-global consistency: patterns
overlapping in space must agree on intersections. This is crucial for
modeling diffusion, reaction--diffusion dynamics, and morphogenetic
constraints. Intuitively, \(\mathcal{F}\) describes the \emph{computable
pattern space} over the culture domain.

\subsection{Entropy and Fisher information as scalar functionals}

CYC is explicitly thermodynamic, so each culture state carries global
entropy and information measures.

\begin{definition}[Entropy functional]
Let \(C = (X,\mathcal{F})\) be a culture state, and let
\(\sigma_C : X \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}\) denote a local entropy density
associated with a particular realization of the sheaf data. The
\emph{thermodynamic entropy} of \(C\) is
\begin{equation}
  S(C) \;=\; \int_X \sigma_C(\mathbf{x}) \, \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

In parallel, we introduce a dual measure of structure.

\begin{definition}[Fisher information]
Given a parametric family of local state distributions
\(\{p_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x})\}\) over \(\mathcal{F}(\{\mathbf{x}\})\),
we define the \emph{local Fisher information} as
\begin{equation}
  I_C(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[
    \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x})
    \right)^2
  \right],
\end{equation}
and the global Fisher information as
\begin{equation}
  I(C) \;=\; \int_X I_C(\mathbf{x}) \, \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Heuristically, \(S(C)\) quantifies thermodynamic ``spread'' while
\(I(C)\) quantifies structural precision. CYC computation can be viewed as
a structured compromise: patterns evolve under operations that, on
average, increase \(S\) while selectively concentrating \(I\) into
computationally meaningful modes.

\section{Morphisms: The Core Sieving Step}

\subsection{Sieve transfer as a composite morphism}

Morphisms in \(\catC\) represent physically admissible operations on
culture states. The core step in CYC is the \emph{sieve transfer}:
patterned yogurt is forced through a stencil onto a fresh culture.

\begin{definition}[Sieve transfer]
Given culture states \(C_s = (X_s,\mathcal{F}_s)\) and
\(C_t = (X_t,\mathcal{F}_t)\), a \emph{sieve transfer} is a morphism
\(f : C_s \to C_t\) of the form
\begin{equation}
  f \;=\; \psi \circ \phi,
\end{equation}
where \(\phi\) is a filtration operator on the source pattern, and \(\psi\)
is a propagation operator embedding the filtered pattern into the target
culture.
\end{definition}

We now specify these components more explicitly.

\subsection{Filtration: coarse-graining as semantic compression}

The filtration operator \(\phi\) models the effect of the physical sieve or
stencil that selectively transmits features of the source pattern.

\begin{definition}[Filtration operator]
Let \(K : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}\) be a \emph{sieve kernel}
encoding the geometry and pore structure of the sieve (or more general
non-linear weight). A filtration operator
\(\phi : C_s \to C_s'\) acts at the level of sections by a non-linear
convolution
\begin{equation}
  \phi(\mathcal{F}_s)(\mathbf{x}) 
  \;=\; \Phi\!\left(
    \int_{X_s} K(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}) \, \mathcal{F}_s(\mathbf{y}) \,
    \mathrm{d}\mathbf{y}
  \right),
\end{equation}
for a suitable non-linearity \(\Phi\) reflecting thresholding,
saturation, or other biological responses.
\end{definition}

Physically, \(\phi\) implements semantic compression: fine-grained
variations below the sieve scale are suppressed; only features that
couple strongly to \(K\) survive. From the perspective of information
theory, \(\phi\) is typically non-invertible and strictly reduces
Fisher information:
\begin{equation}
  I(C_s') \;\le\; I(C_s),
\end{equation}
with strict inequality in generic cases.

\subsection{Propagation: stochastic embedding and entropy gradients}

Once filtered, the pattern is projected onto a fresh culture.

\begin{definition}[Propagation operator]
Let \(C_s' = (X_s,\mathcal{F}_s')\) be the filtered source and
\(C_t = (X_t,\mathcal{F}_t)\) a fresh target. A \emph{propagation operator}
\(\psi : C_s' \to C_t\) is a stochastic monomorphism determined by a
transition kernel
\begin{equation}
  P(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) : X_s \times X_t \to [0,1],
\end{equation}
such that sections of \(\mathcal{F}_t\) are drawn from
\(\mathcal{F}_s'\) according to \(P\). Formally, for suitable test
functionals \(G\),
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[G(\mathcal{F}_t)\right]
  \;=\; \int_{X_t}\!\!\int_{X_s}
  G\!\left(\mathcal{F}_s'(\mathbf{x})\right)
  P(\mathbf{z}\mid \mathbf{x}) \,
  \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\,\mathrm{d}\mathbf{z}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The act of landing the filtered culture onto the fresh medium typically
introduces an immediate local entropy gradient \(\nabla S\) on \(X_t\),
driving further entropic descent and pattern formation. In this sense,
\(\psi\) is both a structural embedding and an entropic kick, aligning
with the idea that computation in CYC is always embedded in a
thermodynamic story.

\subsection{The identity morphism as a zero-work baseline}

The identity morphism in \(\catC\) corresponds to a vanishingly idealized
sieve transfer that performs no net work on the pattern.

\begin{definition}[Identity morphism]
For a culture state \(C = (X,\mathcal{F})\), the identity morphism
\(\id_C : C \to C\) is realized as the limit in which
\begin{itemize}
  \item the filtration kernel \(K\) approaches a Dirac delta
  (\(K \to \delta\)), so that \(\phi\) performs no coarse-graining; and
  \item the propagation kernel \(P(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})\) becomes
  a deterministic identity map with zero stochastic variance and no net
  entropy gradient (\(\nabla S = 0\)).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Physically this limit is unattainable: any real sieve introduces both
irreversible loss of information and some thermodynamic cost. However,
\(\id_C\) serves as a conceptual baseline. The ``distance'' between a
real sieve transfer \(f\) and \(\id_C\) (e.g.\ in terms of changes in
\(S\) and \(I\)) quantifies the entropic and informational work done by
\(f\).

\section{Monoidal Structure: Parallelism and Resource Management}

\subsection{The monoidal product as parallel juxtaposition}

We now introduce parallel composition.

\begin{definition}[Monoidal product]
Given culture states \(C_1 = (X_1,\mathcal{F}_1)\) and
\(C_2 = (X_2,\mathcal{F}_2)\) with disjoint domains, their \emph{monoidal
product} is
\begin{equation}
  C_1 \otimes C_2
  \;=\; \bigl(X_1 \sqcup X_2,\; \mathcal{F}_1 \sqcup \mathcal{F}_2\bigr),
\end{equation}
where \(\sqcup\) denotes disjoint union of spaces and sheaves.
\end{definition}

Morphisms tensor similarly: given \(f_i : C_i \to D_i\), the monoidal
product \(f_1 \otimes f_2 : C_1 \otimes C_2 \to D_1 \otimes D_2\) acts
componentwise. The associativity and symmetry constraints are provided by
the canonical homeomorphisms between iterated disjoint unions, making
\((\catC,\otimes, I)\) a symmetric monoidal category once the unit
object \(I\) is specified.

\subsection{Entropy additivity for parallel cultures}

Because the domains are disjoint, entropy behaves additively under the
monoidal product.

\begin{proposition}[Additivity of entropy]
Let \(C_1 = (X_1,\mathcal{F}_1)\) and \(C_2 = (X_2,\mathcal{F}_2)\) be
culture states with disjoint domains. Then
\begin{equation}
  S(C_1 \otimes C_2) \;=\; S(C_1) + S(C_2).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\begin{equation}
  S(C_1 \otimes C_2)
  \;=\; \int_{X_1 \sqcup X_2} \sigma(\mathbf{x}) \, \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}
  \;=\; \int_{X_1} \sigma_1(\mathbf{x})\,\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}
        + \int_{X_2} \sigma_2(\mathbf{x})\,\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x},
\end{equation}
which is exactly \(S(C_1) + S(C_2)\).
\end{proof}

This strict additivity has two important implications. First, parallelism
incurs no ``mysterious'' entropic overhead purely by juxtaposing culture
bins: the cost of running \(n\) computations in parallel is \(n\) times
the cost of running one, at least in the idealized model where bins do
not interact. Second, it justifies modeling CYC as a resource theory in
which resources combine under a strictly monoidal law for entropy.

\subsection{The unit object as waste sink and null culture}

We now introduce the neutral element for \(\otimes\).

\begin{definition}[Unit object]
The unit object \(I\) is either
\begin{itemize}
  \item the empty culture \((\emptyset,\mathcal{F}_\emptyset)\), or
  \item a maximally homogenized culture \(C_{\mathrm{max}}\) in which all
  spatial gradients have relaxed and the pattern sheaf carries only
  uniform sections.
\end{itemize}
In both cases, \(I\) satisfies
\begin{equation}
  C \otimes I \;\cong\; C 
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  I \otimes C \;\cong\; C
\end{equation}
for all \(C\).
\end{definition}

Operationally, it is convenient to treat \(I\) as a \emph{waste sink}:
a reservoir into which structured patterns can be discarded and
homogenized.

\subsection{Comonoid structure: duplication and erasure}

To complete the resource-theoretic picture, we endow each culture state
with a comonoid structure that models duplication and erasure.

\begin{definition}[Comonoid on a culture state]
A culture state \(C\) is a \emph{comonoid} in \((\catC,\otimes,I)\) if it
is equipped with morphisms
\begin{equation}
  \Delta : C \to C \otimes C
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \epsilon : C \to I
\end{equation}
satisfying the usual coassociativity and counitality axioms:
\begin{align}
  (\Delta \otimes \id_C)\circ \Delta
  &= (\id_C \otimes \Delta)\circ \Delta, \\
  (\epsilon \otimes \id_C)\circ \Delta
  &= \id_C
  = (\id_C \otimes \epsilon)\circ \Delta.
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\subsubsection*{Duplication via physical splitting}

The comultiplication \(\Delta : C \to C \otimes C\) models splitting a
single culture into two boxes and propagating the pattern into both
halves. Physically, this might involve pouring a patterned culture into
two receiving trays and allowing diffusion and growth to fill each.

The key thermodynamic insight is that duplication is not free. Even if
the pattern is replicated with high fidelity, mixing and re-equilibration
in each half generate additional entropy.

\begin{theorem}[Thermodynamic cost of duplication]
Let \(C\) be a culture state with entropy \(S(C)\). Then the duplicated
state \(\Delta(C)\) satisfies
\begin{equation}
  S(\Delta(C)) \;=\; 2S(C) + \Delta S_{\mathrm{mix}},
\end{equation}
where \(\Delta S_{\mathrm{mix}} \ge 0\) is a mixing entropy term arising
from the reorganization of the medium during splitting and propagation.
\end{theorem}

\noindent
In particular, \(\Delta S_{\mathrm{mix}}>0\) in generic cases, so
duplication is thermodynamically irreversible. This mirrors no-cloning
constraints in other physical theories: perfect duplication without
entropy increase is forbidden.

\subsubsection*{Erasure as homogenization or autoclaving}

The counit \(\epsilon : C \to I\) represents erasure or consumption of a
culture state.

\begin{definition}[Counit as erasure]
The \emph{counit} \(\epsilon : C \to I\) is realized physically by
operations such as
\begin{itemize}
  \item homogenization: vigorous stirring or mixing that eliminates all
  spatial gradients and pattern structure; or
  \item autoclaving/thermal shock: driving the culture to a thermodynamic
  fixed point in which microbial life ceases and only uniform disorder
  remains.
\end{itemize}
At the level of sheaves, \(\epsilon\) collapses all sections of
\(\mathcal{F}\) to the unique section of \(\mathcal{F}_\emptyset\),
effectively forgetting all pattern information.
\end{definition}

Erasure is, by construction, a maximally irreversible operation. The
associated entropy production approximates the Landauer cost of
destroying information:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{dissipated}}
  \;\approx\; S_{\mathrm{max}} - S(C),
\end{equation}
where \(S_{\mathrm{max}}\) is the entropy of the fully homogenized state.

Counitality ensures that duplication followed by erasure of one copy
restores the original resource (up to approximations set by
\(\Delta S_{\mathrm{mix}}\)): splitting a culture into two and immediately
destroying one half leaves the other half essentially unchanged at the
level of computational state.

\section{Higher-Order Computation: Closed Monoidal Structure}

\subsection{Why internal homs matter}

So far, \(\catC\) can express sequences and parallel combinations of
sieve transfers. To capture \emph{higher-order} behavior---cultures that
encode and modify other cultures' dynamics---we require an internal
representation of morphisms. This is precisely what a closed monoidal
structure provides.

\begin{definition}[Closed monoidal category]
A symmetric monoidal category \((\catC,\otimes,I)\) is
\emph{(right) closed} if for every pair of objects \(C,D\) there exists an
object \([C,D]\) (the \emph{internal hom}) and a natural isomorphism
\begin{equation}
  \Hom(A \otimes C, D) \;\cong\; \Hom\bigl(A, [C,D]\bigr)
\end{equation}
that is natural in \(A\).
\end{definition}

In CYC terms, \([C,D]\) should be a \emph{control culture} whose internal
pattern encodes an entire family of sieve transfers from \(C\) to \(D\).

\subsection{Internal hom as control culture}

We now propose an interpretation of \([C,D]\) for CYC.

\begin{definition}[Control culture]
Given culture states \(C_1 = (X_1,\mathcal{F}_1)\) and
\(C_2 = (X_2,\mathcal{F}_2)\), a \emph{control culture}
\([C_1,C_2]\) is a culture state \((X_{\mathrm{ctrl}},\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{ctrl}})\)
equipped with a sheaf morphism
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{ctrl}} \;\to\;
  \underline{\Hom}(\mathcal{F}_1,\mathcal{F}_2),
\end{equation}
where \(\underline{\Hom}(\mathcal{F}_1,\mathcal{F}_2)\) is the
\emph{Hom-sheaf} assigning to each open set \(U\subseteq X_1\) the set of
sheaf morphisms \(\mathcal{F}_1|_U \to \mathcal{F}_2|_{\phi(U)}\) for
suitable embeddings \(\phi\).
\end{definition}

Physically, the control culture consists of specialized regulatory
bacteria and chemical gradients. When brought into interaction with an
input culture \(C_1\), the combined system
\[
  C_1 \otimes [C_1,C_2]
\]
relaxes, through the usual CYC dynamics, into a pattern that matches the
desired output state in \(C_2\). In other words, the control culture
biases the filtration and propagation operators toward a specific family
of morphisms \(C_1 \to C_2\).

\subsection{Currying, uncurrying, and learning in CYC}

The closed structure asserts that giving a morphism
\[
  f : A \otimes C \to D
\]
is equivalent to giving a morphism
\[
  \tilde{f} : A \to [C,D].
\]
In the CYC picture, the first is a direct, ``hard-wired'' sieve pipeline
that takes an auxiliary culture \(A\) and an input culture \(C\) and
produces an output \(D\). The second is a process by which the auxiliary
culture \(A\)---perhaps a training dataset, environment, or ``curriculum
culture''---shapes the control culture \([C,D]\) itself.

This is precisely the categorical shape of learning:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Uncurried view:} apply a fixed control culture to many
  inputs in parallel to compute outputs.
  \item \emph{Curried view:} treat observed input--output behavior as a
  morphism from an environment \(A\) into the space of possible control
  cultures.
\end{itemize}
In more concrete terms, a control culture could be evolved by repeatedly
subjecting it to a training protocol: co-culturing with \(C\), applying
sieves and vibrational patterns, and selecting for those descendants that
move the joint system toward the desired state in \(D\). Over time, this
implements a physical approximation to a map \(A \to [C,D]\).

\section{Conclusion: CYC as a Thermodynamically-Aware Resource Theory}

\subsection{Synthesis of the categorical structure}

We can now summarize the categorical formalism of CYC:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The category \(\catC\) of culture states has objects
  \(C=(X,\mathcal{F})\) representing spatially distributed microbial
  patterns, and morphisms \(f:C_s \to C_t\) representing sieve transfers
  composed from filtration and propagation operators.
  \item Entropy \(S(C)\) and Fisher information \(I(C)\) are scalar
  functionals on objects that quantify thermodynamic spread and structural
  precision, respectively.
  \item A symmetric monoidal structure \((\catC,\otimes,I)\) models
  parallel juxtaposition of cultures, with entropy strictly additive under
  \(\otimes\), ensuring that parallel computation is transparently
  accounted for in thermodynamic terms.
  \item Comonoid structure \((\Delta,\epsilon)\) on objects captures
  duplication (with irreducible mixing entropy) and erasure (with
  Landauer-like dissipation), making CYC a resource theory in which
  objects are computational resources and morphisms are allowed physical
  processes.
  \item A closed monoidal upgrade introduces internal homs \([C,D]\) that
  represent control cultures encoding families of morphisms \(C \to D\),
  supplying a natural setting for higher-order computation and learning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Positioning within a broader theoretical ecosystem}

While Cymatic Yogurt Computing is deliberately whimsical in its choice of
substrate, the formal structure developed here is fully general. Any
computational medium with the following properties
\begin{enumerate}
  \item spatially extended states modeled by sheaves or related
  local-to-global data structures,
  \item inherently stochastic and thermodynamically constrained
  transformations, and
  \item physically meaningful duplication and erasure operations
\end{enumerate}
can, in principle, be organized into a similar thermodynamically-aware
resource theory.

In this sense, CYC can be seen as a toy model of more ambitious frameworks
in which scalar--vector field theories, semantic infrastructures, and
entropic alignment mechanisms are unified. The category \(\catC\) is a
local caricature of much larger plenum-like systems where information and
energy flow through structured, self-organizing media.

\subsection{Future directions}

Several extensions suggest themselves:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Enrichment over stochastic kernels.} One can refine the
  formalism by treating \(\catC\) as enriched over a category of stochastic
  matrices or Markov kernels, so that morphisms carry explicit probability
  structures and compositional noise models.
  \item \textbf{Compact closure and feedback.} Introducing duals and
  exploring compact closed or traced monoidal structure would allow
  feedback loops and cyclic sieve pipelines to be modeled explicitly,
  capturing the dynamics of resonant or recurrent CYC setups.
  \item \textbf{Higher sheaf-theoretic structure.} Replacing simple
  sheaves with more sophisticated objects (e.g.\ cosheaves or factorization
  algebras) could provide richer handles on locality, causality, and
  multi-scale organization in bio-stochastic computation.
  \item \textbf{Experimental toy models.} Finally, building small physical
  prototypes---using dye patterns, colloids, or simple microbial cultures
  under vibration and sieving---would test which parts of the categorical
  story survive contact with experimental reality.
\end{itemize}

Cymatic Yogurt Computing thus plays a dual role: it is both a playful
thought experiment and a serious probe into what it means to treat
computation as an explicitly thermodynamic, spatially extended, and
categorically organized process. In situating CYC as a
thermodynamically-aware resource theory, we obtain a template for future
work on non-digital architectures---from yogurt cultures to cosmological
plenums---that compute by falling, mixing, and reorganizing in the
direction of entropy.
