The Flutter of Fans

By Blanche McManus
(The Telegraph-Courier, Kenosha, Wisconsin, November 22, 1923)

Have you ever spread out a hand of cards and noticed how it looks like a fan? Or opened a fan and realized you’re holding something that resembles a deck of cards? In fact, the two are built the same way—structured around a single pivot, meant to be fanned out in the hand.

But while a deck of cards is for games, the fan—especially in Spain—is an instrument of art, emotion, and secret communication.

In Spanish culture, fans speak. Literally. A well-trained Spanish woman—or man—can use a fan to express love, hate, joy, sorrow, and fear, without saying a word. The fan becomes a kind of aerial shorthand, more sophisticated than anything in a typing pool.


A Weapon of Coquetry and Code

The art of fan signaling is ancient. It began in the East, with the Chinese claiming to have invented it. Legend tells of a mandarin’s daughter, Kan-si, who rebelled against the tradition of wearing a public mask. On a sweltering festival night, she cleverly fluttered her mask in front of her face to both cool herself and protect her modesty. Thus, the fan was born—from a rebellious breeze.

From rigid ovals to half-moons to folding fans, the fan evolved across Asia. In the 15th century, Korean inventors created the collapsible fan. Chinese artisans spread it through trade routes, and eventually it landed in the hands of passionate, intrigue-loving Spaniards.

Spain transformed the fan from a simple cooling device into an elaborate secret language—a kind of codebook of gestures and taps, filled with flirtation and espionage.


Spain’s Secret Code of the Fan

Long before Morse code, Spaniards developed a tapping language using fans. The left hand, palm-up, became a kind of “keyboard” where each finger joint stood for a capital letter. The right hand mapped lowercase letters, and the fan itself served as a pointer and signal tool. When tapped in specific places, it could spell out entire messages—names, times, locations, emotions.

Messages were often shared in social settings where conversation was forbidden: the opera, the bullring, or across balconies. A lady could schedule a meeting, pass on secret information, or deliver a romantic response—all by fluttering her fan.

Example: A senorita wants to meet her admirer at San Sebastián Church at 3:30 on Friday. She taps the church name in capital letters on her left hand, the time by opening the fan to 30 ribs and laying it on the left, then half-open and placed on the right to signal “and a half.” If Aunt Pilar, her near-sighted chaperone, will be coming, the fan in the right hand (lowercase) will say so—implying just how much privacy can be expected.


Fans for Every Occasion

In Spain, everyone has a fan:

Senoritas use large fans for flirtation.

Senoras carry medium-sized ones.

Senors tuck small fans in their sleeves or vests.


Fans vary by season and occasion—weddings, funerals, walks, city or country outings, or bullfights (with fans decorated with favorite matadors and ring scenes). The sound of fluttering fans in a plaza is compared to birds in flight—a perfect metaphor, since the fan, like a bird’s wing, is designed to lift and move through the air.


The Art and Etiquette of Fan Communication

Beyond the alphabetic code, fans are used for gestures with specific meanings:

Running a finger over closed ribs: “I want to speak to you.”

Holding the fan to the lips: “I don’t trust you.”

Dropping the fan: “Leave me.”


These gestures, and their meanings, vary slightly from one Spanish region to another—Seville, Toledo, Madrid, and Córdoba all have their own versions—but the basic “fan wireless” language is universal.
