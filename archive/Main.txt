\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Across psychology, neuroscience, cybernetics, control theory, and contemporary theories of cognition, one finds a recurring structural motif: adaptive agents maintain themselves by keeping their \emph{perceptions} within an acceptable range of \emph{internally specified normative models}. This architecture emerges independently in William Powers’ Perceptual Control Theory (PCT), William Glasser’s Choice Theory, Karl Friston’s Free Energy Principle (FEP) with active inference, and—more implicitly—in computational theories of narrative-driven or simulated agency based on sparse semantic projection. Each tradition arrives at the same canonical insight: behaviour is not the control of action, but the control of perception through the minimisation of a discrepancy functional.

The convergence is historically surprising. Glasser and Powers derived their theories from clinical observation and cybernetic reasoning long before Bayesian brain theories were articulated. Friston’s work arises from variational calculus and thermodynamics, supported by deep hierarchical generative models. Sparse semantic projection emerges from representational compression and narrative selection in high-dimensional cognitive architectures. Yet when formalised, all three show that adaptive agents employ exactly two degrees of freedom: \begin{enumerate} \item \textbf{Act upon the environment} to make sensory evidence conform to the internal model. \item \textbf{Modify the internal model} to better accommodate sensory evidence. \end{enumerate} The central thesis of this paper is that this duality is not merely a suggestive analogy but a mathematically enforced constraint of any bounded, self-maintaining, self-evidencing system.

\subsection*{1.1 The Need for a Unified Framework}

Despite parallel insights, the literatures remain siloed: \begin{itemize} \item Clinical psychology lacks a variational or probabilistic grounding for reorganisation processes. \item Active inference lacks psychologically interpretable constructs such as \emph{quality world}, \emph{reference signals}, or \emph{total behaviour}. \item Sparse semantic projection models emphasise narrative coherence but seldom connect narrative selection to formal control dynamics. \end{itemize} A unified framework is needed not simply to compare these systems but to reveal the abstract control architecture they each instantiate. Such unification serves three goals: \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)] \item \textbf{Theoretical clarity}: Identifying the minimal mathematical structure that explains adaptive behaviour across biological, psychological, and artificial systems. \item \textbf{Rigor}: Demonstrating equivalence via explicit generative models, linear-Gaussian reductions, and categorical representations rather than intuitive metaphor. \item \textbf{Applicability}: Providing a common language for clinical intervention, computational modelling, and AI design. \end{enumerate}

\subsection*{1.2 Structure of This Paper}

This essay builds such a unified framework in five steps:

\begin{enumerate} \item \textbf{Universal Control Duality.}
We begin by formalising the abstract dual-control architecture using functional analysis. We introduce the discrepancy functional and prove that its minimisation necessarily decomposes into two gradient flows: one in action space and one in model space. This establishes the structural necessity of the duality across all adaptive systems.

\item \textbf{Choice Theory and Perceptual Control Theory.} We provide a technically rigorous treatment of PCT, including hierarchical control levels, reference-value dynamics, and mathematically explicit reorganisation. This section clarifies why PCT anticipates modern predictive frameworks. \item \textbf{Free Energy Principle and Active Inference.} FEP is introduced from first principles, beginning with variational free energy and continuing through expected free energy, epistemic vs pragmatic value decomposition, and continuous-time gradient flows. We introduce the nonequilibrium steady-state (NESS) interpretation, demonstrating how normative preferences encode implicit thermodynamic boundary conditions. \item \textbf{Formal Equivalence: PCT as a Special Case of Active Inference.} This is the mathematical core of the paper. We show via a linear-Gaussian generative model with quadratic preferences that active inference yields exactly the PCT control law. The result extends to higher levels of the PCT hierarchy under mild assumptions. We further formalise Glasserian reorganisation as prior learning in a variational hierarchy. \item \textbf{Sheaf-Theoretic, Field-Theoretic, and Sparse-Projection Extensions.} We reinterpret perception and reference values as sheaf sections, discrepancy as a natural transformation, and psychological conflict as a cohomological obstruction. We embed the architecture into RSVP scalar-vector-entropy fields, framing discrepancy as a field gradient. Finally, we show that sparse semantic projection instantiates the same duality at a narrative-compressional level. 

\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{1.3 Why This Convergence Matters}

The results presented here are not merely comparative but explanatory. They show that: \begin{itemize} \item Any system that maintains itself far from thermodynamic equilibrium must implement a dual process of action and belief updating. \item Any agent with internal normative structure must constrain perception via reference values or priors. \item Any system that compresses its world-model into a sparse, narrative-like form must enact behaviour that minimises projection error. \end{itemize} The convergence is therefore not an artifact of analogy but a reflection of deep mathematical invariants governing adaptive behaviour.

\subsection*{1.4 Contributions}

This essay makes the following contributions: \begin{enumerate} \item Proves a universal control duality theorem for adaptive agents. \item Provides the most complete derivation to date showing that PCT is a special case of active inference. \item Introduces a sheaf-theoretic semantics for reference signals and discrepancy. \item Embeds discrepancy-based control into RSVP scalar-vector-entropy fields. \item Shows how sparse narrative templates instantiate the same architecture. \item Presents a unified categorical framework for control, inference, and reorganisation. \end{enumerate}



\section{The Universal Control Duality}

Every adaptive agent—biological, cognitive, artificial, or hybrid—must maintain its internal organisation in the face of a changing environment. This requirement forces a particular form onto the control architecture. In this section we formalise this constraint and prove that any system seeking to minimise a perceptual discrepancy functional must do so through two and only two types of operations: (i) changes to the world via action, and (ii) changes to the internal normative model via inference or reorganisation. This duality is structurally inevitable, independent of representational format, implementation substrate, or physical embodiment.

We begin with a minimal formalisation of an agent–environment coupling and then derive the two corresponding gradient flows that define the duality.

\subsection*{2.1 Agent–Environment Structure}

Let the environment be modelled as a measurable state space

\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n 

Define: \begin{itemize} \item \textbf{Observation map:}

h : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{O} \item \textbf{Action map:} f : \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{X} \item \textbf{Internal normative model:} m \in \mathcal{M} 

\end{itemize}

These components suffice to state the central requirement: the agent must regulate its perceptual inputs relative to its internal model.

\subsection*{2.2 Discrepancy Functional}

Let

D : \mathcal{M} \times \mathcal{O} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0} 

Examples include: \begin{itemize} \item Quadratic error: . \item KL divergence: . \item Variational free energy: . \item Bregman divergence with potential . \end{itemize}

We do not assume any specific form; only that is differentiable almost everywhere in each argument and coercive (i.e., grows unbounded as mismatch increases), guaranteeing well-posed minimisation.

\subsection*{2.3 Control Objective}

The agent's objective is:

\min_{(a,m)} \; D\big(m, \, h(x(a))\big), 

The optimisation variable is two-dimensional:

(a, m) \in \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{M}. 

\subsection*{2.4 Formal Decomposition of the Gradient}

We now compute the directional derivatives of with respect to actions and model parameters.

\paragraph{Gradient with respect to action.}

Since action affects discrepancy only indirectly via its effect on state and thus on observations, the derivative is:

\frac{d}{da}D\big(m, h(x(a))\big) = \nabla_o D(m,o) \cdot J_h(x(a)) \cdot J_f(x(a),a), 

Define:

\mathcal{G}_a = \nabla_a D(m,o) = \nabla_o D(m,o) \, J_h \, J_f. \dot{a} = -\eta_a \mathcal{G}_a, 

\paragraph{Gradient with respect to the internal model.}

By direct differentiation:

\mathcal{G}_m = \nabla_m D(m,o). \dot{m} = -\eta_m \mathcal{G}_m. 

\paragraph{Immediate Corollary.} Since the two gradients are defined on distinct manifolds and affect through independent channels, the decomposition is mathematically forced.

\dot{a} = -\eta_a \nabla_a D(m,h(x(a))), \qquad \dot{m} = -\eta_m \nabla_m D(m,h(x(a))). 

\end{theorem}

\begin{proof} Direct from multivariate calculus: since is defined on the product manifold , its gradient decomposes additively:

\nabla D = (\nabla_a D, \nabla_m D). \partial D/\partial a \perp \partial D/\partial m. 

\end{proof}

\subsection*{2.6 Interpretive Consequences}

The theorem implies:

\begin{itemize} \item \textbf{All adaptive behaviour is control of perception.}
Action exists only to reduce mismatch between perception and model.

\item \textbf{Inference and reorganisation are not optional.} If action cannot reduce discrepancy (e.g., physical constraints, social constraints, conflicting goals), the agent must adjust its internal model. \item \textbf{Behaviour–belief duality is universal.} Every adaptive agent, regardless of complexity or implementation (biological, neural, symbolic, generative, narrative), must perform both. \item \textbf{Psychological distress corresponds to persistent non-zero discrepancy.} Chronic error indicates failure of both action-path and model-path minimisation, leading to the need for higher-order reorganisation (meta-priors, narrative refurbishment, identity shifts). \item \textbf{Active inference and PCT are structurally identical at this level.} Their differences concern representational format, not functional architecture. \item \textbf{Sparse narrative agency is forced into the same duality.} Narrative selection corresponds to model updating; enactment corresponds to action. 

\end{itemize}

Higher layers of the PCT hierarchy, as well as meta-inference in active inference, arise when the discrepancy minimisation is itself insufficient. Then higher-order model parameters regulate the space of possible first-order models . The same duality applies recursively:

\dot{m}^{(2)} \in -\nabla_{m^{(2)}} D^{(2)}(m^{(2)},m). 



\section{Choice Theory and Perceptual Control Theory}

Perceptual Control Theory (PCT), originally formulated by William T. Powers, represents one of the earliest and most precise mathematical theories of behaviour in psychology. Glasser’s Choice Theory later translated the same structure into clinically grounded terminology. The conceptual core is robust: organisms do not behave to control actions or outputs; they behave to control \emph{perceptions}. Behaviour is the process of selecting actions whose environmental consequences bring perceptual signals into alignment with internally generated reference conditions.

This section provides a formal and extended treatment of the cybernetic architecture underpinning PCT, the role of reference values and perceptual functions, the mechanics of continuous control, and Glasser’s reformulation as clinical theory. We establish the formal apparatus necessary for later equivalence with active inference.

\subsection*{3.1 Historical and Conceptual Foundations}

The roots of PCT lie in the classical cybernetics of Ashby, Wiener, McCulloch, and Rosenblueth. However, where earlier cybernetic models treated behaviour primarily as stimulus–response or as regulation of outputs, Powers’ central insight is that the regulated quantity is \emph{internal}: not the behaviour itself, but the perceptual consequences of behaviour.

This inversion produces a control architecture that anticipates the predictive-processing and active-inference paradigm by decades. It also provides an empirical and operational definition of psychological function: an agent’s successful control of perceptual variables.

Glasser’s later work in Choice Theory reframed these cybernetic mechanisms in clinical terms. His “Quality World”—a structured internal representation of desired states—maps directly onto Powers’ hierarchy of reference signals, while Glasser’s notion of “total behaviour” corresponds to the entire closed-loop control cycle.

\subsection*{3.2 Architecture of a Single Control Unit}

A PCT control unit regulates a single perceptual variable. Formally, define:

\begin{itemize} \item : reference signal (desired internal value) \item : perceptual signal (current sensed value) \item : error signal \item : output function transforming error into action \item : environmental feedback function involving disturbances \end{itemize}

Together, these equations define a closed-loop control system. The goal is not to produce a predetermined action, but to ensure:

p(t) \approx r(t) 

\subsection*{3.3 Mathematical Dynamics}

Assume differentiability of and . The dynamics are expressible as:

\dot{q}(t) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial e} \cdot \frac{\partial e}{\partial p} \cdot \frac{\partial p}{\partial q}, \dot{q}(t) = f'(e(t)) \left( -\frac{\partial p}{\partial q} \right). 

In standard control-theoretic terms, stability follows from Lyapunov analysis with candidate function:

V(e) = \frac{1}{2}e^2. \dot{V} = e \cdot \dot{e} = - e \cdot \frac{\partial p}{\partial q} \cdot f'(e). 

This provides the formal guarantee that PCT’s core mechanism indeed regulates perception.

\subsection*{3.4 Hierarchical Organisation}

Crucially, PCT is not a single-level controller but a \emph{hierarchy} of such units, each controlling perceptual variables at increasing levels of abstraction. Powers articulated seven canonical levels: \begin{enumerate} \item Intensities
\item Sensations
\item Configurations
\item Transitions
\item Events
\item Programs
\item Principles
\item System Concepts (later versions) \end{enumerate}

Formally, let:

p_{k}(t) = g_k(p_{k-1}(t)) r_{k}(t) e_k(t) = r_k(t) - p_k(t), r_{k-1}(t) = h_k(q_k(t)). 

This produces a tower of coupled differential equations, yielding a multi-scale control architecture.

\subsection*{3.5 Reference Signals and the Quality World}

Reference signals encode desired perceptual states. They may be: \begin{itemize} \item innately specified (e.g., physiological variables), \item learned through experience, \item inherited from higher-level conceptual structures, \item socially mediated or normatively informed. \end{itemize}

Glasser’s \textit{Quality World} corresponds to a collection of relatively stable high-level reference signals. We may model it as:

\mathcal{Q} = \{ r^{(k)} : k = \text{principles, system concepts} \}. 

The Quality World is therefore formally equivalent to a set of high-level priors over desired states.

\subsection*{3.6 The Role of Disturbances}

Disturbances are exogenous influences that affect independently of the agent's actions. PCT’s emphasis on disturbance rejection rather than stimulus–response correlation is central. The system is judged by its ability to counteract disturbances and preserve perceptual targets.

Given:

p(t) = h(q(t), d(t)), \frac{\partial p}{\partial d} = \text{small or cancelled}. 

\subsection*{3.7 Reorganisation: The Failure Mode of Control}

When perception cannot be made to match reference values—due to physical impossibility, conflicting goals, inadequate perceptual functions, or pathological reference settings—the agent enters a mode Powers calls \emph{reorganisation}. This is a higher-order mechanism that modifies: \begin{itemize} \item reference values , \item perceptual functions , \item gain parameters in , \item structural connectivity within the hierarchy. \end{itemize}

Formally, reorganisation is defined by a stochastic or gradient-based process:

\dot{\theta} = -\eta_\theta \nabla_\theta D(r,p), 

This is a key conceptual precursor to variational inference and hierarchical model learning in FEP.

\subsection*{3.8 Behaviour as Phenomenological Consequence}

In PCT, \emph{behaviour is a side effect}. The critical controlled variable is internal:

e(t) = r(t) - p(t). 

\begin{itemize} \item Apparent behaviour depends on the environment’s inverse dynamics.
\item Different behaviours can achieve the same perceptual target.
\item Behaviour cannot be interpreted without reference to the controlled perceptual variable. \end{itemize}

Glasser’s concept of \emph{total behaviour}—acting, thinking, feeling, and physiology—acknowledges that the entire organism participates in control. All are downstream manifestations of internal error correction.

\subsection*{3.9 Clinical Interpretation (Glasser)}

Glasser integrates PCT’s cybernetic architecture with therapeutic insights: \begin{itemize} \item Psychological suffering arises from chronic, unresolved discrepancy . \item Conflict occurs when two high-level reference signals cannot be simultaneously satisfied. \item Therapy facilitates reorganisation by helping clients adjust maladaptive reference values or develop new behavioural strategies. \end{itemize}

Formally:

\lim_{t\to \infty} |e(t)| \not\to 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \exists k \, \text{such that} \, \dot{r}_k(t) \neq 0. 

\subsection*{3.10 Summary}

PCT and Choice Theory jointly provide: \begin{itemize} \item a mathematically principled negative-feedback architecture, \item a hierarchical organisation of perceptual control, \item explicit mechanisms for reorganisation, \item an operational definition of psychological “needs” and “goals” as reference conditions, \item a clinical interpretation of chronic discrepancy. \end{itemize}

These structures set the stage for the next section, where we show that the variational mechanics of active inference reproduce these same dynamics under Bayesian assumptions.



\section{The Free Energy Principle and Active Inference}

The Free Energy Principle (FEP), due primarily to Karl Friston, is a unifying theoretical framework that describes how biological and cognitive systems resist the natural tendency toward disorder by minimising a variational upper bound on surprisal. Whereas PCT frames behaviour as the control of perception, FEP frames action and perception as forms of Bayesian model inversion: agents maintain themselves in nonequilibrium steady states by inferring and enacting the causes of their sensory impressions.

The syntactic differences between these frameworks conceal a deeper equivalence. PCT describes how agents regulate perceptual variables relative to internally specified reference values. FEP formalises those reference values as priors or preferences over sensory states. Both insist that adaptive behaviour requires two coupled processes: action on the environment and inference over internal models.

This section provides a rigorous reconstruction of FEP from first principles, introduces active inference as the dual gradient flows on actions and beliefs, and clarifies the thermodynamic foundations that motivate the framework. These results prepare the ground for the equivalence proof in Section 5.

\subsection*{4.1 Generative Models and Bayesian Agents}

Under the Bayesian brain hypothesis, an agent maintains a probabilistic generative model:

p(o, s \mid m) 

\begin{itemize} \item are observations, \item are latent states or causes of observations, \item parameterises the generative model (structure, priors, likelihood functions). \end{itemize}

The generative model factorises as:

p(o, s) = p(o \mid s) \, p(s), 

Inference corresponds to inverting the generative model to compute:

p(s \mid o) = \frac{p(o,s)}{p(o)}. 

\subsection*{4.2 Variational Inference and Free Energy}

Let be a tractable density approximating . Variational inference selects by minimising the Kullback–Leibler divergence:

\mathrm{KL}[q(s)\|p(s \mid o)]. \ln p(o) = F[q] + \mathrm{KL}[q(s)\|p(s \mid o)], F[q] = \mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(s)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(o,s)]. 

\subsection*{4.3 Interpretation: Free Energy as a Discrepancy Functional}

The free energy can be decomposed into two terms:

F = \underbrace{\mathrm{KL}[q(s)\|p(s)]}_{\text{complexity}} - \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(o\mid s)]}_{\text{accuracy}}. 

\begin{itemize} \item \textbf{Accuracy:} How well the agent's beliefs predict sensory observations. \item \textbf{Complexity:} How much the posterior deviates from prior expectations. \end{itemize} Minimising free energy therefore implements a trade-off between fidelity to sensory evidence and preservation of parsimonious internal models—a principle that can be seen as a formal analogue of Glasser’s “quality world” and Powers’ reference values.

\subsection*{4.4 Gradient Flows: Perceptual and Active Inference}

Variational and active inference arise from gradient descent on free energy:

\dot{q}(s) = - \frac{\partial F}{\partial q}, \qquad \dot{a} = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial a}. 

\paragraph{Perceptual inference (belief updating).}

For recognition density parameters :

\dot{\mu} = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial \mu}. 

\paragraph{Active inference (action updating).}

Action influences observations via a sensory mapping , so:

\frac{\partial F}{\partial a} = \frac{\partial F}{\partial o}\frac{\partial o}{\partial a}. \dot{a} = - \eta \frac{\partial F}{\partial a}, 

This is precisely the duality identified in Section 2:

\dot{a} \in -\nabla_a D(m,o), \qquad \dot{m} \in -\nabla_m D(m,o), 

\subsection*{4.5 Expected Free Energy and Policy Selection}

In temporally deep settings, agents must select policies based on predicted future free energy:

G(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{q(o_\tau, s_\tau \mid \pi)}[F]. G(\pi) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\text{ambiguity}]}_{\text{epistemic value}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\text{preference deviation}]}_{\text{pragmatic value}}. 

Key consequence:

When epistemic value is negligible (as in simple control environments), PCT’s purely pragmatic control law is recovered.

This becomes crucial for the equivalence theorem.

\subsection*{4.6 Nonequilibrium Steady States and Thermodynamic Foundations}

Biological systems exist far from equilibrium but maintain their macroscopic structure by exchanging energy with the environment. In the FEP, this is formalised through nonequilibrium steady states (NESS):

p(o,s) = \text{NESS density generated by the agent–environment coupling}. 

The preservation of these densities requires:

\partial_t p(o,s) = 0, 

\begin{itemize} \item autonomous dynamics (flows in state space), \item agent-induced dynamics (actions), \item entropy production due to fluctuations. \end{itemize}

Minimising expected free energy ensures trajectories remain within the low-surprisal set defined by the NESS distribution. This is the thermodynamic analogue of maintaining perceptual reference conditions in PCT.

\subsection*{4.7 Preferences as Priors}

In FEP, “preferences” are encoded as high prior probability on desired sensory outcomes:

p(o) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{(o - r)^2}{2\sigma_r^2}\right). 

This identification will be formalised in Section 5, where emerges naturally as a parameter minimising the variational free energy.

\subsection*{4.8 Generative Models as Perceptual Functions}

Powers described perceptual functions as mappings from lower-level perceptual variables to higher-level concepts. In FEP, this is mirrored by hierarchical generative models:

p(o, s^{(1)}, s^{(2)}, \dots, s^{(K)}). \text{Generative models} \equiv \text{hierarchical perceptual functions}. 

\subsection*{4.9 Learning the Model: Structural and Parametric Updates}

Beyond inference and action, FEP provides mechanisms for: \begin{itemize} \item \textbf{Parametric learning:} gradient descent on model parameters. \item \textbf{Structural learning:} Bayesian model selection via model evidence. \end{itemize}

PCT reorganisation mechanisms correspond to:

\dot{m} = -\eta \frac{\partial F}{\partial m}. 

\subsection*{4.10 Summary}

The FEP provides: \begin{itemize} \item a variational characterisation of perception,
\item a principled method for action selection via active inference,
\item a thermodynamic interpretation of adaptive behaviour,
\item explicit treatment of preferences as priors,
\item hierarchical generative models analogous to PCT’s perceptual hierarchy. \end{itemize}

At this level, the parallels with PCT are unmistakable. The next section demonstrates that PCT is in fact a special case of active inference under linear-Gaussian assumptions, with reference signals and preference priors playing formally identical roles.

\section{Formal Equivalence: PCT as a Special Case of Active Inference}

This section establishes the central mathematical result of the paper: under linear-Gaussian assumptions and quadratic preference functions, Perceptual Control Theory (PCT) is exactly recoverable as a special case of active inference within the Free Energy Principle (FEP). We show that the PCT control law

a_t \propto r - p_t 

This equivalence reveals that PCT is not merely compatible with FEP but is a simplified instance of it—an instantiation where epistemic value is negligible, the generative model is low-dimensional, and perception is treated as near-veridical.

\subsection*{5.1 Setup and Assumptions}

We consider a minimal environment–agent coupling with: \begin{itemize} \item a single latent environmental state , \item an observation , \item an action , \item a generative model parameterised by a reference value , \end{itemize} and assume linear-Gaussian dynamics:

s_{t+1} = A s_t + B a_t + \omega_t, \qquad \omega_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_\omega), 

o_t = C s_t + \nu_t, \qquad \nu_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_\nu). 

Preferences are encoded as:

p(o_t) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{(o_t - r)^2}{2\sigma_r^2} \right). 

The agent maintains a Gaussian recognition density:

q(s_t) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_t, \Sigma_t), 

These simplifications capture the essential structure without loss of generality for the equivalence proof.

\subsection*{5.2 Variational Free Energy for the Minimal Model}

The free energy at time is:

F_t = \mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(s_t)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(o_t, s_t)], p(o_t, s_t) = p(o_t \mid s_t)p(s_t). 

Expanding and dropping constant terms:

F_t = \frac{1}{2\sigma_\nu^2} (o_t - C\mu_t)^2 + \frac{1}{2\sigma_r^2} (o_t - r)^2 + \frac{1}{2\sigma_p^2} (\mu_t - \mu_{t|t-1})^2, 

Interpretation: \begin{itemize} \item First term: sensory prediction error. \item Second term: preference deviation (distance from reference value). \item Third term: dynamic prediction error (from the state evolution model). \end{itemize}

These three terms correspond directly to PCT constructs.

\subsection*{5.3 Perceptual Inference: Gradient Flow for }

Taking the gradient:

\frac{\partial F_t}{\partial \mu_t} = -\frac{C}{\sigma_\nu^2} (o_t - C\mu_t) + \frac{1}{\sigma_p^2}(\mu_t - \mu_{t|t-1}). 

The perceptual inference update is:

\dot{\mu}_t = \eta_\mu \left[ \frac{C}{\sigma_\nu^2}(o_t - C\mu_t) - \frac{1}{\sigma_p^2}(\mu_t - \mu_{t|t-1}) \right]. 

Interpretation: \begin{itemize} \item First term pulls beliefs toward observations. \item Second term pulls beliefs toward predictions. \end{itemize}

Under the simplifying assumption of fast perceptual inference (standard in PCT), we take:

\mu_t \approx o_t. 

This assumption is precisely the PCT assumption that perception is “near-veridical” at the controlled level.

\subsection*{5.4 Active Inference: Gradient Flow for Actions}

Action influences free energy through its effect on sensory outcomes:

\frac{\partial F_t}{\partial a_t} = \frac{\partial F_t}{\partial o_t} \cdot \frac{\partial o_t}{\partial a_t}. 

Compute:

\frac{\partial F_t}{\partial o_t} = \frac{1}{\sigma_\nu^2}(o_t - C\mu_t) + \frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r). 

Given and depending linearly on ,

\frac{\partial o_t}{\partial a_t} = C B. 

Thus:

\frac{\partial F_t}{\partial a_t} = C B \left[ \frac{1}{\sigma_\nu^2}(o_t - C\mu_t) + \frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r) \right]. 

Under fast perceptual inference (), the first term vanishes:

o_t - C\mu_t \approx 0. 

Thus:

\frac{\partial F_t}{\partial a_t} \approx \frac{CB}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r). 

The active inference update is:

\dot{a}_t = -\eta_a \frac{\partial F_t}{\partial a_t} \approx -\eta_a \frac{CB}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r). 

Rearranging:

\dot{a}_t \propto (r - o_t). 

\paragraph{This is exactly the PCT control law.}

a_t \propto (r - p_t). 

This completes the core derivation.

\subsection*{5.5 Formal Equivalence Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[PCT as a Special Case of Active Inference] Under linear-Gaussian generative models with quadratic preference potentials, and assuming fast perceptual inference, the active inference action update reduces to the PCT proportional control law:

a_t = K (r - p_t), \dot{r} = -\eta_r \frac{\partial F}{\partial r}. 

\begin{proof} Given the derivation above, we have:

\dot{a}_t = -\eta_a \frac{CB}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r) = \eta_a \frac{CB}{\sigma_r^2}(r - o_t). \dot{a}_t = K(r - p_t). \frac{\partial F}{\partial r} = -\frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r), \dot{r} = \eta_r \frac{1}{\sigma_r^2}(o_t - r) = -\eta_r (r - p_t). 

The equivalence holds modulo the assumptions stated above. \end{proof}

\subsection*{5.6 Interpretation and Significance}

\paragraph{(1) Control of perception is Bayesian inference.}

PCT’s fundamental assertion—that behaviour controls perception—emerges naturally from the action-gradient of variational free energy. Action is always selected to fulfil prior preferences encoded in the generative model.

\paragraph{(2) Reference signals are preference parameters.}

The equivalence between the PCT reference value and the FEP prior mode is exact under quadratic preferences.

\paragraph{(3) Reorganisation is meta-inference.}

Updating the Quality World corresponds to updating prior parameters of the generative model.

\paragraph{(4) Hierarchical PCT = Hierarchical generative models.}

At higher levels, PCT reference signals correspond to priors in deep hierarchical models.

\paragraph{(5) PCT is a reduced FEP regime.}

The primary assumptions required are: \begin{itemize} \item negligible epistemic value, \item nearly veridical perception at controlled levels, \item low dimensionality, \item relatively stable dynamics. \end{itemize}

This matches the typical control setting considered by Powers.

\subsection*{5.7 Summary}

We have shown: \begin{itemize} \item PCT control is equivalent to active inference with quadratic preference potentials. \item PCT reorganisation is equivalent to gradient descent on generative-model parameters. \item The PCT hierarchy corresponds to the hierarchical structure of variational generative models. \end{itemize}

This equivalence unifies two historically independent traditions and sets the stage for a categorical, sheaf-theoretic, and field-theoretic integration in the subsequent sections.



\section{Sheaf-Theoretic Reformulation}

Having established the equivalence between PCT and active inference in the previous section, we now turn to a more abstract and general representation of adaptive agency using sheaf theory. Sheaves provide a natural mathematical infrastructure for dealing with local-to-global relationships, contextual representations, and gluing conditions. They allow us to express perception, reference conditions, and discrepancy as structured collections of local data that must cohere across overlapping contexts.

This approach is especially powerful in unifying: \begin{itemize} \item local perceptual encodings, \item local normative constraints, \item action policies defined on contexts, \item PCT’s hierarchical loops, \item active inference’s variational conditioning, \item narrative- or template-based representations in simulated agency. \end{itemize}
