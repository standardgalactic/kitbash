% ==================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ==================================================

Recent work on Categorical Deep Learning proposes a unifying mathematical
language for neural network design, one capable of bridging the traditional
divide between top-down constraints and bottom-up implementation. By appealing
to category theory—specifically to universal algebra in a 2-category of
parametric maps—this approach offers a principled account of architectural
invariance, recursion, and weight tying.

The ambition of this project is substantial and largely justified. Many
longstanding heuristics in deep learning acquire clean algebraic explanations,
and entire classes of models can be understood as instances of a single
compositional framework. However, this essay argues that the very success of
this unification exposes a boundary. While categorical methods excel at
formalizing constraints on computation, they do not capture commitment in the
sense required for agency, history, or accountability.

The argument is not that the framework is incomplete by accident, but that it
is complete with respect to a narrower target: lawful computation. What lies
beyond that target—irreversible obligation, refusal, and material consequence—
cannot be expressed without extending the ontology of the theory.

% ==================================================
\section{Algebraic Constraints and Their Scope}
% ==================================================

Algebraic invariance captures a wide class of constraints that are structural or
spatial in nature. In Geometric Deep Learning, permutation equivariance,
translation equivariance, and other symmetries ensure that a model treats certain
variations of input as irrelevant. These constraints are invertible: applying a
symmetry and then undoing it preserves information.

Categorical Deep Learning generalizes this picture by relaxing invertibility.
By moving from groups to monoids and more general categorical structures, it
accommodates non-invertible computation. Algorithms such as shortest-path
solvers map many distinct inputs to a single output, destroying information in
the process. Category theory provides a natural language for such one-way
transformations.

However, these constraints remain constraints on computation rather than on
action. They shape how a function behaves, not how an agent is bound by what it
has done. The framework distinguishes lawful from unlawful mappings, but it does
not distinguish reversible exploration from irreversible settlement.

% ==================================================
\section{Reparameterization Without Cost}
% ==================================================

Within the categorical framework, constraints such as weight tying are expressed
as morphisms in a 2-category of parametric maps. Reparameterizations are
two-morphisms that preserve the algebraic structure of computation. A neural
network respects a constraint insofar as it remains a homomorphism of the
relevant algebra.

This perspective clarifies why certain architectural choices work. If a
reparameterization violates the preserved structure—if a supposed recurrent
cell fails to respect the fold structure of a list, for example—it is no longer
a valid morphism in the category.

Yet these constraints remain costless. They can be removed, altered, or retied
so long as the algebraic laws are satisfied. Nothing in the framework renders a
constraint binding in time. The distinction between \emph{having} a constraint
and being \emph{committed} to it is not represented.

\section{Parametric Maps and Asymmetric Loss}
% ==================================================

Parametric maps make parameters explicit objects of study. They explain weight
sharing, modularity, and multi-agent coordination at the level of structure.
Non-invertible morphisms represent information loss during computation.

Yet this loss is internal to the transformation. Information is destroyed, but
no agent bears the cost. The framework does not model ownership of parameters,
control over updates, or unilateral loss. Asymmetry exists in the mapping, not in
the distribution of consequence.

This distinction becomes decisive when considering cooperation. Structural
alignment is sufficient for compositionality, but not for shared risk.

% ==================================================
\section{Refusal as a Missing Primitive}
% ==================================================

The framework occasionally gestures toward refusal, for example when suggesting
that a system might decline to answer a question that is computationally
intractable. However, this refusal is instrumental rather than principled. It is
a performance optimization, not a binding negation.

Selection, optimization, and reparameterization dominate the formalism.
Negation appears only as a boundary case. Treating refusal as first-class would
require tracking paths not taken and rendering them causally relevant—something
the current categorical apparatus does not do.

Without refusal, constraints remain advisory.

% ==================================================
\section{Time, Reversibility, and Worldhood}
% ==================================================

Categorical methods distinguish reversible symmetries from non-invertible
computations, but they do not distinguish reversible interaction from
irreversible lived time. Optimization proceeds indefinitely. Processes continue
until externally halted.

To end a game, rather than compose another morphism, would require a terminal
commitment: a state from which no lawful continuation exists. Such states are
not part of the framework’s ontology.

A system that optimizes indefinitely forms a process, not a world.

% ==================================================
\section{The Limits of Algebraic Unification}
% ==================================================

Algebraic unification discards detail inaccessible to structure-preserving maps.
This is its strength. It is also its limit. Phenomena that only appear once
something cannot be undone—obligation, accountability, history—vanish under
purely compositional descriptions.

The framework acknowledges that “more is different,” but its response is to
seek higher abstraction, not deeper settlement. Lawfulness increases; binding
does not.

% ==================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ==================================================

Categorical Deep Learning succeeds at unifying constraints and implementation. It
offers a principled account of invariance, recursion, and non-invertible
computation. In doing so, it formalizes intelligence as lawful structure.

What it does not formalize is commitment. Constraints shape computation, but
they do not bind future action. Irreversibility appears as information loss, not
as obligation. Refusal remains an optimization strategy rather than a primitive.

If computation explains how systems act, it does not explain why they never
give. To cross that boundary would require extending the theory beyond algebraic
lawfulness into a domain where actions can settle, costs can be borne, and
history can accumulate.
