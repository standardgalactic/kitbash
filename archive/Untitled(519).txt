1. Historical Fears of Government Economic Control 
Throughout history, governments have sought control over economies, from the era of 
Roosevelt's gold confiscation in 1933 to the postwar Bretton Woods era when states 
imposed capital controls and repressive financial policies to manage economies and pay off 
debt.?1? 
Economic sanctions and “financial weapons” have been used as tools of war and coercion, 
making populations fear the broader reach of government in economic life.?2? 
Governments have often used fear (of enemies, instability, or crisis) to justify expanded 
economic control, which can stoke distrust and anxiety among citizens.?3? ?4? 
2. Fears of a Cashless Society and Surveillance 
The move toward a cashless world is linked with rising fears of being constantly monitored. 
Every digital transaction can be recorded, leading to concerns over privacy, surveillance, 
and loss of autonomy.?5? ?6? 
Without cash, people can no longer buy anonymously, raising ethical questions about data 
ownership, surveillance, behavior profiling, and the possibility of government or corporate 
overreach. This anxiety is especially pronounced in countries like China, where social credit 
systems already exist.?6? 
?5? 
People worry about targeted marketing, the potential for data misuse, and even the freezing 
or seizure of funds by authorities, which would not be possible with cash.?5?

4. Modern Context 
Current concerns are revived by new technologies: digital currencies, the push for cashless 
payment systems, and increased data collection by both states and corporations. 
Privacy advocates and civil liberties groups often warn about the potential abuse of these 
powers, underlining the importance of democratic control, transparency, and the need for
