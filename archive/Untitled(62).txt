Historical Echoes: Giordano Bruno’s Donkey Rebellion and the Long Pattern of Suppressed Resistance

The failed unionization subplot in Good Fortune resonates with a much older current in Western intellectual history, one that surfaces unexpectedly in the allegorical writings attributed to Giordano Bruno. In one of the earliest Bruno traditions—fragments of which survive through references in later commentators—we encounter a striking fable: aboard Noah’s Ark, the donkeys rise up in protest against the foxes, lions, and other noble beasts who occupy the upper decks in comfort while the donkeys perform the exhausting labor necessary to keep the vessel afloat. The donkeys complain that the arrangement is unjust, that the burden is unequally distributed, and that the Ark, supposedly the divine refuge of all creatures, has become an emblem of rigid hierarchy. Their rebellion is quashed, the status quo restored, and—most telling of all—contemporary readers recorded that a pope, perhaps ironically, expressed approval of the story.

What Bruno encoded in this miniature allegory, with characteristic sharpness, was not simply an animal fable but a critique of the monastic and ecclesiastical hierarchies of his time. The foxes and lions stood in for the well-fed monks and priests whose spiritual prestige exempted them from manual labor, while the donkeys represented the lay brothers, servants, and lower clergy who bore the physical burdens that sustained monastic life. Bruno’s fable, like Orwell’s Animal Farm centuries later, reveals a structural truth: when a hierarchy is framed as sacred, natural, or divinely ordained, resistance becomes not merely futile but narratively suppressed. Even when the oppressed articulate their grievances with perfect clarity, the story snaps back to its original configuration. The donkeys return to their work. The foxes remain in comfort. The Ark continues its voyage unchanged.

The union subplot in Good Fortune follows this same allegorical arc almost to the letter. The workers recognize their conditions as exploitative. They attempt organization. They challenge the internal order of their Ark—the megastore whose corporate structure mirrors the stratification of Bruno’s monastic metaphor. But the film, constrained by the committees governing its production, cannot imagine the rebellion succeeding. It must collapse quickly, harmlessly, even humorously. The laborers return to their posts. Management remains unshaken. And the audience is gently ushered into the belief that this outcome is natural, inevitable, perhaps even for the best.

The parallel is precise enough that it illuminates the deeper ideological machinery at work. Bruno’s fable was subversive in content but conservative in structure; even while exposing hypocrisy, it reproduced the inevitability of hierarchy. In this sense, it prefigured the dialectic that Karl Marx would later formalize: the oppressed classes develop consciousness, articulate demands, and confront their oppressors, but institutional power, ideological conditioning, and material force conspire to prevent structural change. The donkeys anticipate the proletariat; the upper decks stand in for capital; Noah’s Ark becomes a floating prototype of the factory, monastery, and modern corporation. The rebellion’s failure is not merely narrative convenience but a reflection of how systems resist their own transformation.

When a pope expressed approval of Bruno’s story, the endorsement was not of the donkeys’ insight but of the story’s final shape. The rebellion’s suppression reaffirmed the perceived rightness of hierarchical order. The fact that the critique had been voiced at all became harmless once the story closed over it. It is this same mechanism that animates Good Fortune. The film acknowledges exploitation, precarity, and the need for solidarity, but the committee-governed narrative ensures that these gestures loop back into a resigned equilibrium. The union organizer plays the role of Bruno’s donkey: brave, lucid, momentarily articulate, yet destined to be overwritten by the structure he seeks to challenge.

Seen through this historical lens, the film’s ideological wobble is not an accidental feature but a recapitulation of a long cultural lineage in which stories about resistance are permitted as long as the resistance fails. What Bruno encoded as monastic satire becomes, in Good Fortune, a corporate fable. What Orwell dramatized as a tragedy of revolutionary betrayal is here rendered as comedy. And what Marx diagnosed as a structural impossibility becomes in the film a harmless subplot, a brief ripple in an otherwise placid narrative sea.

This echo across centuries reveals something crucial about the constraints shaping contemporary cinema: the suppressed union, like Bruno’s crushed rebellion, is not simply a narrative event but an ideological function. It preserves hierarchy by depicting resistance as admirable but doomed, righteous but impractical, imaginable but never victorious. The end result is the same in all three cases: the Ark continues on its course, unchanged; the workers return to their stations; the film resets to equilibrium. Only the faint residue of critique—Bruno’s irony, Orwell’s despair, Good Fortune’s end-credits plea—remains to signal that another world was glimpsed but never allowed to cohere.
