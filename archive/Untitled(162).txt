\begin{abstract}
% ==================================================

This essay argues that storage-centric models of computation—organized around
files, snapshots, and persistent state—are artifacts of reversible abstraction
rather than reflections of computational reality. As computation has become
distributed, collaborative, and semantically heterogeneous, these models no
longer adequately describe how meaning is produced, maintained, or reconciled.

We propose an alternative perspective in which computation is understood as an
irreversible, constraint-preserving semantic process operating over local
contexts. From this standpoint, merge is not a lossless operation but an
entropy-increasing physical event; infrastructure is not a repository but a
field of constraints; and state is not primary but derived from event-historical
processes. The resulting framework reframes computation as ongoing maintenance
of coherence rather than execution over stored artifacts.
\end{abstract}

% ==================================================
\section{Introduction: The End of Storage as a Primitive}
% ==================================================

Classical computational infrastructure treats storage as a primitive: files,
records, and persisted objects are taken to be the natural units of meaning,
coordination, and control. This assumption was historically justified by the
constraints of early computing systems, in which computation was local,
sequential, and materially expensive.

However, contemporary computation—distributed across networks, mediated by
human collaboration, and embedded in evolving social contexts—no longer conforms
to this model. The persistence of storage metaphors obscures the irreversibility,
context-dependence, and semantic fragility of modern computational practice.

This essay advances the thesis that computation is no longer organized around
persisted state, but around constraint-respecting transformations under entropy.

% ==================================================
\section{Storage Is a Reversible Fiction}
% ==================================================

Storage presupposes a cluster of assumptions: stable identity over time,
reversible access to past states, and negligible entropy cost for persistence.
These assumptions allow stored artifacts to be treated as timeless referents,
independent of context and history.

In practice, these assumptions fail. Distributed systems fracture identity;
collaborative interpretation induces semantic drift; and reconciliation between
divergent histories incurs irreversible cost. Storage thus functions as an
epistemic convenience rather than a physical or semantic primitive.

\begin{remark}
The persistence of storage metaphors reflects cognitive and institutional
inertia rather than ontological necessity.
\end{remark}

% ==================================================
\section{Computation as Irreversible Process}
% ==================================================

From a thermodynamic perspective, computation is an irreversible process.
Operations increase global entropy even when logical reversibility is preserved
at an abstract level. The distinction between logical reversibility and physical
irreversibility is therefore essential.

History cannot be undone. It can only be reinterpreted, recontextualized, or
locally repaired under constraint. Any model of computation that treats reversal
as primitive mistakes simulation for reality.

% ==================================================
\section{From Files to Semantic Locality}
% ==================================================

Meaning does not reside in stored objects. It emerges from admissible
transformations within local contexts constrained by consistency, authorization,
and interpretability.

We introduce the notion of \emph{semantic locality}: the domain within which
meaningful coordination is possible. Semantic locality determines what is
knowable, what is mergeable, and what can be rendered coherent without external
intervention.

\begin{definition}[Semantic Locality]
A semantic locality is a bounded context in which transformations preserve
interpretability under shared constraints.
\end{definition}

% ==================================================
\section{Infrastructure as Constraint Space}
% ==================================================

Infrastructure is commonly understood as either a repository or a transport
layer. Both conceptions are inadequate. Infrastructure is better understood as a
space of constraints shaping admissible transformations over time.

Type systems, schemas, protocols, and social norms function not as data stores
but as slow-moving semantic regulators. They define what kinds of coherence are
possible and at what cost.

% ==================================================
\section{Merge Is Not an Operation, but a Physical Event}
% ==================================================

Merge is often treated as a deterministic, commutative, and automatable
operation. In reality, merge is an entropy-increasing reconciliation between
incompatible histories.

Merge is neither lossless nor fully automatable. It is a local repair of global
inconsistency under constraint, and it necessarily discards information.

\begin{proposition}
No nontrivial merge operation can be simultaneously lossless, commutative, and
fully automated.
\end{proposition}

% ==================================================
\section{A Sheaf-Theoretic View of Meaning}
% ==================================================

Sheaf theory offers a vocabulary for reasoning about local coherence without
requiring global consistency. Local sections correspond to semantic commitments;
gluing corresponds to merge under constraint.

Obstructions represent irreducible conflicts rather than system failures. Global
consistency is optional; local consistency is not.

\begin{remark}
This perspective explains why partial coherence is not a defect but a stable
regime.
\end{remark}

% ==================================================
\section{Computation Without Snapshots}
% ==================================================

Event-historical computation treats state as derived rather than primary. Logs,
traces, and projections replace snapshots as the fundamental representational
objects.

The notion of a single \enquote{current state} is always contextual and
observer-dependent. Snapshots are conveniences, not foundations.

% ==================================================
\section{Entropy, Cost, and Semantic Scarcity}
% ==================================================

Semantic capacity is scarce. Not all meanings can coexist, and interpretability
is limited by entropy rather than storage.

Every semantic commitment incurs irreversible cost. Coherence must be actively
maintained rather than passively preserved.

% ==================================================
\section{Post-Git Infrastructure}
% ==================================================

Version control systems such as Git represent a transitional stage. They correctly
recognize the importance of immutability and history, but they cannot express
semantic intent, constraint violation, or partial coherence.

Post-storage systems must operate directly on constraint satisfaction rather
than artifact synchronization.

% ==================================================
\section{Computation as Maintenance, Not Execution}
% ==================================================

The dominant metaphor of computation as execution obscures the ongoing labor of
semantic repair. Contemporary systems persist through maintenance, not runs.

Computation is the stewardship of constraint satisfaction over time.

% ==================================================
\section{Implications for Intelligence and Agency}
% ==================================================

Intelligence is not the possession of a global model but the capacity to navigate
constraints locally under entropy. Agency consists in selective projection and
commitment rather than representation.

Adequacy is local; completeness is unnecessary.

% ==================================================
\section{Limits of Automation}
% ==================================================

Full semantic automation is impossible. Human judgment functions as an entropy
management mechanism, resolving conflicts that cannot be reduced to formal
criteria.

Automation accelerates local processes but cannot eliminate global ambiguity.

% ==================================================
\section{Related Frameworks and Positioning}
% ==================================================

This framework contrasts with classical computation theory, database-centric
models, knowledge graph approaches, and immutability narratives derived from
blockchain systems. It does not deny the utility of these approaches, but rejects
their elevation to ontological status.

Storage was a historical scaffold, not a foundation. As computation escapes the
conditions under which storage metaphors were viable, new conceptual frameworks
are required.

Computation is an irreversible semantic evolution. Infrastructure is physics,
not tooling.

% ==================================================
\section{Thermodynamic Foundations of Computation}
% ==================================================

Any attempt to understand computation without storage must confront the physical
conditions under which computation occurs. Computation is not an abstract mapping
between symbols; it is a physical process instantiated in time, subject to
irreversibility, dissipation, and constraint.

The conventional separation between logical computation and physical realization
has encouraged the illusion that reversibility is a primitive property of
computation itself. In reality, reversibility is at best a simulated property of
certain idealized formalisms. Every physically realizable computational process
is embedded in an environment with finite energy, finite precision, and
irreversible coupling to unobserved degrees of freedom.

We therefore adopt a thermodynamic perspective in which computation is understood
as a history-dependent process whose forward evolution is constrained but whose
past cannot be reconstructed without loss.

\begin{definition}[Computational Entropy]
Let a computational process transform an admissible semantic state $s_0$ into
$s_1$. The computational entropy associated with this transformation is defined
as
\[
\Delta S(s_0 \to s_1) \;=\; k_B \ln \Omega(s_1) - k_B \ln \Omega(s_0) + S_{\mathrm{diss}},
\]
where $\Omega(s)$ denotes the number of microstates compatible with the semantic
macrostate $s$, and $S_{\mathrm{diss}}$ accounts for dissipated entropy arising
from information erasure and constraint enforcement.
\end{definition}

This formulation makes explicit that entropy increase is not an implementation
detail but a structural feature of semantic transformation. Constraint
preservation itself requires energetic expenditure, since maintaining semantic
structure against noise and drift demands active regulation.

\begin{theorem}[Irreversibility of Constraint-Preserving Computation]
Any computational transformation $T : S \to S$ that is many-to-one, preserves a
nontrivial constraint set, and is realized with finite energy in finite time
necessarily satisfies $\Delta S(T) > 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Because $T$ is many-to-one, it erases information about prior states. By
Landauer's principle, erasure of $n$ bits of information requires dissipation of
at least $n k_B T \ln 2$ of heat. Constraint preservation further restricts the
space of admissible microstates, preventing arbitrarily efficient realizations.
Thus entropy production is unavoidable.
\end{proof}

This result formalizes the intuition that semantic reconciliation cannot be free.
The cost of meaning is paid in entropy.

% ==================================================
\section{Formal Structure of Semantic Localities}
% ==================================================

The notion of semantic locality introduced earlier can now be given a precise
mathematical form. The purpose of this formalization is not to impose an
axiomatic straitjacket, but to make explicit which properties of locality are
structural and which are contingent.

\begin{definition}[Context Space]
A context space is a tuple $\mathcal{C} = (S, \mathcal{T}, \vdash, \Delta)$ where
$S$ is a set of semantic states, $\mathcal{T}$ is a set of admissible
transformations, $\vdash$ is a satisfaction relation between states and
constraints, and $\Delta$ assigns a nonnegative entropy cost to each
transformation-state pair.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Semantic Locality]
A semantic locality is a context space equipped with a coherence predicate
$\mathrm{Coh} : S \to \{0,1\}$ satisfying closure under admissible
transformations, preservation of satisfied constraints, and bounded entropy
production.
\end{definition}

Semantic locality formalizes the idea that meaning is not globally available.
Only those transformations that preserve coherence within an entropy budget are
admissible. This immediately implies that admissibility is not an independent
design choice but is induced by the constraint structure itself.

\begin{proposition}
For a fixed constraint set and entropy bound, the admissible transformation set
of a semantic locality is uniquely determined.
\end{proposition}

The significance of this result is conceptual rather than technical: it shows
that infrastructure does not \emph{choose} what transformations are allowed.
It merely instantiates constraints whose consequences are inescapable.

% ==================================================
\section{Merge as an Entropic Event}
% ==================================================

We are now in a position to sharpen the claim that merge is not an operation but
a physical event. Merge takes place when two histories, each locally coherent,
are brought into contact under a shared constraint regime.

Formally, merge corresponds to the attempt to construct a state that satisfies
the union of constraints induced by two distinct semantic localities. This
construction is necessarily lossy whenever the constraints are not jointly
satisfiable.

\begin{theorem}[Impossibility of Perfect Merge]
No merge process can be simultaneously lossless, reversible, fully automated,
and constraint-preserving for all admissible semantic states.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume such a merge exists. Losslessness requires preservation of all semantic
information from both inputs. Constraint preservation requires that incompatible
commitments not be jointly realized. Automation requires that no external
semantic judgment be invoked. For incompatible constraint sets, these
requirements are mutually inconsistent, yielding a contradiction.
\end{proof}

Merge therefore always involves semantic sacrifice. What is lost is not merely
data, but alternative histories that can no longer be sustained simultaneously.

% ==================================================
\section{Sheaf-Theoretic Semantics and Obstruction}
% ==================================================

The tension between local coherence and global inconsistency finds a natural
expression in sheaf theory. Semantic states can be viewed as sections defined
over regions of contextual applicability, with restriction maps encoding
forgetful projection.

Global coherence corresponds to the existence of a global section. Failure of
coherence manifests as a cohomological obstruction rather than as an error in
local reasoning.

\begin{theorem}
If locally coherent semantic sections disagree on overlaps, then no globally
coherent semantic state exists.
\end{theorem}

This result reframes conflict as a topological property of semantic space rather
than as a procedural failure. Merge becomes the task of minimizing obstruction
rather than eliminating it.

% ==================================================
\section{Event-Historical Computation}
% ==================================================

Once snapshots are abandoned, computation must be reinterpreted as an
event-historical process. State is no longer a primitive object but a projection
from history relative to a chosen interpretive frame.

Event-historical computation treats logs, traces, and commitments as primary.
What is commonly called the \enquote{current state} is a lossy summary optimized
for local action rather than a faithful representation of history.

This perspective aligns computation with physical processes such as sedimentation
and repair rather than with mathematical functions.

% ==================================================
\section{Integration with Entropic Field Frameworks}
% ==================================================

The preceding formalism is compatible with, and naturally extends, entropic
field-based models of structure. In particular, the tripartite distinction
between scalar semantic density, vectorial transformation flow, and entropy
production provides a continuous analogue of semantic locality.

Semantic states correspond to scalar field configurations; admissible
transformations correspond to constrained flows; entropy bounds correspond to
field dissipation limits. Merge events correspond to regions of high curvature
where incompatible flows interact.

From this perspective, infrastructure is not a layer atop computation but the
geometry within which computation occurs.

% ==================================================
\section{Agency as Constraint Navigation}
% ==================================================

Agency emerges when a system can selectively project futures that remain locally
coherent under entropy constraints. Intelligence does not consist in possessing
a global model but in maintaining adequate local projections across time.

This reframing dissolves the distinction between computation and cognition.
Both are instances of constrained semantic evolution.

% ==================================================
\section{Limits of Automation Revisited}
% ==================================================

Because semantic coherence is locally defined and globally obstructed, no
algorithm can fully automate merge, interpretation, or repair. Human judgment
functions as a mechanism for injecting external constraints that cannot be
derived from within the system.

Automation is therefore best understood as local acceleration within bounded
contexts, not as global semantic resolution.

% ==================================================
\section{Future Directions}
% ==================================================

Several questions remain open. The computational complexity of approximate merge
under bounded entropy constraints remains poorly understood. The relationship
between semantic locality and learning dynamics warrants further study. Finally,
the extension of these ideas to self-modifying systems raises unresolved
questions about stability and control.

% ==================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ==================================================

Storage was a scaffold erected under conditions that no longer obtain.
Computation after storage is not less disciplined but more physically honest.
Meaning is not stored, but maintained. History is not undone, but repaired.
Infrastructure is not a toolset, but a field of constraints shaping irreversible
semantic evolution.
