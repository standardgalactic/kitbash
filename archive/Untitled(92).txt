Key Additions in This Section

1. Complexity Lower Bound Revision

Previously estimated bits.

New remark: logical capacity of minimal life may be only bits if we consider just functional logic, not full biochemical redundancy.

But this is explicitly noted as a lower bound, since real cells include structural, metabolic, and error-correcting overhead.

Implication: algorithmic complexity ≠ biochemical complexity; life’s origin might be computationally simpler than often assumed.

2. Universal Computation in Chemistry

Highlights that chemical reaction networks (CRNs) can: 

Implement universal computation under mass-action kinetics.

Be Turing complete (supported by refs [66], [67]).

Suggests emergence of logic functions and computation-like behavior is not just possible but inevitable in large enough chemical spaces.

Reinforces the perspective that life = physical computation.

3. Discussion of Feasibility

A protocell could theoretically assemble within 500 Myr if:

\eta \sim 10^{-8} 

Introduces requirements checklist: 

Physical/chemical biases (compartmentalization, cycles, autocatalytic networks).

Persistence and error suppression for cumulative information retention.

Some phase transition-like leap in complexity (rather than smooth progression).

Main point: pure randomness is too lossy; prebiotic structure is necessary.

4. Open Questions

Where did directionality, memory, and error suppression come from without enzymes?

What environmental constraints enabled long-term persistence?

Timeline compression problem: LUCA’s complexity near 4.2 Gy ago implies protocells emerged even earlier.

5. Philosophical Turn

Terraforming hypothesis revisited: not implausible but adds complexity → Occam’s razor disfavors it.

Emergence as fulfillment of physics (Laughlin): reductionism breaks down not because physics fails, but because complexity masks predictability.

Raises the idea that quantitative feasibility, not exact reconstruction, may be the best we can achieve.

6. AI as a Catalyst

AI could: 

Explore attractor landscapes of chemical state spaces.

Reverse-engineer pathways to protocell assembly.

Detect deep regularities missed by human intuition.

Skepticism: generative AI ≠ origin-of-life solver, but might become a “Cohen’s microscope”—a transformative scientific instrument.

Subtle warning: if AI surpasses interpretability, science risks Gödelian incompleteness in explaining life.

7. Epistemological Coda

Ends with Wigner’s quote on the mystery of mathematics’ applicability, tying the discussion back to: 

The limits of reductionism.

The paradox of emergent order.

The possibility that life’s origin is undecidable within its own system.
