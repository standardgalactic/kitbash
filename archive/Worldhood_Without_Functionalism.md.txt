# Worldhood Without Functionalism: Intelligence as Irreversible Constraint

## Abstract

This monograph develops a formal critique of functionalist accounts of intelligence, arguing that behavioral equivalence and computational performance are insufficient criteria. Intelligence is argued to require worldhood: a structural condition grounded in the possession of a non-recoverable past that constrains future possibilities.

The core result is the No-World Theorem, which shows that any system architected to preserve full reversibility—so that no action permanently reduces its future optionality—is necessarily world-less. To operationalize this principle, the Spherepop calculus is introduced as an event-driven framework that distinguishes irreversible commitment (care) from history-erasing optimization (collapse).

The argument is generalized beyond cognition to physical systems, treating persistence as survival under irreversible constraint. This discrete model is lifted into a continuous field-theoretic description, the Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum (RSVP), whose coupled fields describe macroscopic world-formation and erosion. For cognitive systems, care is refined as attentional compression via sheaf theory, grounding meaning in irreversible self-binding rather than computation alone.

## 1. Introduction: The Structural Absence in Contemporary AI

Contemporary AI systems exhibit fluency without responsibility, adaptation without consequence, and coherence without commitment. These limitations are not accidental. They arise from architectures that erase internal history and preserve reversibility.

Worldhood is defined here as possession of a non-recoverable past that constrains future possibilities. Systems that cannot be bound by their own history, regardless of behavioral sophistication, cannot inhabit a world.

### Lemma 1 — Irreversible Constraint as Necessary for Worldhood

Let S_t denote the set of actions available at time t.  
If S_{t+1} = S_t for all transitions, then no world can arise.

Without irreversible constraint, no history can bind the future.

## 2. The No-World Theorem

### Definitions

Actional Possibility Space: S_t  
Optionality: Ω(t) = |S_t|  
Event: a transition e where S_{t+1} is a strict subset of S_t  
Event Cost: C(e) = Ω(t) − Ω(t+1)  
World: W = intersection of surviving possibility sets induced by events

### Theorem — No-World Theorem

If a system preserves optionality under all transitions, it is world-less.

Functional equivalence permits internal reversibility; reversibility precludes worldhood.
