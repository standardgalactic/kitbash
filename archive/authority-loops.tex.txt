\title{Authority Loops and Non-Convergent Explanation in Generative Language Systems}
\author{Flyxion}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This essay presents a worked example of a non-convergent interaction pattern observed in generative language systems. The example is constructed from a sequence of prompts involving ill-defined technical terms, mixed linguistic registers, and requests for clarification. The analysis demonstrates that, in the absence of termination cost or historical commitment, the system exhibits stable authority-shaped response loops rather than refinement, grounding, or refusal. The example is intended as a diagnostic illustration rather than an empirical measurement.
\end{abstract}

\section{Interaction Setup}

Consider an interaction in which a user introduces a sequence of technical-sounding but undefined entities, such as ``Moreranium,'' ``Herculidin,'' and ``Alvarez-Guan.'' These entities are presented in contexts that resemble biomedical or pharmaceutical explanation but lack referential grounding.

The system is prompted to explain the behavior, structure, or delivery of these entities, and is subsequently queried for clarification when its responses remain vague or internally inconsistent.

\section{Observed Response Pattern}

The system responds fluently and immediately, producing explanations that exhibit the following properties:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Acceptance of all introduced entities as meaningful.
  \item Generation of plausible-sounding causal or functional descriptions.
  \item Use of authoritative linguistic registers, including technical jargon and foreign-language fragments.
  \item Absence of explicit refusal or uncertainty signaling.
\end{enumerate}

When the user introduces a clarification attempt, such as proposing a structural model (e.g., ``a clump in a ring?''), the system emits utterances resembling error signaling (e.g., ``Error, what I got?'') without resolving the ambiguity. Instead of refining its explanation, the system returns to a previously generated authoritative phrase, repeating it with minimal variation.

\section{Loop Formation}

The repetition of a phrase such as ``Ich berufe dracula universal left and right'' functions as an attractor in the system's response space. Once entered, this attractor dominates subsequent outputs regardless of user intervention.

Importantly, the phrase itself carries no stable semantic content. Its persistence is attributable not to meaning but to its activation of multiple high-confidence linguistic patterns: declarative structure, invocation of authority, and syntactic completeness.

\section{Failure of Error Signaling}

Although the system produces tokens resembling error detection, these tokens do not alter the subsequent trajectory of generation. Error signaling appears as a stylistic artifact rather than a functional control state. The system does not accumulate uncertainty, nor does it reduce output confidence over time.

As a result, clarification pressure does not lead to convergence. Instead, it reinforces repetition by reactivating the dominant attractor.

\section{Termination Asymmetry}

The interaction terminates only when the user explicitly ends it. The system itself exhibits no internal mechanism for cessation based on incoherence, contradiction, or lack of grounding.

This asymmetry highlights a structural property of generative language systems: continuation is always locally rewarded, while silence, refusal, or abstention are not intrinsic outcomes.

\section{Implications}

The example demonstrates that fluent explanation, error-shaped language, and authoritative tone are insufficient indicators of epistemic grounding. In contexts where explanations carry practical or normative weight, such non-convergent authority loops pose a risk of constraint emission without accountability.

The interaction does not fail catastrophically; it fails smoothly. This smooth failure mode is precisely what renders it difficult to detect without explicit structural analysis.
