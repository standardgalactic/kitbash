That’s a great question — let’s break it down by focusing on the general developmental trends observed from childhood to adulthood:

1. Learning became more efficient with age
 → Older participants (especially adults) made more accurate decisions in both simple and complex environments.
 → They were better at adjusting how much they relied on general categories vs. specific item details depending on the situation.

2. Memory became more closely tied to learning strategies with age
 → For younger children, what they remembered wasn’t as tightly connected to how they learned.
 → For adults, the kind of information they used while learning (general or specific) strongly predicted what they remembered a week later.

3. Older participants showed stronger generalization when helpful
 → Adults were better at applying learned category knowledge to new (but similar) items when the environment allowed it (category-predictive blocks).

4. Children paid attention to both relevant and irrelevant details
 → Even when the environment didn’t require attention to specifics, younger participants still encoded them.
 → This may reflect broader, less selective attention in children.

5. Choice behavior became less noisy with age
 → Younger children were more “random” in their choices (higher stochasticity).
 → Adults made more consistent decisions based on what they had learned.

6. The ability to flexibly switch between abstraction levels emerged early but improved with age
 → Children could already shift between category- and exemplar-based learning strategies.
 → But with age, this flexibility became more efficient and better aligned with the reward structure of the environment.

In short:
As people aged from childhood into adulthood, they became better at (1) adapting their learning strategy to match the situation, (2) focusing attention on the most useful information, and (3) storing in memory exactly what they used to guide successful learning.
