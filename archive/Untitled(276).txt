\chapter*{Abstract}
Efforts to safeguard humanity from hypothetical artificial general intelligences (AGIs) increasingly rely on centralized oversight, epistemic control, and the presumption that unbounded intelligence is inherently unsafe. Yet these same measures risk entrenching mistrust at the human level, eroding the very feedback systems—dialogue, transparency, and corrigibility—that have historically stabilized civilization. This essay argues that the greatest danger of AGI precautionism lies not in machines but in the institutionalization of paranoia: a cultural substrate that precludes the mutual vulnerability on which trust depends.

\section{Setup: Agents, Fields, and Couplings}

Consider \(N\) agents indexed by \(i\) interacting over a domain \(\Omega\) and time interval \([0,T]\). Each agent carries \RSVP{} fields \((\scalar{\Phi_i}, \vector{\mathbf{v}_i}, \entropy{S_i})\) with smoothness sufficient for variational calculus. Let \(\kappa_{ij}\) be the trust (permeability) coefficients that mediate bidirectional error/entropy exchange between agents \(i\) and \(j\), and let \(\mathcal{G}\) be the undirected interaction graph with edges \((i,j)\).

We assume local conservation of description-length/energy density \(\mathcal{E}_i\) for each agent, with a global budget \(\sum_i \int_\Omega \mathcal{E}_i \, dx \le E_{\max}\), and we impose bounded flux at the boundary \(\partial \Omega\) to avoid pathological blow-up.

\section{The Trust Lagrangian}

Define the trust Lagrangian density \(\mathcal{L}\) as the difference between a coherence gain functional \(\coherence{C}\) and a risk/complexity functional \(\risk{R}\):

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \coherence{C}(\{\scalar{\Phi_i},\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}\},\{\kappa_{ij}\}) - \risk{R}(\{\entropy{S_i}\},\{\kappa_{ij}\}, \{\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}\}).
\end{equation}

A concrete, minimal choice that matches Appendix A’s semantics is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\coherence{C} &:= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{(i,j)\in \mathcal{G}}\kappa_{ij}\,\Big(\nabla\scalar{\Phi_i}\cdot \vector{\mathbf{v}_j} + \nabla\scalar{\Phi_j}\cdot \vector{\mathbf{v}_i}\Big),\\[4pt]
\risk{R} &:= \frac{\alpha}{2}\sum_{i=1}^N \big\|\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}\big\|^2 \;+\; \frac{\beta}{2}\sum_{i=1}^N \entropy{S_i}^2 \;+\; \frac{\gamma}{2}\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{G}}\kappa_{ij}^2,
\end{aligned}}
\end{equation}

The action is

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}[\Phi,\mathbf{v},S,\kappa]=\int_0^T\!\int_{\Omega} \mathcal{L}\,dx\,dt.

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}=\mathcal{L}

\section{Equilibria and Stability}

A steady state satisfies

\begin{equation}
\partial_t \entropy{S_i}=0,\quad \partial_t \lambda_i=0\Rightarrow \entropy{S_i}=0,\ \lambda_i=\text{const},
\end{equation}

If \(\gamma\) is too small (cheap coupling), the system over-connects and can oscillate (trust overshoot). If \(\gamma\) is too large (expensive coupling) or \(\beta\) is large (heavy disorder penalty), the system under-connects and fragments (paranoia phase). There exists a coherence window \(\gamma_{\min} < \gamma < \gamma_{\max}\) where the dominant eigenvalues of the linearized operator have negative real parts, guaranteeing return to equilibrium.

\section{Discrete-Time Implementation (Algorithmic Sketch)}

For timestep \(t\), iterate:

\begin{aligned}
&\text{(i) } \kappa_{ij}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \Pi_{[0,\kappa_{\max}]}\!\left(\kappa_{ij}^{(t)} + \eta_\kappa\, \frac{\partial \mathcal{S}}{\partial \kappa_{ij}}\right),\\
&\text{(ii) } \vector{\mathbf{v}_i}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \vector{\mathbf{v}_i}^{(t)} + \eta_v\, \frac{\partial \mathcal{S}}{\partial \vector{\mathbf{v}_i}},\qquad
\scalar{\Phi_i}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \scalar{\Phi_i}^{(t)} + \eta_\Phi\, \frac{\partial \mathcal{S}}{\partial \scalar{\Phi_i}},\\
&\text{(iii) } \entropy{S_i}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \entropy{S_i}^{(t)} - \Delta t\,\big[\nabla\!\cdot(\scalar{\Phi_i}^{(t)}\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}^{(t)}) - \!\sum_j \kappa_{ij}^{(t)}(\scalar{\Phi_j}^{(t)}-\scalar{\Phi_i}^{(t)}) + \sigma_i^{(t)}\big],\\
&\text{(iv) } \lambda_i^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \lambda_i^{(t)} + \Delta t\,\beta \entropy{S_i}^{(t)},\qquad
\eta \leftarrow \max\{0,\ \eta + \rho\,(\sum_i\!\int \mathcal{E}_i - E_{\max})\}.
\end{aligned}

\section{Governance Readout (Policy Parameters ↔ Phenomenology)}

Raising \(\gamma\) (cost of coupling) simulates regulatory or institutional friction that makes forming/maintaining trust expensive → lower \(\kappa\), weaker corrigibility, higher risk of fragmentation.

Raising \(\beta\) (penalty on disorder) encourages rapid suppression of uncertainty → faster growth of constraint prices \(\eta\), which—via \(\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}^\star\)—can either discipline or choke exchange depending on \(\alpha\).

Raising \(\alpha\) (action cost) damps aggressive flows \(\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}\), avoiding runaway optimization at the expense of slower alignment.

Tuning \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) balances exploration (interpretive work) vs. conservation (safety margins).

\textbf{Design Principle.} Maintain \(\gamma\) within the coherence window and allocate \(E_{\max}\) so that \(\eta\) does not chronically saturate; this preserves positive \(\dot{\entropy{S}}\) contributions and keeps the joint system in the mutual-corrigibility regime rather than the precautionary freeze.

\section{Summary Statement}

The Trust Lagrangian formalizes alignment as a variational compromise: maximize bidirectional interpretive–action coherence while minimizing action, disorder, and coupling overhead, under energy budgets and continuity of entropic exchange. Its stationarity conditions yield operational update rules for actions \(\vector{\mathbf{v}_i}\), interpretations \(\scalar{\Phi_i}\), and trust \(\kappa_{ij}\), and they identify parameter regimes where precaution either supports or erodes human-AI trust.
