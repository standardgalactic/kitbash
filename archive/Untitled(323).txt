\chapter*{Abstract}

Delta–sigma (ΔΣ) modulators remain the highest-precision tools for analog–digital
conversion, not because they suppress noise but because they \emph{shape} it.
This book provides a unified treatment of ΔΣ modulation that integrates
classical engineering, information geometry, entropy flow, and field-theoretic
perspectives. Quantization is recast as a symbolic projection operator, loop
filters become controlled vector flows, and noise shaping emerges as an entropy
routing mechanism. The Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum (RSVP) framework
provides a conceptual bridge linking ΔΣ behavior to cognition, thermodynamics,
manifold geometry, derived stacks, and active inference. The result is a
comprehensive hybrid text: rigorous for practicing engineers, expansive for
theorists, and conceptually deep for physicists and cognitive scientists.

\chapter{Introduction: Why Delta–Sigma? Why Now?}

Delta–sigma (ΔΣ) modulation occupies a unique place in signal processing and
mixed-signal engineering. It is simultaneously simple and profound: a system
that takes a coarse, noisy, highly nonlinear quantizer and produces astonishing
resolution by embedding it within a feedback loop. Historically, this principle
emerged from analog oversampling techniques, but its foundation lies deeper.

ΔΣ modulators work because they shape uncertainty. The quantization error is
not eliminated; it is \emph{redirected} into frequency regions where it causes
minimal harm. This redirection is traditionally understood through linear
systems analysis---noise transfer functions (NTFs), loop filters, signal
transfer functions (STFs), and oversampling ratios (OSRs).

Yet classical treatments are incomplete. They do not explain why noise shaping
is so universal, why ΔΣ loops remain stable despite severe nonlinearities, or
why oversampling drastically improves resolution even in nonideal conditions.

This book argues that ΔΣ modulators succeed because they instantiate a general
principle:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Any coherent system with limited resolution must actively shape its
own uncertainty.}
\end{quote}

This principle applies not only to electronics, but also to biological
sensing, cognitive inference, and physical field measurement. The ΔΣ loop,
viewed through the lens of the Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum (RSVP),
becomes an entropy-routing engine enforcing local curvature constraints on an
error manifold. The quantizer becomes a symbolic collapse map. The loop filter
becomes a vector-flow operator.

The goal of this book is to trace this structure from classical engineering
into deep theoretical territory---carefully, rigorously, without losing
practicality.

\chapter{Signals as Fields, Quantization as Dynamics}

Classical signal processing treats a signal as a real-valued function
\(u: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\). In a field-theoretic viewpoint, a signal is
a \emph{section} of a field bundle over time. This shift is conceptually small
but mathematically potent: it allows quantization, filtering, and feedback to
be interpreted as operators acting on fields and their derivatives.

\section*{2.1 Signals as Field Sections}

Let \(M = \mathbb{R}\) denote time, and let \(\mathcal{F} \to M\) be a
fiber bundle with fiber \(\mathbb{R}\). A signal is a smooth section
\(\phi: M \to \mathcal{F}\). Classical sampling corresponds to restricting
\(\phi\) to a discrete subset \(M_d \subset M\).

Oversampling corresponds to sampling on a finer lattice, thereby reducing
finite-difference curvature of \(\phi\) between adjacent points.

\section*{2.2 Quantization as Projection}

A quantizer \(Q: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{Z}\) is traditionally defined as a
memoryless nonlinearity. In the field-theoretic view, it is a discontinuous
projection:
\[
Q(\phi(t)) = \operatorname{Proj}_{\Lambda}(\phi(t)),
\]
where \(\Lambda \subset \mathbb{R}\) is a discrete lattice of representational
states. This projection creates a local increase in entropy:

\[
S_Q(t) = S(\phi(t)) + \Delta S_{\mathrm{proj}},
\]

where \(\Delta S_{\mathrm{proj}}\) depends on the collapse region.

\section*{2.3 Scalar, Vector, and Entropy Dynamics}

RSVP decomposes any evolving system into:

- A \emph{scalar potential} \(\Phi(t)\)
- A \emph{vector flow} \(v(t)\)
- An \emph{entropy field} \(S(t)\)

ΔΣ modulators reflect this decomposition naturally:
\[
\Phi(t) \leftrightarrow \text{integrator state}, \qquad
v(t) \leftrightarrow \text{loop filter dynamics}, \qquad
S(t) \leftrightarrow \text{quantization disorder}.
\]

This chapter establishes the vocabulary used throughout the book: signals as
fields, quantizers as projections, ΔΣ loops as field-flow systems, and
oversampling as entropy smoothing.

\chapter{Classical Delta–Sigma Modulation}

Before developing the RSVP-inspired formulations, we present a rigorous review

\section*{3.1 Oversampling and Quantization Noise}

A uniform quantizer with step size \(\Delta\) has mean-squared quantization
noise:
\[
\sigma_q^2 = \frac{\Delta^2}{12}.
\]
Oversampling by a factor OSR reduces in-band noise power by approximately
\(\text{OSR}^{-1}\).

\section*{3.2 Noise Shaping}

The basic ΔΣ loop consists of:

\[
e[n] = x[n] - y[n],
\qquad
w[n] = H(z)*e[n],
\qquad
y[n] = Q(w[n]).
\]

Linearizing the quantizer as:
\[
Q(w[n]) = w[n] + q[n],
\]
the output spectrum becomes:
\[
Y(z) = \underbrace{1}_{\text{STF}} X(z)
     + \underbrace{\bigl(1 - H(z)\bigr)}_{\text{NTF}} Q(z).
\]

\section*{3.3 Loop Filters and Implementations}

Common loop filters include:

- Integrator-based (CIFB, CIFF)
- Feedforward/feed-backward structures
- MASH cascades

\section*{3.4 Stability Considerations}

Classically, stability is addressed through:

- Root locus analysis
- Maximum stable amplitude (MSA)
- Describing functions
- Empirical evaluation

These methods work surprisingly well despite the inherent nonlinearity.

The rest of the book explains \emph{why} they work by revealing the underlying
field-theoretic structure.

\chapter{Entropy Shaping as the Core Mechanism}

Classical delta--sigma (ΔΣ) modulation is framed as a clever method for
high-resolution conversion using coarse quantization. In this chapter we reveal
the deeper structure: ΔΣ loops operate by \emph{shaping entropy}. The
quantization error is treated as a localized disorder field \( S(x,t) \) whose
distribution and spectral content are governed by the loop dynamics.

\section*{4.1 The Entropy Field \( S(x,t) \)}

Let \( S(x,t) \) denote the local entropy density of the state variable.
Quantization events produce sharp local increases:
\[
S(x,t^+) = S(x,t^-) + \Delta S_Q.
\]
These increases are not catastrophic because the ΔΣ loop redistributes the
entropy across time and frequency. The relevant PDE analogy is:
\[
\partial_t S = -\nabla \cdot J_S + \sigma_Q,
\]
where \(J_S\) is the entropy flux and \( \sigma_Q \) is the quantization source.

\section*{4.2 Noise Shaping as Entropy Redirection}

Noise shaping corresponds to forcing the entropy flux into high-frequency
regions. If \(H(z)\) is the loop filter and \( \mathrm{NTF}(z) = 1 - H(z)\),
then:
\[
S_\text{in-band} \approx \int_{\omega < \omega_c}
    |\mathrm{NTF}(e^{j\omega})|^2 \, S_Q(\omega) \, d\omega.
\]

A high-order NTF with a zero of order \(p\) at \(z=1\):
\[
\mathrm{NTF}(z) = (1 - z^{-1})^p G(z)
\]
pushes entropy toward higher Fourier modes.

\section*{4.3 Oversampling as Entropy Smoothing}

Oversampling increases the temporal resolution of the discretized system:
\[
\Delta t = \frac{1}{f_s}.
\]
With finer timesteps, the system behaves like a field under a more finely
grained operator, reducing local entropy gradients:
\[
\frac{S(t+\Delta t) - S(t)}{\Delta t}
    \sim -\alpha \nabla^2 S(t),
\]
where \( \alpha\) scales with OSR.

\section*{4.4 Boundary Conditions and Entropy Dissipation}

ΔΣ loops implicitly enforce boundary conditions on entropy:
\[
J_S(t=0) = 0, \qquad J_S(t=T) = \text{free}.
\]
This asymmetry creates a preferred direction for entropy export—high
frequencies or out-of-band channels.

Entropy shaping is therefore not a heuristic but a direct consequence of the
feedback geometry.

\chapter{Flow Dynamics of Delta--Sigma Loops}

ΔΣ modulators can be recast as controlled flow systems. State variables behave
as trajectories on a low-dimensional manifold governed by vector fields whose
structure determines stability, spectral content, and error distribution.

\section*{5.1 State Variables as Vector Fields}

Let \(x(t)\) be the integrator state. We interpret:
\[
\dot{x}(t) = v(x,t),
\]
where \( v : \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is the loop’s
\emph{vector flow}. In discrete time:
\[
x[n+1] = x[n] + v(n, x[n]).
\]

\section*{5.2 Loop Filter as Controlled Flow}

For a loop filter \(H(z)\) with state-space realization:
\[
x[n+1] = A x[n] + B e[n],
\qquad
y[n] = C x[n],
\]
the flow structure becomes:
\[
v(x,e) = A x + B e.
\]
Nonlinearity emerges only through the quantizer, but the flow captures most
stability properties.

\section*{5.3 Divergence and Stability}

Define the divergence of the flow:
\[
\nabla \cdot v = \operatorname{tr}(A).
\]
In stable modulators, \( \nabla \cdot v < 0 \) ensures the state trajectory
remains bounded under quantization perturbations.

\begin{lemma}[Small-Signal Stability]
If \( \rho(A) < 1 \) then the linearized ΔΣ loop is BIBO stable for bounded
quantization noise.
\end{lemma}

\section*{5.4 Nonlinearities as Field--Geometry Interactions}

The quantizer introduces curvature into the flow:
\[
x[n+1] = A x[n] + B (u[n] - Q(Cx[n])).
\]
This creates a piecewise-smooth dynamical system with invariant sets and
limit cycles. These geometrical structures explain phenomena typically
addressed only empirically in engineering texts.

\section*{5.5 Fixed Points and Attractors}

A fixed point satisfies:
\[
x^* = A x^* + B(u - Q(C x^*)).
\]
Because \(Q\) is discontinuous, fixed points correspond to quantizer bins.
The arrangement of these bins determines loop behavior in a geometric manner.

\chapter{Manifold-Aligned Representations}

Many real-world signals do not occupy \(\mathbb{R}^n\) uniformly. Instead, they
lie on low-dimensional manifolds reflecting physical constraints. ΔΣ modulators
can exploit this structure by aligning prediction to the manifold rather than
full ambient space.

\section*{6.1 Signals on Manifolds}

Let \( \mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^n \) be a \(d\)-dimensional manifold with
\(d \ll n\). A signal \(x(t)\) often satisfies \(x(t) \in \mathcal{M}\). The ΔΣ
loop predicts:
\[
\hat{x}[n] = f(x[n-1],\dots),
\]
and error becomes:
\[
e[n] = x[n] - \hat{x}[n].
\]

If \( \hat{x}[n] \notin \mathcal{M}\), the loop wastes dynamic range attempting
to predict unattainable configurations.

\section*{6.2 Never Predict Noise}

This principle states:
\begin{quote}
A ΔΣ modulator must predict only the manifold-constrained component, not the
noise component.
\end{quote}

Predicting noise makes the loop attempt to follow high-curvature directions,
reducing stability.

\section*{6.3 Manifold-Constrained Dynamics}

The predicted state is restricted by a projection:
\[
\hat{x}[n] = \Pi_{\mathcal{M}}(x[n]).
\]
This reduces effective error energy:
\[
\|e[n]\|^2 = \|x[n] - \Pi_{\mathcal{M}}(x[n])\|^2.
\]

\section*{6.4 Connection to Predictive Coding}

The manifold-aligned ΔΣ architecture mirrors predictive coding networks, where
the latent space evolves on a constrained manifold and prediction error drives
updates. In this sense, ΔΣ modulators implement a minimal version of predictive
processing.

\section*{6.5 Consequences for High-Resolution Converters}

- reduced noise shaping burden  
- improved stability margins  
- lower sensitivity to quantizer nonlinearity  
- enhanced robustness to overload  

This chapter completes the conceptual foundation for the remainder of the book.

\chapter{First-Order Delta--Sigma Modulators}

The first-order ΔΣ modulator is the canonical example of a system that achieves
high-resolution conversion using coarse quantization. Its structure is simple:
an integrator, a feedback loop, and a quantizer. Yet this simplicity masks a
deep connection between prediction, error diffusion, and entropy descent.

\section*{7.1 Classical Structure}

A first-order modulator has the form:
\[
x[n+1] = x[n] + (u[n] - y[n]),
\qquad y[n] = Q(x[n]).
\]

Linearizing the quantizer:
\[
y[n] \approx x[n] + q[n],
\]
we obtain the canonical noise transfer function:
\[
Y(z) = X(z) + (1 - z^{-1}) Q(z).
\]

\section*{7.2 Entropy Descent Interpretation}

The integrator behaves like a gradient-flow system minimizing quantization
error. Define an entropy-like potential:
\[
V(x) = \frac{1}{2} x^2.
\]
The dynamics satisfy:
\[
x[n+1] - x[n] = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} + (u[n] - Q(x[n])).
\]
This equation resembles a discrete entropy descent subject to quantization
forcing.

\section*{7.3 Stability via Entropy Bounds}

\begin{theorem}[Boundedness of First-Order ΔΣ]
If \(u[n]\) is bounded and \(Q\) is a mid-tread quantizer, then the first-order
ΔΣ modulator is globally bounded.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Because \(Q(x) = x + q\) with \(|q| \le \Delta/2\), it follows that the update
increment satisfies:
\[
|x[n+1] - x[n]| \le |u[n]| + \frac{\Delta}{2}.
\]
An inductive argument yields global boundedness.
\end{proof}

\section*{7.4 Power Spectral Density}

For an input \(u[n] = 0\), the PSD of the quantization noise is:
\[
S_y(\omega) = |1 - e^{-j\omega}|^2 S_q(\omega)
= 4 \sin^2(\omega/2) \frac{\Delta^2}{12}.
\]

\section*{7.5 Implementation Details}

Practical designs must consider:

- integrator saturation
- quantizer metastability
- clock jitter
- reference drift

Even so, first-order ΔΣ systems are robust due to their entropy-minimizing
structure.

\chapter{Higher-Order Delta--Sigma Modulators}

Higher-order ΔΣ modulators achieve much steeper noise shaping by stacking

\section*{8.1 Classical Architectures}

Canonical structures include:

- CIFB: Cascade of Integrators with Feedback
- CIFF: Cascade of Integrators with Feedforward
- MASH: Multi-Stage Noise Shaping
- Feedforward/Feedback (FF/FB) hybrids

Each produces an NTF with higher-order zeros at \(z=1\).

\section*{8.2 Entropy Routing Layers}

In the entropy-field picture, each stage acts as a routing layer redirecting
quantization disorder:
\[
S^{(k+1)}(x,t) = \mathcal{L}_k(S^{(k)}(x,t)),
\]
where \( \mathcal{L}_k\) is a loop-dependent smoothing operator.

\section*{8.3 NTFs as Operators on Entropy Fields}

An \(p\)-th order NTF:
\[
\mathrm{NTF}(z) = (1 - z^{-1})^p G(z)
\]
acts like a discrete derivative operator of order \(p\) on the quantization
noise field.

\section*{8.4 Stability: Classical vs. Entropic}

Classically:
- pole placement near the unit circle risks instability  
- excessive NTF peaking increases sensitivity to jitter  

Field-theoretically:
- large \(p\) increases local curvature of the error manifold  
- entropy flux becomes harder to dissipate  
- divergence of the flow can grow sharply  

\begin{lemma}
A necessary condition for stability is that the entropy flux divergence remain
negative:
\[
\nabla \cdot J_S < 0.

\end{lemma}

\section*{8.5 MASH as Decoupled Entropy Cascades}

MASH architectures cascade stable first- or second-order loops and recombine
their outputs to cancel intermediate quantization noise. This corresponds to a
multi-layer entropy cascade with explicit cancellation channels.

\chapter{Stability: Classical \& Field-Theoretic Criteria}

Stability is the central constraint in ΔΣ design. As the order of the noise
transfer function increases, the system becomes more sensitive to overload,
loop gain variations, and quantizer nonlinearities. This chapter unifies
classical stability analysis with entropy-balance and geometric flow criteria.

\section*{11.1 Classical Discrete-Time Stability}

The loop is stable if the poles of the linearized system lie inside the unit
circle. For a state-space realization:
\[
x[n+1] = A x[n] + B q[n],
\]
stability requires:
\[
\rho(A) < 1.
\]
However, quantization introduces state-dependent nonlinearities that break the
assumptions of linear analysis.

\section*{11.2 Describing Functions}

A describing-function approximation replaces the quantizer with a gain:
\[
Q(x) \approx k x,
\]
yielding an approximate stability test. While widely used, this method lacks
rigor and often fails for high-order modulators.

\section*{11.3 Entropy-Balance Stability}

Define entropy production:
\[
\sigma = \sum_n \left( S(x[n^+]) - S(x[n^-]) \right).
\]
For stability, the loop must dissipate entropy faster than quantization
injects it:
\[
\sigma_{\text{diss}} > \sigma_Q.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Entropy-Balance Criterion]
If the loop filter induces a negative-definite entropy flow:
\[
\nabla \cdot J_S < 0,
\]
the ΔΣ system remains bounded for all bounded inputs.
\end{theorem}

\section*{11.4 Divergence of Flow}

Let \(v(x)\) be the closed-loop vector field. The divergence:
\[
\nabla \cdot v = \operatorname{tr}\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\right)
\]
dictates the average contraction/expansion of phase space.

If \( \nabla \cdot v < 0 \), trajectories contract toward invariant sets.

\section*{11.5 Overload Recovery}

Overload pushes the state out of the quantizer's linear region. Recovery
requires that the vector flow re-enters the invariant set:
\[
x[n+1] = A x[n] + B (u[n] - Q(C x[n])).
\]
Entropy-balancing explains why some architectures recover and others do not.

\chapter{Noise Shaping Viewed as PDEs}

Noise shaping appears in ΔΣ loops because the discrete-time update rule
approximates a PDE with a particular structure. This chapter develops that PDE
interpretation.

\section*{12.1 State Update as a Finite-Difference PDE}

For a generic ΔΣ modulator:
\[
x[n+1] = F(x[n], u[n], q[n]).
\]
Expanding in a Taylor series:
\[
x[n+1] - x[n] \approx
\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\Delta t
= \mathcal{D}(x) + \mathcal{Q}(x),
\]
where \( \mathcal{D}\) is a diffusion-like term from filtering and
\(\mathcal{Q}\) is a forcing term from quantization.

\section*{12.2 Diffusion vs. Anti-Diffusion}

A first-order modulator behaves like:
\[
\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} \approx \frac{\partial e}{\partial t}
\]
with mild diffusion.

A second-order modulator approximates:
\[
\frac{\partial^2 e}{\partial t^2},
\]
which resembles an anti-diffusive operator—naturally unstable unless bounded
by nonlinearities.

\section*{12.3 Boundary Conditions}

In the spectral domain:
\[
X(\omega) = \mathrm{NTF}(e^{j\omega}) Q(\omega),
\]
which corresponds to a PDE with boundary conditions setting:
\[
S(\omega \approx 0) \to 0, \qquad S(\omega \to \pi) \to \infty.
\]

\section*{12.4 Spectral Leakage}

Finite-length analysis induces leakage that corresponds to open or reflective
boundary conditions in the PDE layer.

\section*{12.5 Link to RSVP PDEs}

RSVP defines entropy flows by:
\[
\partial_t S = -\nabla \cdot (\Phi v) + \kappa \nabla^2 S.
\]
ΔΣ loops approximate a discrete version of this equation, where \(v\) is the
loop filter and \(\Phi\) the scalar potential.
