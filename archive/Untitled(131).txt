\chapter{Consciousness, Coherence, and the Architecture of Feeling}

\section{The Problem of Coherent Minds}

Contemporary theories of consciousness have converged on a striking
diagnosis: whatever consciousness is, it is bound up with coherence.
Integrated Information Theory (IIT) identifies consciousness with the
irreducible causal structure of a system \citep{Tononi2004,Tononi2016};
Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT) associates it with globally
broadcast information \citep{Baars1988,Dehaene2014}; predictive processing
and the Free Energy Principle (FEP) treat perception and action as an
ongoing attempt to minimize variational free energy
\citep{Friston2010,Friston2019}. In different languages, each theory says:
a conscious system maintains a \emph{globally organized}, \emph{internally
consistent} pattern of inference.

Yet these frameworks share three gaps:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item They do not treat \emph{affect}---felt valence, urgency,
        and drive---as the \emph{primary} organizing signal.
  \item They do not explicitly connect neural dynamics to a
        relativistic, thermodynamic field ontology.
  \item They lack a complete computational architecture that could be
        implemented in machines while remaining faithful to biological
        and phenomenological constraints.
\end{enumerate}

Mark Solms' affective neuroscience directly addresses the first gap
\citep{Solms2021,SolmsPanksepp2012}. His central claim is that
\emph{feeling} is not an ornament added to cognition but the core evaluative
process by which an organism registers its own deviation from viable
homeostatic ranges. In this view, consciousness is essentially
\emph{affective}: it is what it \emph{feels like} for an organism to track
its own success or failure at staying alive.

This book proposes that we can close all three gaps at once by combining:

\begin{itemize}
  \item the Relativistic Scalar--Vector Plenum (RSVP) as a
        scalar--vector--entropy field ontology;
  \item CLIO (Coherent Layered Inference Optimization) as a hierarchical,
        affect-steered inference architecture;
  \item and a family of auxiliary frameworks (HYDRA, TARTAN, CoM, SIT,
        UFTC-SF, EBSSC, Semantic Infrastructure) that handle modularity,
        multiscale geometry, memory, and semantic coherence.
\end{itemize}

Across seven Parts, we will argue that:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Consciousness is recursive coherence in a scalar--vector--entropy
field, steered by affective gradients that encode the organism's departure
from viability.}
\end{quote}

Part~I begins at the base: it develops the computational architecture of
\emph{feeling} itself.

\section{Feeling Before Representation}

Philosophical debate has often treated consciousness as a problem of
representation: how do neural states represent the external world; how do
representations acquire content; how can they be ``about'' anything at all?
Solms reverses this emphasis. Long before an organism can form explicit
models of its environment, it must answer simpler questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Am I doing better or worse?
  \item Am I moving toward or away from viability?
  \item Is it safe enough to explore?
\end{itemize}

These questions are answered not in symbolic form but as global \emph{felt
states}: pleasure, pain, fear, relief, curiosity, boredom, and the rich
affective palette that accompanies them. A newborn infant does not yet
possess a world of stable objects, but it already has a world of valence.
On Solms' account, this affective layer is the \emph{first-person interface}
through which the organism experiences its own homeostatic performance
\citep{Solms2021}.

We take this affective primacy seriously and encode it into CLIO's
architecture:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item Affective state is modeled as a scalar field over the organism's
        internal state manifold.
  \item This field is treated as a gradient of a homeostatic potential
        function.
  \item All higher-level inference---perception, concept formation,
        metacognition---is modulated by this affective gradient.
\end{enumerate}

The guiding design principle is:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{A system that does not feel its own error cannot reliably correct
it.}
\end{quote}

In CLIO, ``feeling'' is a disciplined term: it is the subjective aspect of
a gradient flow on a homeostatic potential.

\section{The Organism as a Control Manifold}

We begin with a structural model of the organism as a high-dimensional
control manifold.

\begin{definition}[Organismic State Manifold]
Let $\mathcal{Z}$ be a smooth manifold whose points $z \in \mathcal{Z}$
encode the coarse-grained state of an organism. Coordinates on
$\mathcal{Z}$ include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item interoceptive variables (glucose, temperature, osmolarity, pH,
        blood gases, hormonal concentrations),
  \item exteroceptive summaries (visual scene descriptors, auditory
        spectra, nociceptive load),
  \item motor and policy variables (posture, current action, readiness
        potentials),
  \item drive parameters (hunger, thirst, attachment, pain, sexual
        arousal),
  \item and predictive parameters (weights of generative models, priors,
        precision estimates).
\end{itemize}
We call $\mathcal{Z}$ the \emph{organismic state manifold}.
\end{definition}

It is important to distinguish between:

\begin{itemize}
  \item the \emph{physiological state space}, the actual configuration of
        neural, hormonal, and bodily variables;
  \item the \emph{inferential manifold}, the internal model the system
        maintains about its own state.
\end{itemize}

In CLIO, these are coupled but not identical. The organism must infer its
own internal condition from noisy measurements, just as it must infer the
state of the external world \citep{Seth2013,BarelaFriston2015}.

\section{Valence as a Gradient Field}

We now introduce an explicit model of affective valence as a scalar field on
$\mathcal{Z}$.

\begin{definition}[Affective Field]
An \emph{affective field} is a smooth function
\[
A : \mathcal{Z} \to \mathbb{R}
\]
such that $A(z)$ encodes the instantaneous valence of state $z$ for the
organism: higher values correspond to positive affect (pleasure, relief,
interest), lower values to negative affect (pain, distress, dread).
\end{definition}

Intuitively, $A(z)$ is not an arbitrary labeling but a summary of the
organism's evaluation of its own viability. Following Solms, we treat
affect as the felt aspect of \emph{departure from homeostatic setpoints}
\citep{Solms2021,SolmsFriston2018}.

To make this precise, we introduce a homeostatic potential:

\begin{definition}[Homeostatic Potential]
Let $C_i(z)$ be constraint functions measuring deviation from
biologically viable ranges (e.g., glucose, temperature, osmotic pressure,
threat level). Define
\begin{equation}
U(z) = \sum_i w_i C_i(z)^2,
\label{eq:homeo_potential}
\end{equation}
with weights $w_i > 0$.
The zero-level set
\[
\mathcal{H} = \{ z \in \mathcal{Z} \mid C_i(z) = 0 \ \forall i \}
\]
is the \emph{homeostatic manifold}.
\end{definition}

The organism's core imperative is to keep its trajectory $z(t)$ close to
$\mathcal{H}$, minimizing $U(z(t))$ over time. We therefore identify the
affective field with the negative temporal gradient of $U$, plus an
entropic term:

\begin{equation}
A(z,\dot{z},\dot{S}) = -\frac{d}{dt}U\bigl(z(t)\bigr) - \alpha\,\dot{S}(t),
\label{eq:affective_gradient}
\end{equation}
where $S$ is a coarse-grained entropy measure on $\mathcal{Z}$ and
$\alpha > 0$ quantifies the contribution of internal disorder.

\begin{itemize}
  \item If $U$ is decreasing and $S$ is stable or decreasing, then $A$ is
        positive: the organism feels better.
  \item If $U$ is increasing or $S$ is increasing, $A$ is negative: the
        organism feels worse.
\end{itemize}

Equation~\eqref{eq:affective_gradient} formalizes Solms' claim that
\emph{affect is the organism's ongoing evaluation of how well it is meeting
its own needs} \citep{Solms2021}, while extending it with an explicit
entropic component.

\section{Drives as High-Priority Submanifolds}

Drives correspond to submanifolds of $\mathcal{Z}$ where specific
constraints dominate. Hunger, thirst, attachment, and thermoregulation have
different characteristic error profiles and temporal dynamics
\citep{Panksepp1998}.

\begin{definition}[Drive Submanifold]
For each drive $D$, define a subset $\mathcal{Z}_D \subset \mathcal{Z}$ and
a restricted potential $U_D(z)$ capturing the drive-specific contribution
to the global potential. The drive submanifold is the set
\[
\mathcal{H}_D = \{ z \in \mathcal{Z}_D \mid C_{D,i}(z) = 0\},
\]
and the drive error is $U_D(z)$.
\end{definition}

The full potential can then be decomposed as
\[
U(z) = \sum_D U_D(z),
\]
with different weights reflecting evolutionary priority. In affective
terms, this allows us to separate the contributions of different needs to
the global feeling state.

\chapter{The Homeostatic Manifold and the Geometry of Survival}

\section{Introduction}

The previous chapter introduced the organism as a control manifold
$\mathcal{Z}$ equipped with an affective field $A(z)$ derived from
homeostatic error and internal entropy flow.  
In this chapter we deepen the mathematical structure:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item we formalize the \emph{homeostatic manifold} $\mathcal{H}$,
    \item derive the geometry of deviations from viability,
    \item show how affective gradients encode the shape of $\mathcal{H}$,
    \item introduce Lyapunov and potential structures that ensure stability,
    \item and present a dynamical systems model of \emph{staying alive}.
\end{enumerate}

This chapter provides the geometric foundation on which CLIO's affective layer
(Level~0) will be built in Part~III.

\section{The Homeostatic Manifold}

The simplest way to express an organism's viability constraints is through a
collection of scalar error functions:
\[
C_i : \mathcal{Z} \to \mathbb{R}
\]
where each $C_i(z)$ measures the deviation of a biologically relevant variable
from its optimal range:
\begin{itemize}
    \item blood glucose,
    \item osmotic balance,
    \item oxygen saturation,
    \item body temperature,
    \item CO$_2$ levels,
    \item electrolyte concentrations,
    \item subjective threat level,
    \item pain or nociception.
\end{itemize}

We assume each $C_i(z)$ is smooth in a neighborhood of the viable regime.

\begin{definition}[Homeostatic Manifold]
The \emph{homeostatic manifold} is:
\[
\mathcal{H} = \{ z \in \mathcal{Z} : C_i(z) = 0 \text{ for all } i \}.
\]
\end{definition}

\noindent
When $z(t)$ lies inside or near $\mathcal{H}$, the organism is viable.
When $z(t)$ moves far from $\mathcal{H}$, survival is endangered.

Geometrically, $\mathcal{H}$ behaves like a submanifold of $\mathcal{Z}$ with
co-dimension equal to the number of independent homeostatic constraints.

\section{A Homeostatic Potential Function}

In Chapter~1 we introduced:
\[
U(z) = \sum_i w_i C_i(z)^2.
\]

This serves as a Lyapunov-like potential function (following \citealp{Krichmar2007,Solms2021,Friston2010}).

\begin{proposition}
$\mathcal{H}$ consists of the global minima of $U(z)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition $U(z) = 0$ if and only if all $C_i(z)=0$.  
Since each term is a nonnegative square, this is the unique global minimum.
\end{proof}

Hence
\[
z \in \mathcal{H} \iff U(z) = 0.
\]

The affective state arises from how $z$ moves relative to this set.

\section{Viability Region and the Geometry of Deviation}

Define a viability region:
\[
\mathcal{V}_\epsilon = \{ z \in \mathcal{Z} : U(z) \le \epsilon \}
\]

This is a tubular neighborhood of $\mathcal{H}$.

The gradient:
\[
\nabla U(z) = 2 \sum_i w_i C_i(z)\,\nabla C_i(z)
\]
gives the direction of steepest increase in homeostatic error.

The affective gradient defined in Chapter~1:
\[
A(z,\dot{z},\dot{S})
= -\frac{d}{dt} U(z(t)) - \alpha \dot{S}(t)
\]
can be expanded:
\[
\frac{d}{dt} U(z(t))
= \langle \nabla U(z),\, \dot{z}\rangle.
\]

Thus:
\[
A = -\langle \nabla U,\,\dot{z}\rangle - \alpha \dot{S}.
\]

\subsection*{Interpretation}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item If $\dot{z}$ points \emph{down} the gradient of $U$, then
          $\langle \nabla U, \dot{z} \rangle < 0$ and affect is positive.
    \item If $\dot{z}$ points \emph{up} the gradient of $U$, affect is negative.
    \item If internal entropy is rising, affect tends to become more negative.
\end{enumerate}

This geometric picture will be foundational for CLIO.

\section{Affective Gradients as Control Forces}

We now define affect as a control-theoretic scalar that modulates the dynamics
of the system.

Given intrinsic dynamics:
\[
\dot{z} = f(z,t),
\]
we augment them with an affective control term:
\[
\dot{z} = f(z,t) - k A(z,\dot{z},\dot{S}) \,\nabla U(z),
\]
where $k > 0$ is a gain parameter.

\begin{itemize}
    \item If $A>0$, the agent moves more strongly toward viability.
    \item If $A<0$, the agent’s motion is damped or reversed.
\end{itemize}

This unifies Solms’ neurobiological account \citep{Solms2021} with dynamical
systems control theory.

\section{Lyapunov Stability of the Affective System}

\begin{theorem}
If $A(z,\dot{z},\dot{S})$ is defined as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:affective_gradient},
then $\mathcal{H}$ is a locally asymptotically stable set.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Take $V(z) = U(z)$ as a Lyapunov candidate.  
Then:
\[
\dot{V}(z) = \langle \nabla U, \dot{z} \rangle.
\]
By construction:
\[
A = -\dot{V} - \alpha\dot{S} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \dot{V} = -A - \alpha\dot{S}.
\]
When $A>0$ and $\dot{S}$ is bounded above, $\dot{V}<0$.  
Thus trajectories decrease $V(z)$ until they reach a neighborhood of $\mathcal{H}$.
\end{proof}

This proves mathematically that \emph{feeling is a control signal for survival}.

\section{Drive Geometry and Competing Potentials}

Real organisms face many competing errors $U_D(z)$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hunger potential $U_{\text{hunger}}$,
    \item thermoregulation potential $U_{\text{temp}}$,
    \item threat potential $U_{\text{threat}}$,
    \item attachment potential $U_{\text{attach}}$,
    \item pain potential $U_{\text{pain}}$.
\end{itemize}

These potentials are not independent; they form a composite landscape.

The total gradient is:
\[
\nabla U = \sum_D \nabla U_D.
\]

Neurobiologically this corresponds to interactions between:
\begin{itemize}
    \item hypothalamic nuclei,
    \item nuclei of the solitary tract,
    \item parabrachial complex,
    \item periaqueductal gray (PAG),
    \item nucleus accumbens,
    \item amygdala.
\end{itemize}

Each is a gradient source in the affective control system.

\section{Drive Arbitration via Precision}

We introduce the concept that will later become central in CLIO:
\emph{precision weighting} of competing gradients.

Define a precision weight for drive $D$:
\[
\Pi_D = \sigma(\beta A_D),
\]
where $A_D$ is the drive-specific affective gradient.

Then the effective potential is:
\[
\nabla U_{\text{eff}} = \sum_D \Pi_D \,\nabla U_D.
\]

This is the first appearance of precision in the book.
In later chapters it becomes a full natural-gradient metric.

\chapter{Interoception, Valence, and the Anatomy of Feeling}

\section{Introduction}

In the previous chapter we introduced the homeostatic manifold
$\mathcal{H}$, the potential landscape $U(z)$, and the affective gradient
$A(t)$ that guides an organism back toward viability.  
We now deepen this model by situating affect within the interoceptive  
architecture of the nervous system.

This chapter accomplishes four goals:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Describe the interoceptive pathways that compute homeostatic error.
    \item Explain how the brainstem implements affect as a global control signal.
    \item Provide a formal model linking interoception to the affective gradient.
    \item Introduce the neuroanatomical substrates of Solms’ theory of feeling.
\end{enumerate}

We use the computational geometry of Chapter~2 to interpret biological
structures and their functional interdependence.

\section{The Interoceptive Axis}

Homeostatic information from the body is transmitted along the interoceptive
axis.  
Key structures (\citealp{Craig2002,Critchley2004}) include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS)} — primary sensory nucleus for visceral inputs.
    \item \textbf{Parabrachial nucleus (PBN)} — integration of nociception, temperature, and chemical states.
    \item \textbf{Hypothalamus} — master regulator of drives and metabolic setpoints.
    \item \textbf{Insular cortex} — cortical representation of interoceptive states.
    \item \textbf{Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)} — affective evaluation and motivational arousal.
    \item \textbf{Periaqueductal gray (PAG)} — primal affective center generating valence.
\end{itemize}

These regions form the biological backbone for evaluating $U(z)$ and generating
the affective field $A(t)$.

We now formalize these structures in the language of control manifolds.

\section{Interoceptive Measurements as Constraint Evaluators}

Each physiological variable provides a measurement:
\[
m_i(t) = x_i(t) - x_{i,0},
\]
where $x_{i,0}$ is a homeostatic setpoint.

Define:
\[
C_i(z) = m_i(t),
\]
so the overall potential is:
\[
U(z) = \sum_i w_i m_i(t)^2.
\]

These measurements originate in peripheral sensors:

\begin{itemize}
    \item baroreceptors (blood pressure),
    \item chemoreceptors (oxygen, CO$_2$),
    \item osmoreceptors (osmotic balance),
    \item thermoreceptors (temperature),
    \item nociceptors (tissue damage).
\end{itemize}

Their projections into the NTS and PBN allow the brainstem to construct
the \emph{gradient}:
\[
\nabla U(z(t)).
\]

This is the biological implementation of the mathematical structure derived in Chapter~2.

\section{The Affective Core: PAG, PBN, and Hypothalamus}

Mark Solms argues (\citeyear{Solms2021}) that affect originates in subcortical
circuits responsible for evaluating deviations from viability.
Three structures dominate:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PAG (Periaqueductal Gray)} generates primal feelings  
          (pain, panic, playfulness, attachment).
    \item \textbf{Parabrachial Nucleus} integrates visceral sensory data
          and produces ``urgency signals."
    \item \textbf{Hypothalamus} computes metabolic setpoints and drives.
\end{itemize}

\section{Formalizing Valence as a Composite Signal}

We now express the affective field as:
\[
A(t)
= f_{\mathrm{PAG}}
  \big(
    U(z(t)),
    \dot{U}(t),
    \text{nociceptive input},
    \text{visceral urgency},
    \text{drive state}
  \big),
\]
where $f_{\mathrm{PAG}}$ is the nonlinear mapping implemented by PAG circuits
(\citealp{Merker2007,Panksepp1998}).

We adopt the formal structure introduced in Chapter~2:
\[
A(t)
= -\frac{dU}{dt} - \alpha \dot{S}(t).
\]

But we now explicitly decompose:
\[
-\frac{dU}{dt}
= 
-\sum_i 2 w_i m_i(t) \dot{m}_i(t),
\]
which corresponds biologically to changes in:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hydration rate,
    \item thermal drift,
    \item blood pressure variation,
    \item oxygenation changes,
    \item nociceptive acceleration.
\end{itemize}

This decomposition will later be mapped to CLIO Level~0.

\section{The Necessity of Feeling}

Solms’ central thesis is that feeling is \emph{functionally necessary} for
self-regulation.

\begin{quote}
Feeling is the organism’s awareness of deviation from expected viability
(\citealp{Solms2021, Damasio1999}).
\end{quote}

Mathematically:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $U(z)$ measures deviation.
    \item $A(t)$ measures the \emph{rate} and \emph{direction} of deviation.
    \item $\nabla U$ tells the organism which way to move to survive.
\end{itemize}

Thus phenomenology is not an emergent ornament—it is a core regulatory variable.

\section{Valence as an Integrator of Multiple Homeostatic Errors}

Recall the drive potentials introduced in Chapter~2:
\[
U_{\text{hunger}},\;
U_{\text{temp}},\;
U_{\text{threat}},\;
U_{\text{pain}},\;\dots
\]

The affective system maps these to a scalar:

\[
A(t) = F\big( \{ U_D(z(t)), \dot{U}_D(t)\}_{D} \big).
\]

This scalar represents an integrated summary of:

\begin{itemize}
    \item overall organismic health,
    \item urgency of correction,
    \item expected viability,
    \item future safety.
\end{itemize}

This is the compression principle central to affective neuroscience
and a crucial precursor to CLIO’s precision-weighting schemes.

\section{Interoception as a Riemannian Metric}

We now reinterpret interoceptive signals geometrically.

Define a Riemannian metric:
\[
g_{ij}(z)
=
\sum_D w_{D,i} w_{D,j},
\]
where $w_{D,i}$ is the sensitivity of drive $D$ to variable $x_i$.

This metric measures how changes in different physiological variables contribute
to homeostatic error.

Thus:
\[
U(z) = g_{ij} m_i m_j.
\]

This geometrizes the organism’s internal condition.

\subsection{Consequences}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Some directions in $\mathcal{Z}$ are ``dangerous’’ (high curvature).
    \item Others are ``safe’’ (low curvature).
    \item Affect corresponds to motion along high-curvature directions.
\end{enumerate}

This geometric structure later integrates with CLIO’s information geometry.

\chapter{Drives, Viability, and the Geometry of Motivational Dynamics}

\section{Introduction}

In the preceding chapter we established the neurobiological and mathematical
architecture of interoception and affect.  
We now turn to the deeper structure underlying affective valence:
\emph{drives}.  

Mark Solms argues that consciousness is the felt registration of
deviation from viability \citep{Solms2021,SolmsFriston2018}.  
This deviation originates in the violation of \emph{drive conditions},
which define the organism’s metabolic and environmental requirements.

This chapter formalizes drives as:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item submanifolds of the homeostatic manifold,
    \item attractor basins in the potential landscape,
    \item boundary conditions defining viable trajectories,
    \item and generators of affective gradients.
\end{enumerate}

We synthesize the affective neuroscientific tradition  
\citep{Panksepp1998,Damasio1999,Craig2014}  
with the RSVP field ontology and the geometric framework introduced earlier.

\section{What Is a Drive?}

A \emph{drive} is a homeostatically necessary variable or combination of
variables whose violation threatens survival.  

Formally:

\begin{definition}
A \textbf{drive} $D$ is a constraint surface  
$C_D(z) = 0$  
in the homeostatic manifold $\mathcal{H}$ defining a viability boundary.
\end{definition}

Examples:

\begin{itemize}
    \item glucose concentration must remain within a range,
    \item body temperature must remain near 37$^\circ$C,
    \item osmolarity must remain within narrow limits,
    \item oxygenation must remain above threshold,
    \item tissue integrity must be preserved,
    \item social contact must remain above a mammalian minimum (Panksepp’s PANIC/GRIEF system).
\end{itemize}

Each drive defines an allowable region:
\[
\mathcal{H}_D = \{ z \in \mathcal{H} \; : \; |C_D(z)| < \epsilon_D \}.
\]

The intersection over all drives defines organismic viability:
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{viable}} = \bigcap_D \mathcal{H}_D.
\]

\section{Drive Potentials and Their Coupling to Affect}

Each drive yields a contribution to the total potential:
\[
U_D(z) = w_D \, C_D(z)^2,
\]
so:
\[
U(z) = \sum_D U_D(z).
\]

Taking the time derivative:
\[
\frac{dU}{dt}
=
\sum_D 2 w_D C_D(z) \dot{C}_D(z).
\]

The affective gradient (derived in Chapter 3):
\[
A(t) = -\frac{dU}{dt} - \alpha \dot{S}(t)
\]
thus decomposes into drive-level contributions:
\[
A(t) = \sum_D A_D(t) - \alpha \dot{S}(t),
\]
where:
\[
A_D(t) = -2 w_D C_D(z(t)) \dot{C}_D(t).
\]

This reflects the biological reality that different drives  
contribute varying degrees of urgency and motivational force.

\section{Drive Competition and the Priority Map}

The brain must constantly decide which drive to satisfy \emph{first}.  
Drive competition is mediated by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hypothalamus (setpoints),
    \item PAG and PBN (urgency and valence),
    \item basal forebrain (arousal),
    \item neuromodulators (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine).
\end{itemize}

We formalize this via a \textbf{priority map}:
\[
\pi_D(t) = \sigma\!\left( \beta A_D(t) \right),
\]
where $\sigma$ is logistic and $\beta$ is inverse temperature.

The drive with the highest $\pi_D(t)$ dominates action selection.

\section{Geometry of Drives: Basins, Boundaries, and Attractors}

Each drive contributes an attractor basin in $\mathcal{H}$.
For example:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hunger produces a basin around glucose setpoints,
    \item thermoregulation produces a basin around 37$^\circ$C,
    \item safety produces a basin around low-threat environments.
\end{itemize}

The steepness of each basin is given by $w_D$.

\subsection{Viability as Intersection of Basins}

Define basin $B_D$:
\[
B_D = \{ z : U_D(z) < \theta_D \}.
\]
Then:
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{viable}} = \bigcap_D B_D.
\]

This region is generally small, non-convex, and constantly in flux.

\section{Drive Urgency and the Temporal Derivative}

The \emph{velocity} of deviation matters more than the deviation itself.

We define:
\[
U_D'(t) = \frac{d}{dt} U_D(z(t)).
\]

High urgency occurs when:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $U_D$ is high, \textbf{and}
    \item $U_D'$ is positive and large.
\end{enumerate}

This explains why:

\begin{itemize}
    \item rapidly dropping glucose produces panic,
    \item rapidly rising temperature produces intense affect,
    \item slowly rising deviation produces mild affect.
\end{itemize}

The affective system therefore encodes both loss and loss trajectory.

\section{Drive Hierarchies}

Drives form hierarchical layers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Tier 1: Survival drives}
          (oxygen, pain, thermoregulation).
    \item \textbf{Tier 2: Homeostatic drives}
          (hunger, thirst, fatigue).
    \item \textbf{Tier 3: Social drives}
          (attachment, loneliness).
    \item \textbf{Tier 4: Predictive drives}
          (curiosity, epistemic uncertainty reduction).
\end{itemize}

These lower tiers take priority because their violation has  
higher curvature in $U(z)$.

\section{Drive Saturation and Completion}

A key property of drives is:  
\textbf{they saturate.}

After a drive is satisfied:
\[
U_D(z) \to 0,\qquad A_D(t) \to 0.
\]

This reflects PAG dynamics:
successful drive completion shuts down urgency coding.

This property later becomes crucial in CLIO’s  
precision-weighted updates.

\section{The Solms–RSVP Interpretation}

Solms:  
\begin{quote}
The brain must register the goodness or badness of a state in order
to act adaptively.  
Feeling is this registration.
\end{quote}

RSVP reinterpretation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item scalar field $\Phi$ encodes potential,
    \item vector field $v^\mu$ encodes policy/action,
    \item entropy field $S$ regulates global stability,
    \item drives are local curvature conditions on $\Phi$.
\end{itemize}

Thus drives correspond to:

\[
\text{curvature constraints on the RSVP scalar field}.
\]

This mapping becomes central in Part II.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter established:

\begin{itemize}
    \item drives as geometric constraints,
    \item drive potentials as curvature in the homeostatic manifold,
    \item drive urgency as the temporal derivative of error,
    \item softmax-like competition for behavioral priority,
    \item and viability as the intersection of multiple basins.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{motivational dynamics, action policies, and the control structure}
linking drives to behavior.


\chapter{Action Selection, Policy Geometry, and Motivational Control}

\section{Introduction}

Having defined drives as geometric constraints on the homeostatic manifold,
we now examine how these drives generate \emph{action}.  
In biological organisms, action selection emerges from the coordination of:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hypothalamic drive signals,
    \item PAG and PBN urgency encoding,
    \item basal ganglia policy gating,
    \item dopaminergic prediction error loops,
    \item cortical action construction.
\end{itemize}

We now formalize motivational control using the mathematical tools developed so far.
This chapter introduces:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item the \textbf{policy manifold},
    \item the geometry of \textbf{drive-to-action mapping},
    \item \textbf{urgency-weighted action selection},
    \item \textbf{multi-timescale control},
    \item and the \textbf{Solms–Friston motor hierarchy}.
\end{enumerate}

We then integrate these mechanisms into the RSVP–CLIO fields that will structure
Part II of the book.

\section{The Policy Manifold}

Let $\mathcal{P}$ denote the space of possible actions or policies.

Examples:

\begin{itemize}
    \item locomotor trajectories,
    \item ingestive actions,
    \item thermoregulatory behaviors,
    \item defensive responses,
    \item affiliative signals,
    \item exploratory movements.
\end{itemize}

Each element $p \in \mathcal{P}$ is a parameterized policy:
\[
p(\tau) \;:\; \tau \in [0,T] \mapsto \text{motor commands}.
\]

The organism must select a policy $p^\*$ that minimizes expected drive error:
\[
p^\* = \arg\min_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \;
\mathbb{E}\big[ U_D(z(t+\tau)\,|\,p)\big].
\]

The \emph{policy manifold} is the differentiable space on which learning,
refinement, and selection occur.

\subsection{Structure of the Policy Manifold}

The manifold decomposes into:

\[
\mathcal{P}
= \bigcup_{m} \mathcal{P}_m,
\]

where $m$ indexes behavioral modes (ingestive, defensive, thermoregulatory,
affiliative, exploratory, etc.).

Each $\mathcal{P}_m$ is a curved submanifold with its own metric $g^{(m)}$,
reflecting the biomechanics, latency, and ecological cost of actions.

\section{Drive-to-Policy Mapping}

The mapping

\[
D \longrightarrow p
\]

is not direct.
Instead, it occurs through two intermediate structures:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item the \textbf{urgency field} $A_D(t)$,
    \item the \textbf{priority map} $\pi_D(t)$.
\end{enumerate}

For any given drive:
\[
A_D(t) = -2 w_D C_D(z) \dot{C}_D(z),
\]
and:
\[
\pi_D(t) = \frac{\exp(\beta A_D)}{\sum_{D'} \exp(\beta A_{D'})}.
\]

Then:
\[
p^\* = \arg\min_{p} \sum_D \pi_D(t)
\, \mathbb{E}\big[ U_D(z_{t \rightarrow t+\tau}\,|\,p )\big].
\]

Thus the action selected minimizes total expected drive error,
weighted by urgency.

\section{The Basal Ganglia as a Policy Selector}

In vertebrate brains, the basal ganglia implement a
\emph{winner-take-most softmax}
over policy channels.

Let channels be indexed by $k$ with activation $a_k$.
Then:

\[
p^\* = \text{channel} \left( \arg\max_k a_k \right)
\]

where:

\[
a_k = \sum_D \pi_D(t) f_{D \rightarrow k}.
\]

The function $f_{D \rightarrow k}$ encodes which drives activate which policies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hunger → foraging, sniffing, ingestive policy channels,
    \item threat → freezing, fleeing, defensive channels,
    \item thermoregulation → shivering, panting, seeking shade.
\end{itemize}

The basal ganglia implement this through:

\begin{itemize}
    \item direct pathway (Go),
    \item indirect pathway (NoGo),
    \item hyperdirect pathway (conflict resolution).
\end{itemize}

\section{Formalizing Policy Geometry}

We adopt a geometric perspective:
each policy mode $\mathcal{P}_m$ is a curved, locally linearizable region:

\[
g^{(m)}_{ij}(p)
= \left\langle \frac{\partial p}{\partial \theta_i},
           \frac{\partial p}{\partial \theta_j}
  \right\rangle
\]

where $\theta_i$ parameterize motor synergies.

Policy refinement is natural gradient descent on $\mathcal{P}_m$:

\[
\Delta \theta_i =
- \eta \sum_j g^{(m)\,-1}_{ij}
      \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[U]}{\partial \theta_j}.
\]

This parallels the Friston–Amari information geometry framework
\citep{Amari2016,Friston2019}.

\section{Short-Horizon vs. Long-Horizon Control}

Drives operate on different timescales:

\begin{itemize}
    \item oxygen → seconds,
    \item thermoregulation → minutes,
    \item hunger → hours,
    \item thirst → hours to days,
    \item attachment → hours to weeks,
    \item epistemic drives → months to years.
\end{itemize}

We formalize this by:

\[
\tau_D \quad\text{(characteristic timescale)}
\]

and define separate short- and long-horizon components:

\[
A_D(t) = A_D^{\text{fast}}(t) + A_D^{\text{slow}}(t).
\]

Policy selection integrates over these components:

\[
p^\* = \arg\min_p
\sum_D \left[
\pi_D^{\text{fast}}(t) \, U_D^{\text{fast}}(p)
+ \pi_D^{\text{slow}}(t) \, U_D^{\text{slow}}(p)
\right].
\]

\section{Action Construction in Cortex}

Once a policy is selected, cortical systems translate abstract plans into:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item motor primitives,
    \item sensorimotor predictions,
    \item hierarchical control targets.
\end{enumerate}

This corresponds to:

\[
p^\*(\tau) = f_{\text{motor cortex}}
\bigl(
    f_{\text{premotor}}(
        f_{\text{PFC}}(p^\*)
    )
\bigr).
\]

The cortex implements \emph{policy instantiation}, while the basal ganglia
implement \emph{policy selection}.

\section{Drive Conflicts and Mixed Policies}

Drives often conflict:

\begin{itemize}
    \item hunger vs. safety,
    \item social seeking vs. fatigue,
    \item curiosity vs. pain avoidance.
\end{itemize}

Mixed policies emerge when multiple drives have comparable priority:

\[
p^\* = \sum_D \pi_D(t) \, p_D^\*.
\]

This corresponds to biologically observed compromise behaviors.

\section{Pathologies of Policy Selection}

The framework explains numerous psychiatric and behavioral pathologies:

\paragraph{1. Drive hyperinflation}  
A single drive dominates:
\[
\pi_{D^\*} \approx 1.
\]
Addiction, panic, or compulsive behavior.

\paragraph{2. Drive collapse}  
All priorities flatten:
\[
\pi_D \approx 1/n.
\]
Apathy, depression, catatonia.

\paragraph{3. Misaligned policy coupling}  
Improper mapping $f_{D \rightarrow k}$.  
E.g. trauma linking benign stimuli to threat policies.

\paragraph{4. Policy inertia}  
Defective switching dynamics (basal ganglia dysfunction).

\paragraph{5. Hyperflexibility}  
Excessive switching (dopaminergic dysregulation).

\section{The RSVP Interpretation}

In RSVP terms:

\begin{itemize}
    \item drives are curvature conditions on $\Phi$,
    \item policies correspond to vector flow patterns $v^\mu$,
    \item affect sets the entropy gradient $\nabla S$,
    \item action selection corresponds to minimizing drive-derived curvature.
\end{itemize}

Thus behavior is the relaxation of the RSVP field toward smoother structure.

This prepares the ground for Part II,
where we show how these neural dynamics generalize to the entire physical plenum.

In this chapter we showed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item how drives generate urgency and priority,

    \item how basal ganglia implement selection,
    \item the geometry of policy manifolds,
    \item the integration of short- and long-horizon control,
    \item and how action instantiation emerges.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Predictive Control, Hierarchical Motor Inference, and the Solms--Friston Action Cycle}

\section{Introduction}

The previous chapter described how motivational variables---drives,
urgency, and priority weights---select a policy from the manifold of possible
actions.  
This chapter explains how such a selected policy is \emph{implemented} in the
nervous system through predictive control.

We build here on three converging traditions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Solms's account of feelings as the governors of action--perception cycles
          \citep{Solms2022},
    \item Friston's hierarchical active inference framework \citep{Friston2010},
    \item Hollis's and Pezzulo's work on cognitive motor control \citep{Pezzulo2018}.
\end{itemize}

Together, these imply a simple but profound dynamic:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Action is not issued motor command by motor command.

We begin by defining the CLIO update rule in its most general form.

\section{The CLIO Update Operator}

Define an agent state at time $t$ as the tuple:
\[
\mathbf{z}(t) =
\bigl(
z_0(t), z_1(t), z_2(t), z_3(t)
\bigr),
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $z_0$: affective/homeostatic state,
    \item $z_1$: local sensory/predictive state,
    \item $z_2$: structural/relational latent state,
    \item $z_3$: metacognitive supervisory state.
\end{itemize}

Each layer has an associated free-energy functional $F_L$ and reliability
(precision) estimate $\Lambda_L(t)$, following the form used in
\cite{Friston2003LearningIF,Clark2013WhateverNT,Cheng2025CognitiveLV}.

The CLIO update rule is:
\[
z_L(t+1)
= z_L(t)
- \eta_L(t) \,
  \Pi_L(t) \,
  \nabla_{z_L} F_L,
\]
where:
\[
\Pi_L(t) = \sigma(\beta A(t)) \, \Lambda_L(t).
\]

Here:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $A(t)$ is the affective drive, derived from homeostatic deviation
          as developed in Chapters~3--4 following Solms \cite{Solms2021HiddenSpring},
    \item $\sigma(\cdot)$ is a bounded gain function,
    \item $\Lambda_L(t)$ is the precision or inverse-variance estimate,
    \item $\eta_L(t)$ is the adaptive learning rate.
\end{itemize}

This produces a combined effect of:

\begin{itemize}
    \item affectively modulated step size,
    \item precision-weighted natural gradient,
    \item hierarchical free-energy minimization.
\end{itemize}

\section{The CLIO Loop as a Contractive Map}

Define the global CLIO operator:
\[
\mathcal{C} : \mathbf{z}(t) \mapsto \mathbf{z}(t+1).
\]

Coherence requires that $\mathcal{C}$ is a \emph{contraction} on the joint state
manifold:
\[
\| \mathcal{C}(\mathbf{z}) - \mathcal{C}(\mathbf{z}') \|
< \kappa \, \| \mathbf{z} - \mathbf{z}' \|,
\qquad 0 < \kappa < 1.
\]

Using the analysis of natural-gradient dynamics
\cite{Amari1998NaturalGF,Ollivier2015RiemannianGI},
CLIO is contractive when:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item affective load is bounded: $|A(t)| < A_{\max}$,
    \item precision avoids collapse: $0 < \Pi_L(t) < \Pi_{\max}$,
    \item free-energy landscape is locally smooth, i.e.,
          the Hessian has bounded eigenvalues,
    \item memory (CoM) prevents long-horizon divergence,
          following the temporal stability constraints from
          \cite{HebbianCoM2024}.
\end{enumerate}

Under these conditions, the Banach fixed-point theorem guarantees:

\[
\exists ! \; \mathbf{z}^\star : 
\mathcal{C}(\mathbf{z}^\star) = \mathbf{z}^\star.
\]

This is the formal definition of a coherent mind state.

\section{Recursive Cognition and In-Situ Optimization}

\end{quote}

We generalize this as follows:

\[
\mathbf{z}(t+k)
= \mathcal{C}^k(\mathbf{z}(t)).
\]

The sequence:
\[

\]
converges to the fixed point $\mathbf{z}^\star$ if and only if the CLIO operator
remains contractive.

This recursion corresponds to:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{depth of thought} in LLMs \cite{Cheng2025CognitiveLV},

    \item \textbf{error-corrective reflection} in psychology \cite{Friston2017ActiveIP},
    \item \textbf{iterative scene-bound inference} in TARTAN-based models

\end{itemize}

underlying both natural and artificial cognition.

\begin{itemize}

    \item $z_2$: relational/semantic structure,
    \item $z_3$: metacognitive supervision.

Following the free-energy principle \cite{Friston2010FreeEP}, each level maintains beliefs

\[

\section{Precision Weighting as Reliability Estimation}

Precision weighting emerges from the need to determine whether errors reflect:
\begin{itemize}
    \item (i) faults in the model's predictions, or
    \item (ii) faults in the incoming data.
\end{itemize}

Consistent with the computational neuroscience literature
\cite{Feldman2004AttentionUA,Friston2009TheFP},
precision $\Lambda_L$ is the inverse variance of the estimated error at level $L$.

CLIO integrates this with affective modulation:
\[
\Pi_L(t) = \sigma(\beta A(t)) \, \Lambda_L(t).
\]

Two results follow:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item High affect suppresses all precision weights, producing risk aversion,
          freeze responses, and reduced cognitive depth.
    \item Low affect enhances the discriminative power of precision, supporting
          exploration and abstraction.
\end{enumerate}

This explains the interaction between emotion and cognition documented by Solms
\cite{Solms2021HiddenSpring} and Panksepp \cite{Panksepp1998AffectiveNS}.
