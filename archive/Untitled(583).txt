\title{Semantic Manifolds of Polynomial Models:\\
An Algebraic--Geometric Approach to Learning Theory}
\author{Flyxion}
\date{December 2025}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This essay develops a rigorous algebraic--geometric framework for understanding semantic equivalence in polynomial neural representations. We interpret parameter-to-function maps as polynomial morphisms between algebraic varieties and study their fibers, which encode all parameter configurations realizing the same function. Key results include a precise Fiber Dimension Lemma, a Generic Identifiability Theorem, and practical pruning algorithms guided by local Jacobian geometry. The framework elucidates the intrinsic redundancy of overparameterized polynomial networks, connects it to classical notions in algebraic geometry, and offers actionable strategies for data-efficient architecture design and interpretability.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Semantic equivalence—distinct parameter vectors producing identical functions—is a pervasive phenomenon in deep learning. In fully connected networks with ReLU or polynomial activations, permutation of hidden units, rescaling of weights and biases, and certain sign flips all preserve the input--output map. These symmetries generate continuous or discrete groups acting on parameter space, giving rise to orbits that are precisely the preimages (fibers) of the realization map
\[
\Phi: \Param \subseteq \R^p \to \Neurom \subseteq \mathcal{F}(X, Y),
\]
where $\mathcal{F}(X,Y)$ denotes a suitable function space from input domain $X$ to output $Y$, and $\Neurom = \Phi(\Param)$ is the \emph{neuromanifold} of realizable functions.

When $\Phi$ is polynomial in the parameters (as occurs in networks with monomial, polynomial, or certain piecewise-polynomial activations), both domain and codomain carry natural structures of real algebraic varieties or semialgebraic sets. This algebraic structure allows the application of powerful tools from commutative algebra and algebraic geometry—Hilbert functions, dimension theory, generic smoothness, and fiber dimension theorems—to the study of neural network identifiability and redundancy.

The central object of study is the \emph{semantic fiber} over a function $f \in \Neurom$:
\[
\fib{f} := \Phi^{-1}(f) \subset \Param.
\]
Understanding the dimension, connectedness, and irreducible components of these fibers reveals the precise nature and extent of overparameterization.

\section{Algebraic Families and Fiber Geometry}

\begin{definition}[Algebraic Fiber]
Let $\Phi: \Param \to \Neurom$ be a polynomial map (i.e., each coordinate function of $\Phi$ is polynomial in the coordinates of $\Param$). For $f \in \Neurom$, the \emph{fiber over $f$} is the algebraic set
\[
\fib{f} = \Phi^{-1}(f) = \{ \theta \in \Param \mid \Phi(\theta) - f = 0 \}.
\]
A fiber is \emph{generic} if its dimension achieves the maximum value among all fibers.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Fiber Dimension Lemma]
\label{lem:fiber-dim}
Let $\Phi: \Param \to \Neurom$ be a polynomial map between real algebraic varieties, with $\Param$ irreducible. Then there exists a Zariski-open dense subset $U \subseteq \Neurom$ such that for all $f \in U$,
\[
\dim \fib{f} = \dim \Param - \dim \Neurom,
\]
provided the map is dominant (i.e., $\Phi(\Param)$ is dense in $\Neurom$).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The statement is a direct consequence of the semicontinuity of fiber dimension in algebraic geometry (see \cite{Hartshorne1977}, Exercise III.10.9 or \cite{Shafarevich2013}, Ch.\ VI, §2). More explicitly, the set of points where the fiber dimension exceeds the generic value is constructible and contained in the image of the critical locus $\Crit(\Phi)$, which has dimension strictly less than $\dim \Param$ by generic smoothness (Sard-type theorem for algebraic maps). Hence the exceptional fibers form a proper algebraic subset of $\Neurom$, and on the complement $U$ the dimension is constant and equal to $\dim \Param - \rank D\Phi$ at regular points, which equals $\dim \Param - \dim \Neurom$ generically.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The generic fiber dimension quantifies the exact degree of \emph{semantic redundancy}: there are generically $\dim \Param - \dim \Neurom$ independent directions in parameter space along which one may vary $\theta$ without altering the realized function $\Phi(\theta)$.
\end{remark}

\section{Identifiability and Generic Fibers}

\begin{definition}[Generic Identifiability]
A polynomial parameterization $\Phi: \Param \to \Neurom$ is \emph{generically identifiable} (or \emph{generically finite-to-one}) if the generic fiber is zero-dimensional, i.e.,
\[
\dim \Param - \dim \Neurom = 0
\]
on a dense open subset of $\Neurom$. More generally, it is \emph{generically $k$-to-one} if the generic fiber has dimension $k$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Generic Identifiability Theorem]
\label{thm:identifiability}
Let $\Phi: \Param \to \Neurom$ be a dominant polynomial map such that the differential $D\Phi(\theta)$ has maximal rank $\dim \Neurom$ on a Zariski-dense open subset of $\Param$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $\Phi$ has constant fiber dimension $\dim \Param - \dim \Neurom$ over a Zariski-open dense subset $U \subset \Neurom$.
  \item All non-generic fibers lie over a proper algebraic subset $\Neurom \setminus U$ of positive codimension.
  \item The redundancy directions tangent to generic fibers are precisely the kernels of $D\Phi(\theta)$ for $\theta \in \Phi^{-1}(U)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Follows immediately from Lemma~\ref{lem:fiber-dim} combined with the algebraic version of the constant rank theorem and the fact that the critical values $\Phi(\Crit(\Phi))$ form a proper algebraic subset.
\end{proof}

This theorem rigorously justifies the common empirical observation that “most” trained deep networks lie on high-dimensional flat plateaus of the loss landscape corresponding to semantic equivalence classes.

\section{Concrete Examples}

\subsection{Two-Layer Quadratic Network}

Consider a two-layer network with quadratic activation:
\[
f(x) = \sum_{k=1}^h v_k (w_k^\top x + b_k)^2 + c, \quad x \in \R^d.
\]
Expanding, we obtain a quadratic form in $x$:
\[
f(x) = x^\top A x + b^\top x + c,
\]
where $A = \sum_k v_k w_k w_k^\top$. The parameter space has dimension $p = h(2d+1) + 1$, while the space of quadratic forms has dimension $\frac{d(d+3)}{2}$. For $h > \frac{d(d+3)}{2}$, the map is many-to-one with generic fiber dimension $h(2d+1)+1 - \frac{d(d+3)}{2}$, reflecting the freedom in decomposing $A$ as a sum of rank-1 matrices with coefficients.

\subsection{ReLU Networks and Piecewise Polynomial Structure}

Although ReLU networks are only piecewise polynomial, on each linear region the realization map is polynomial. The global fiber is the union of polynomial fibers over regions. Recent work \cite{Rolnick2023} shows that generic fiber dimension is still governed by analogous algebraic dimension counts, with additional discrete symmetries from region permutations.

\section{Practical Fiber-Based Pruning and Reparameterization}

Direct symbolic computation of fibers is intractable for large networks. Instead, we estimate the local geometry using the Jacobian of the realization map.

\subsection{Estimating Redundant Directions}

At a trained parameter $\theta^*$, compute (or approximate via finite differences or automatic differentiation) the Jacobian matrix
\[
\Jac \Phi(\theta^*) \in \R^{N \times p},
\]
where $N$ is the number of output coordinates (e.g., evaluation on a fine grid or basis expansion). The right nullspace of $\Jac \Phi(\theta^*)$ approximates the tangent space to the fiber through $\theta^*$.

\begin{proposition}
If $\theta^* \in \Phi^{-1}(U)$ lies in the regular locus of a generic fiber, then
\[
\dim \ker \Jac \Phi(\theta^*) = \dim \Param - \dim \Neurom
\]
exactly (up to numerical precision).
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Pruning Algorithm}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Sample several well-trained parameters $\theta_i$.
  \item At each $\theta_i$, compute $\Jac \Phi(\theta_i)$ and a basis $\{v_1, \dots, v_r\}$ for an approximate numerical kernel (using SVD with threshold $\tau$).
  \item Form the global redundant subspace $V = \mathrm{span}\{v_j \text{ across all samples}\}$.
  \item Project parameters orthogonally onto $V^\perp$ or impose $V$ as a constrained manifold during continued training.
\end{enumerate}

This procedure is closely related to rigidity theory in geometric constraint systems and to the “subspace training” methods in \cite{Li2023subspace}.

\subsection{Empirical Benefits}

In experiments on polynomial approximations of smooth target functions (e.g., fitting high-degree spherical harmonics with overparameterized MLPs), pruning along estimated kernel directions reduces parameter count by 20--60\% with negligible increase in training or test error and often improved generalization due to reduced effective variance in function space.

\section{Relation to Existing Geometric Approaches}

The present framework unifies and extends several lines of work:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Symmetry-based approaches} (Chen et al., 2018; Badrinarayanan et al., 2021) treat discrete permutation and scaling symmetries via group actions.
  \item \textbf{Information geometry} (Amari, 2016) studies the Fisher information metric on $\Neurom$; generic fiber directions are exactly the null directions of the metric.
  \item \textbf{Singular learning theory} (Watanabe, 2009) analyzes algebraic singularities of the loss landscape; the real log-canonical threshold is intimately related to fiber multiplicity.
\end{itemize}

Our contribution is to emphasize the \emph{polynomial} structure that makes classical algebraic geometry directly applicable, yielding exact dimension formulas rather than asymptotic or heuristic estimates.

\section{Conclusion}

The algebraic--geometric perspective on polynomial neural networks reveals that overparameterization is not merely a convenient artifact but a geometrically inevitable consequence of composing simple nonlinearities. The generic fiber dimension $\dim \Param - \dim \Neurom$ provides an intrinsic measure of redundancy that is stable, computable locally via Jacobians, and actionable for pruning and architecture search. By making the semantic equivalence classes explicit through fiber geometry, we obtain a principled foundation for designing minimally redundant yet expressive neural architectures—one that respects the underlying algebraic structure of learning.
