Chapter 6 — Manifold-Aligned Representations

This chapter develops the geometric theory of manifold-constrained delta–sigma prediction and quantization. The appropriate citations naturally divide among the geometry of manifolds, statistical structure, and the recent literature motivating manifold alignment in modern inference systems. The foundational mathematical references include Lee’s Introduction to Smooth Manifolds and Do Carmo’s differential geometry text for tangent bundles, curvature, and second fundamental forms. Amari’s Information Geometry should be cited for the geometry of statistical manifolds and Fisher metrics, which echo the manifold-aligned predictive structures introduced here. For extrinsic curvature and projection operators, Abraham–Marsden–Ratiu may also be valuable.

Two modern sources have directly inspired this chapter. Li & He’s 2025 paper Back to Basics: Let Denoising Generative Models Denoise motivates the viewpoint that high-dimensional signals cluster on low-dimensional manifolds, and that predictive models respect this structure. Cheng, Broadbent & Chappell’s Cognitive Loop via In-Situ Optimization supports the manifold-constrained inference interpretation.

The classical delta–sigma sources (Schreier & Temes; Candy & Temes) remain relevant because this chapter builds a geometric reinterpretation of their predictive structures. Works in nonlinear control and contraction theory, such as Lohmiller & Slotine, may also be cited if you wish to emphasize geometric stability.

References appropriate to Chapter 6:
Lee (2013); Do Carmo (1992); Abraham–Marsden–Ratiu (1988); Amari (2016); Li & He (2025); Cheng, Broadbent & Chappell (2025); Schreier & Temes (2005); Candy & Temes (1992); Lohmiller & Slotine (1998).

Chapter 7 — First-Order Delta–Sigma Modulation in the RSVP Framework

This chapter re-expresses the classical first-order delta–sigma architecture in the geometric–entropic framework established earlier. The core engineering references for first-order modulators remain Candy & Temes and Schreier & Temes, which provide the canonical linear transfer-function analysis and NTF derivation. Gray & Neuhoff’s foundational paper on quantization should be cited to justify the white-noise model and its limitations.

The dynamical-systems reinterpretation of first-order loops draws from Arnold’s exposition of Hamiltonian and dissipative flows. Willems’s dissipativity theory again plays a central role, because the stability conditions for first-order loops align closely with energy-based inequalities. If you choose to include explicit state-space stability criteria, then Khalil’s Nonlinear Systems may also be cited.

Finally, Amari’s information geometry supports the view that first-order delta–sigma feedback implements a gradient-like flow that aligns the state toward minimal free energy.

References appropriate to Chapter 7:
Candy & Temes (1992); Schreier & Temes (2005); Gray & Neuhoff (1998); Arnold (1989); Willems (1972); Khalil (2002); Amari (2016).

Chapter 8 — Higher-Order Modulators: Stability, Curvature, and Catastrophe

Higher-order delta–sigma modulators notoriously exhibit delicate stability properties that demand careful mathematical justification. The chapter’s analysis of curvature, divergence, and Lyapunov potentials should cite classical nonlinear-systems texts such as Khalil’s Nonlinear Systems, Vidyasagar’s Nonlinear Systems Analysis, and Slotine & Li’s Applied Nonlinear Control. The spectral arguments relating order to NTF shape should refer to Schreier & Temes for the well-known engineering principles.

When discussing catastrophic instability and bifurcations, Strogatz’s Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos is an essential reference, as are Guckenheimer & Holmes for bifurcation theory. The geometric interpretation of higher-order error shaping may again call upon Amari and Arnold. When discussing dissipativity or inequalities that restrict loop gain, Willems should be cited.

If the chapter references modern contraction-based stability or differential Lyapunov methods, Lohmiller & Slotine (1998) or Forni & Sepulchre (2014) may be introduced.

References appropriate to Chapter 8:
Schreier & Temes (2005); Khalil (2002); Vidyasagar (1993); Slotine & Li (1991); Strogatz (1994); Guckenheimer & Holmes (1983); Arnold (1989); Willems (1972); Amari (2016); Forni & Sepulchre (2014).

Chapter 9 — Multi-Stage, Cascaded, and MASH Architectures

This chapter interprets multi-stage delta–sigma structures as hierarchical error-correcting inference pipelines. The classical engineering references include works by Candy & Temes, as well as Norsworthy, Schreier, and Temes’s Delta–Sigma Data Converters, which covers MASH and cascaded architectures in depth. Early papers on multi-stage noise shaping, including works by Arias, Eldar, and Galton, may also be cited depending on the emphasis.

The recursive interpretation of MASH systems as cascaded inference layers ties naturally to the CLIO paper by Cheng, Broadbent & Chappell, which develops multi-scale recursive optimization. If you analyze the stability of cascaded systems, Vidyasagar and Khalil remain relevant. If you include spectral flow arguments, Oppenheim & Schafer may be referenced.

References appropriate to Chapter 9:
Norsworthy, Schreier & Temes (1996); Schreier & Temes (2005); Candy & Temes (1992); Arias et al. (various); Eldar (2003); Galton (2000); Cheng, Broadbent & Chappell (2025); Vidyasagar (1993); Khalil (2002).

Chapter 10 — Vector-Valued Quantization and Multi-Channel RSVP Dynamics

Chapter 10 treats vector-valued signals, multi-channel modulators, and multi-dimensional quantization. This area has deep connections to vector quantization, high-dimensional signal geometry, and multi-agent control. The essential references include Gersho & Gray’s Vector Quantization and Signal Compression, which provides the foundational theory. Zador’s classic results on high-dimensional quantization error rates (and their information-theoretic implications) should also be included.

When the chapter discusses multi-dimensional stability, contraction theory or standard control references may be cited (Lohmiller & Slotine; Slotine & Li). The geometric arguments again may draw from Amari and Lee. If you discuss cross-coupled modulators, Norsworthy–Schreier–Temes remains relevant.

Finally, if you include material about high-dimensional manifolds or embeddings, you may optionally cite works on Whitney embedding or Johnson–Lindenstrauss theory, although these are not mandatory.

References appropriate to Chapter 10:
Gersho & Gray (1991); Zador (1982); Norsworthy, Schreier & Temes (1996); Schreier & Temes (2005); Slotine & Li (1991); Lohmiller & Slotine (1998); Amari (2016); Lee (2013).
