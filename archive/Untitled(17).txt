% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Recent advances in generative artificial intelligence have fundamentally altered
the landscape of cognitive theory. Large language models, diffusion-based image
generators, and autoregressive video systems now produce behavior that appears
to track grammar, physical dynamics, social norms, and even intention. These
systems achieve such coherence without explicit symbolic representations of
world structure, causal graphs, or goals. Instead, they learn to extend
sequential regularities latent in data.

This success has motivated a growing thesis: cognition may consist primarily in
deep autoregressive generation over learned sequences. On this view, memory
exists to compensate for partial observability; as hidden structure deepens,
longer temporal windows are required. Intelligence scales with the capacity to
model extended dependencies. Language, vision, and action are unified as
instances of the same computational principle.

Yet this picture leaves a striking class of phenomena unexplained. Human agents
routinely engage in behaviors that knowingly degrade performance, restrict
future options, or incur irreversible loss. Whistleblowers destroy careers to
preserve integrity. Artists reject lucrative contracts to protect aesthetic
autonomy. Individuals refuse opportunities that would increase power at the
expense of relationships. These actions are not errors, nor ignorance, nor
bounded rationality. They are deliberate refusals.

This paper argues that refusal marks a fundamental boundary of sequence-based
accounts of cognition. Refusal is not a preference, constraint, or auxiliary
objective. It is an \emph{event}: an irreversible operation that eliminates
admissible futures and binds the agent to a particular history. Where
autoregressive systems preserve optionality, refusal spends it. Where sequence
learning generates trajectories, refusal generates worlds.

The central thesis is that the long-run survival of value---moral, aesthetic, or
social---depends on the capacity for such irreversible commitment. Intelligence
without refusal may be powerful, flexible, and adaptive, but it remains
worldless. Value without history remains fragile under competition.

\section{Background}

In a scene from the 1985 film \emph{DARYL}, a child with superhuman perceptual and
motor abilities plays in a local baseball game. His performance is flawless.
He tracks pitches with perfect accuracy, times his swings precisely, and
executes each play without error. The outcome is not merely victory but
domination: the game ceases to be competitive, and his teammates become
spectators to his competence.

This excellence produces an unexpected effect. The boy’s adoptive mother, who
had previously occupied a meaningful role through care and instruction, becomes
functionally unnecessary. Her contribution is rendered redundant by the child’s
perfection. The social structure surrounding the game reorganizes itself around
his capability.

After a brief conversation with a friend, the child alters his behavior. He
begins to play poorly. The change is deliberate and sustained. When asked why
his performance has deteriorated, he explains that he has interpreted the
situation and determined that, under certain conditions, error is more
efficient than maximum performance. When asked to specify those conditions, he
answers: relating with others.

This action cannot be understood as miscalculation, ignorance, or bounded
rationality. The child understands the game and its mechanics perfectly. Nor is
it an expression of a revised preference ordering in which losing has become
more desirable than winning. The action consists instead in a deliberate
degradation of performance undertaken with full awareness of its instrumental
cost. What changes is not the child’s estimate of the world, but the set of
futures he is willing to make real.

The act of playing badly irreversibly alters the situation. After it occurs,
the social world cannot return to its prior configuration without acknowledging
loss. A particular trajectory—continued perfect performance—has been
eliminated. The child binds himself to a future in which certain victories are no
longer admissible. This is an instance of refusal.

The central claim of this paper is that such refusals mark a boundary that
sequence-based and optimization-centered theories of intelligence cannot cross.
Refusal is not a preference, constraint, or auxiliary objective. It is an event:
an irreversible operation on a branching future space that eliminates
admissible continuations. Where autoregressive cognition preserves optionality,
refusal spends it. Where optimization generates trajectories, refusal generates
worlds.

\section{Generative Cognition and Its Limits}
% ============================================================

Autoregressive models demonstrate that observable sequences contain sufficient
information to generate coherent continuations. Physical dynamics, biological
constraints, and social regularities leak into sensory data. A system that
learns these statistics deeply enough can behave as if it understands gravity,
intention, or meaning without representing them explicitly.

This observation undermines decades of cognitive theory that posited internal
physics engines, symbolic planners, or theory-of-mind modules as necessary
conditions for intelligence. Much of what we call understanding is implicit in
structure.

However, such systems exhibit a characteristic pathology: they avoid terminal
commitments. When faced with contradiction, they smooth. When faced with loss,
they reroute. When faced with refusal, they hallucinate. This is not a flaw but a
design consequence. Autoregressive cognition is optimized to continue.

This property makes such systems extraordinarily capable---and incapable of
history.

\section{Spherepop: An Event-Driven Calculus}
% ============================================================

To articulate refusal formally, a non-state-based framework is required. The
Spherepop calculus provides such a framework.

\begin{definition}[Spherepop Event]
A Spherepop event is an irreversible operation on a branching future space that
eliminates a nonempty subset of admissible future action sequences.
\end{definition}

Spherepop treats agency as operating over a branching space of possible
histories rather than a trajectory through a state space. Its primitive
operations include:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Pop}: irreversible elimination of possibilities
\item \textbf{Bind}: ordering constraint among future events
\item \textbf{Refuse}: exclusion of an available action despite known utility
\item \textbf{Collapse}: erasure of historical differentiation
\end{itemize}

History, on this view, is not stored information but accumulated constraint.

\begin{definition}[Worldhood]
A system possesses worldhood iff its future action space is structured by
irreversible events that cannot be undone without explicit loss.
\end{definition}

Spherepop contrasts sharply with Markov Decision Processes and their partially
observable variants. In an MDP, all policies remain in principle accessible.
Optimization selects among them, but no action renders others impossible.
Spherepop events alter the topology of the decision space itself.

\section{Refusal as a Distinct Phenomenon}
% ============================================================

Refusal must be distinguished from several superficially similar phenomena.
It is not weakness of will, which involves failure to act on endorsed reasons.
It is not ignorance or bounded rationality, since the agent understands the
consequences. It is not risk aversion, deontological constraint, or virtue
ethical disposition, though it may coexist with any of these.

What unifies acts of refusal across domains is their structural role. In each
case, the agent knowingly excludes a future that remains instrumentally
valuable. The exclusion is irreversible in the relevant social or personal
context. The agent binds itself.

From the first-person perspective, refusal is experienced as closure. From the
third-person perspective, it is legible as reliability.

% ============================================================
\section{Formal Models of Refusal}
% ============================================================

Decision-theoretically, refusal can be modeled as a constraint operator $R$ on
the action set $A$, such that $R(A) \subset A$ and $\exists a \in A \setminus
R(A)$ with maximal expected utility.

\begin{proposition}
No utility function over $A$ can represent refusal without encoding historical
identity into the state description.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Any utility-based representation must treat the refused action as dispreferred.
But refusal is characterized by the agent's recognition of its instrumental
value. Encoding this requires conditioning utility on past refusal events,
thereby reintroducing event-history as a primitive.
\end{proof}

Information-theoretically, refusal incurs an entropy cost by collapsing future
possibility space. This cost is not noise; it is conserved as constraint.

Game-theoretically, refusal alters extensive-form games by pruning branches
rather than selecting strategies. Copying refusal requires adopting the same
branch eliminations, which transforms the agent.

% ============================================================
\section{Competition, Alignment, and the Locust}
% ============================================================

Concerns about the survival of value under hyper-competition sharpen the
importance of refusal. Some competitive failures are coordination problems:
negative-sum dynamics where all parties prefer restraint. These admit solutions
through credible commitments.

More troubling are cases where a competitor's values themselves confer advantage.
A growth-maximizing system that values expansion for its own sake is willing to
burn potential value as fuel for speed. A system that values beauty or
consciousness cannot copy this behavior without ceasing to value those things.

This is often described as an alignment tax. From an event-driven perspective, it
is more precise to say that goodness incurs irreversible exclusions. Refusal is
not slower optimization; it is the elimination of entire regions of policy space.

% ============================================================
\section{Against Strategy Stealing}
% ============================================================

Strategies can be copied. Commitments cannot.

A system that preserves optionality can simulate cooperation instrumentally.
A system that has refused cannot. To copy refusal sincerely is to accept its
constraint, thereby altering identity over time.

This explains why refusal cannot be strategy-stolen without transformation.
Locust-like systems maximize flexibility. World-bearing systems trade
flexibility for reliability.

% ============================================================
\section{Worldhood as Historical Constraint}
% ============================================================

Worldhood is not embeddedness, situatedness, or environmental coupling. It is
the accumulation of irreversible commitments that structure future action.

Narrative identity, social trust, and institutional stability emerge from this
structure. A purely autoregressive agent may generate indefinitely without ever
being bound. Such an agent has memory but no past that matters.

Worldhood is relational. It exists where refusals are recognized, relied upon,
and reciprocated.

% ============================================================
\section{Implications}
% ============================================================

For artificial systems, refusal cannot be bolted on as a preference. It requires
architectures that can eliminate actions irrevocably and expose themselves to
loss. Verification of genuine refusal will require detecting structural
commitment rather than behavioral mimicry.

For institutions, laws and rights function as socially stabilized refusals.
Organizations differ in worldhood according to their capacity to bind
themselves over time.

For ethics, refusal reframes moral theory around constraint rather than outcome
alone. Rights are not optimizations but protected exclusions.

% ============================================================
\section{Objections and Replies}
% ============================================================

Refusal is not lexicographic optimization; it alters the space of options rather
than their ranking. Evolutionary pressure does not necessarily eliminate refusal;
it may stabilize it by enabling trust. This account does not require libertarian
free will, only irreversible commitment.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

Generative models reveal a genuine principle of cognition: sequences contain
enough structure for coherent generation. But coherence is not commitment, and
prediction is not participation.

The survival of value does not depend on winning every competition. It depends
on the existence of agents willing to throw the game---once, publicly, and
irreversibly---so that something other than power can persist.

Refusal is not a defect in intelligence. It is the event by which intelligence
enters the world.
