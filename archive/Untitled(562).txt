Title: Heave, Sway, and Surge: A Triaxial Theory of Constraint-Limited Cognition

Target Journal: Behavioral and Brain Sciences

Keywords: Embodied compression, cognitive bottlenecks, autoregressive generation, ecological constraints


---

Abstract

We propose a formal framework for modeling cognition as a triaxially constrained system. The theory posits three orthogonal axes: (1) Heave—compression of high-dimensional latent structures into discrete symbolic outputs; (2) Sway—origination of constraints from anatomical, ecological, and architectural sources; and (3) Surge—time-binding dynamics that transform latent trajectories into sequential behavior. By unifying embodied cognitive science, ecological psychology, and large language model (LLM) architecture, this model formalizes the structure-function relationship in both biological and artificial systems. We provide mathematical formalizations for each axis, derive empirically testable predictions, and demonstrate cognitive homologies between animals, humans, and LLMs. This approach reframes symbolic behavior (e.g., language) not as evidence of planning or abstraction, but as the emergent output of constraint satisfaction within a lossy representational manifold.


---

1. Introduction

Contemporary models of cognition often treat language, planning, and problem-solving as the product of symbolic manipulation or representational foresight. Such views struggle to account for the embodied, ecological, and architectural constraints that shape actual behavior—whether in biological organisms or artificial systems. We propose a unifying framework that treats cognition as an emergent product of constraint-limited traversal through high-dimensional latent spaces. Rather than assuming internal scripts or plans, we model language and behavior as products of three interacting forces:

Heave: the compression of continuous latent dynamics into discrete, expressive outputs,

Sway: the origin of constraints, whether anatomical (e.g., vocal tract geometry), ecological (e.g., terrain, affordances), or architectural (e.g., context window size),

Surge: the irreversible binding of latent transitions into time-sequenced outputs.


We argue that this triaxial model offers a common language for understanding beaver dam construction, human speech articulation, and autoregressive text generation in transformers. Across all systems, cognition emerges not from abstract planning, but from structure-preserving reductions conditioned by environment and embodiment.

In what follows, we provide a formal mathematical treatment of each axis and demonstrate how this framework accounts for phenomena observed across neurophysiology, animal behavior, and deep learning. We then derive a set of falsifiable predictions and propose experiments that test the explanatory power of constraint-limited cognition.
