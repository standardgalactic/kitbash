\title{Pipsqueak as a Markov Chain:\\
Phonological Continuation Without Semantic State}
\author{Flyxion}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This paper analyzes a series of recorded utterances produced intentionally without semantic intent but under conversational pressure. The resulting vocalizations, referred to as \emph{Pipsqueak}, exhibit strong internal regularities in phonology, cadence, and discourse timing while lacking stable referents or compositional meaning. I argue that these utterances are best modeled not as an emergent language but as realizations of a first-order Markov chain operating over English-shaped discourse states. Evidence is drawn from turn-taking behavior, length escalation, authority loops, failure under grounding challenges, and termination leakage. The analysis supports the broader claim that fluent speech-like output can arise from probabilistic continuation alone, without semantic memory or symbolic representation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Recent interest in generative language systems has renewed classical questions about the relationship between fluency, meaning, and cognition. It is often assumed that structured linguistic output implies the presence of internal semantic representations or symbolic manipulation. This paper challenges that assumption using a deliberately constrained experiment: the production of rapid, non-semantic vocalizations under conversational prompts.

The resulting utterances, collectively referred to as \emph{Pipsqueak}, sound language-like to English speakers. They exhibit rhythm, repetition, escalation, and closure. However, they are intentionally produced without referential intent. This makes them a useful artifact for isolating the contribution of probabilistic continuation to perceived linguistic structure.

The central claim of this paper is that Pipsqueak is not a language but a Markov process over discourse phonology. What is preserved is not meaning, but the statistical shape of English conversational roles.

\section{Experimental Setup}

The experiment consists of a scripted sequence of spoken English prompts designed to elicit responses without requiring correctness or grounding. The participant responds in a rapid, high-pitched, non-semantic vocal mode. Importantly, responses are not planned in advance; they are generated on the fly, guided only by sound, cadence, and conversational momentum.

Prompts include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item initiation cues (``I’m going to ask you a question''),
  \item explanation requests (``Please explain how it works''),
  \item elaboration prompts (``Go into more detail''),
  \item grounding challenges (``Is it a clump or a vein?''),
  \item and explicit termination (``That’s enough'').
\end{itemize}

All responses are recorded and transcribed phonetically. No attempt is made to assign meanings to syllables.

\section{Observed Regularities}

Despite the absence of semantics, the Pipsqueak utterances exhibit several robust regularities:

\subsection{Turn-Entry Prefixes}

A majority of responses begin with phonological clusters such as \emph{Ik-}, \emph{Ek-}, or \emph{Ip-}. These function as high-probability turn-entry markers analogous to English discourse starters like ``okay,'' ``so,'' or ``well.'' Crucially, these prefixes appear across all prompt types, indicating that they encode turn-taking rather than meaning.

\subsection{Length as a Function of Prompt State}

Response length correlates strongly with the type of preceding prompt. Requests for explanation or elaboration reliably produce longer utterances, while grounding challenges and termination attempts produce shorter ones. This indicates that utterance length is controlled by the current discourse state rather than internal content.

\subsection{Cadence Closure Tokens}

Many responses terminate with sharp phonological endings such as \emph{-up}, \emph{-o}, or \emph{-o-yo}. These function as prosodic full stops. The same closure tokens appear in explanations, answers, contradictions, and nonsense alike, demonstrating that they serve as phonological punctuation rather than semantic markers.

\section{Failure Under Grounding}

The most diagnostic moments occur when the speaker introduces grounding challenges or binary distinctions, such as ``Is it a clump or a vein?'' In natural language, such prompts force branching or clarification. In Pipsqueak, no such branching occurs. Instead, the response smooths over the distinction with a rhythmically averaged utterance.

This behavior is incompatible with symbolic reasoning but entirely consistent with a Markov process: when multiple next states are available and no memory exists to disambiguate, the chain produces an averaged continuation rather than a choice.

\section{Error Without State Change}

Some responses include phonological patterns resembling error signaling. However, these do not alter subsequent behavior. The system does not reduce confidence, shorten output, or terminate. Error appears as a stylistic feature, not as a control signal.

In Markov terms, error tokens do not correspond to absorbing or redirecting states. The transition probabilities remain unchanged.

\section{Termination Asymmetry}

When the speaker issues an explicit stop command (``That’s enough''), Pipsqueak often produces one final brief utterance before stopping. This \emph{termination leakage} indicates that silence is not an internally generated state. Termination must be imposed externally.

This asymmetry mirrors behavior observed in generative systems optimized for continuation: stopping is not a learned outcome but an interruption.

\section{Markov Interpretation}

Taken together, these observations support a first-order Markov model in which:
\begin{itemize}
  \item states correspond to discourse roles (initiation, explanation, authority, procedure, challenge, closure),
  \item transitions are driven by the immediately preceding prompt,
  \item and outputs are sampled from phonological distributions associated with each state.
\end{itemize}

No internal semantic variables are required. Apparent structure arises from stable transition probabilities between discourse-shaped phonological states.

\section{Discussion}

Pipsqueak demonstrates how easily English speakers attribute meaning, intention, and even authority to outputs that merely preserve the statistical surface of discourse. This has implications beyond the present experiment. It suggests that fluency alone is insufficient evidence for semantic grounding or agency.

More broadly, it illustrates how conversational structure can emerge from probabilistic continuation without memory, reference, or commitment.

\section{Conclusion}

The Pipsqueak utterances are best understood not as an invented language but as the audible trace of a Markov chain operating over English discourse phonology. Meaning appears locally persuasive but globally absent. What is preserved is not truth or reference, but interactional shape.

This distinction matters. Systems that speak fluently without semantic state can explain, reassure, and assert without understanding. Recognizing the Markovian nature of such output is therefore essential for correctly interpreting both artificial and human-like speech behaviors.
