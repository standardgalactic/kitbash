VISIONS OF A SPIRIT SEER

                                Written by
                              Nate Guimond

                              (feature film)



                           [DRAFT — FOR REVIEW]

FADE IN:


EXT. NIGHT SKY – STARS – 18TH CENTURY EUROPE

A cold scatter of STARS over a black void. No music yet. Just WIND.

One by one, faint LINES of light connect a few stars, sketching the
beginnings of a GEOMETRIC FIGURE.

The lines tremble... then SNAP OUT.

Silence.


INT. KÖNIGSBERG UNIVERSITY – OBSERVATORY – NIGHT

A cramped, unheated ROOM under a domed roof. Shelves of ASTRONOMICAL
TABLES. A brass TELESCOPE aimed at a small frosted window.

By candlelight, IMMANUEL KANT (early 40s, thin, precise, a touch
overwound) sits at a wooden table, surrounded by open BOOKS and
CHARTS.

His QUILL scratches, steady and even.

CLOSE ON the table:

– STAR MAPS with neat annotations.
– LOG TABLES of angles and positions.
– A HAND-DRAWN DIAGRAM: circles, axes, and an odd, angular SIGIL that
  doesn’t quite belong.

Kant turns a page in an astronomical ephemeris. Pauses.

His eyes narrow.

ON THE PAGE — in the printed tables, someone has penciled a tiny
GEOMETRIC MARK, the same sigil, tucked between columns of numbers.

He frowns.

                      KANT
          (barely audible)
          Who has been at this...?

He checks the margin. No handwriting. No sign of erasure.

He flips to another sheet of calculations — a sheet he KNOWS he wrote
himself.

The sigil appears there too. Exactly the same proportions. Exactly the
same angle.

He glances to a third document: a chart of eclipses. The figure lurks
in the corner, as if it had always been there.

The CANDLE FLAME gutters in a draft.

Kant’s jaw tightens. He sets the quill down with care.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          Fatigue. Nothing more.

He takes off his SPECTACLES, rubs the bridge of his nose.

When he looks up, the sigil on the nearest page is GONE.

The corner of the page is empty. Just blank paper and a faint smudge
where lines might have been.

Kant stares.

He slowly looks back to the ephemeris.

The penciled sigil between the numbers is still there.

IN THE B.G., we hear the distant, hollow TICKING of a TOWN CLOCK.


FLASHBACK:


EXT. TOWN SQUARE – KONIGSBERG – DAY (YEARS EARLIER)

A YOUNG KANT (12), in worn but neat clothes, stands under a large
CLOCK TOWER during a STORM.

Rain lashes the square. The sky is black.

The CLOCK HANDS on the tower JERK — then STOP — then lurch forward in
a stuttering motion.

TICK — silence — TICKTICK — silence.

Young Kant stares, unnerved.

He clutches a small WOODEN MODEL CLOCK in his hands, its gears
exposed.

Lightning flashes; for a split-second, the shadow of the clock tower
seems to lag BEHIND the structure, like an echo trying to catch up.

Young Kant squeezes his eyes shut.

CUT TO:

INT. KANT FAMILY HOME – NIGHT (FLASHBACK)

Young Kant at a small table, fixing the MODEL CLOCK by candlelight.

He aligns the gears, adjusts the tension, cranks the mechanism.

The model TICKS with perfect regularity.

Young Kant exhales, a small, private relief.


BACK TO:


INT. KÖNIGSBERG UNIVERSITY – OBSERVATORY – NIGHT

The distant town clock resumes its regular rhythm.

Kant’s expression settles into the familiar mask of control.

He reaches for the ephemeris, takes a graphite stick, and very
deliberately CROSSES OUT the sigil between the numbers.

A small, rational victory.

He flips to a fresh sheet of paper and begins to copy a table, his
hand precise.

As he writes, the CAMERA DRIFTS slowly over his shoulder toward one of
the STAR CHARTS pinned to the wall.

On the chart: a neat grid of constellations.

Between two constellations, faint and almost invisible, the same
GEOMETRIC FIGURE is sketched in a paler ink, as if it had always been
part of the sky.


EXT. UNIVERSITY COURTYARD – DAWN

Grey light. Frost clings to cobblestones.

Kant crosses the courtyard, cloak tight, carrying his leather
portfolio. His breath fogs in the morning air.

He walks with a measured, habitual rhythm, steps almost like
metronome beats.

As he passes the central WELL, we drop to his feet:

A JAGGED CRACK in the stone path forms a crude version of the sigil.

He steps right OVER it without seeing it.

He reaches the gate, pauses as a distant BELL tolls.

The sound is steady. Ordinary.

Kant adjusts his gloves, and exits.


INT. COFFEEHOUSE – DAY

A low-ceilinged, smoky room. Merchants, STUDENTS, and TRAVELERS talk
over coffee and beer. Maps, pamphlets, and broadsheets litter tables.

Kant sits at a small corner table, alone, a cup of coffee untouched in
front of him. He reads a pamphlet on recent scientific findings.

Two TRAVELERS (40s, road-worn) sit nearby, mid-conversation. Their
voices cut through the murmur.

                      FIRST TRAVELER (O.S.)
          ...told us the house would burn before the night
          was over. Described the flames — the very window
          they would break.

Kant’s eyes flick up, just for a moment.

                      SECOND TRAVELER (O.S.)
          Anyone can guess a fire.

                      FIRST TRAVELER (O.S.)
          He named the STREET. While he was three hundred
          miles away.

Kant returns pointedly to his pamphlet, but his attention is gone.

He folds the page to sharpen his focus, but the words blur.

                      SECOND TRAVELER (O.S.)
          You saw him in person?

                      FIRST TRAVELER (O.S.)
          Swedenborg. Yes. Quiet as a clerk. Not a madman.
          Spoke of the dead like he was describing
          furniture.

Kant’s hand stills on the paper.

He looks up. Watches them now, openly.

                      SECOND TRAVELER
          Spirits and visions. Old tales.

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          Old tales don’t name the pattern of beams in a
          burning ceiling. He described it. After, we
          dug through the ash. It matched.

The second traveler snorts, unconvinced, but uneasy.

                      SECOND TRAVELER
          Perhaps he set the fire himself.

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          From Stockholm?

A few other PATRONS glance over, drawn to the story.

Kant’s gaze drifts to a BROADSHEET nailed to a wooden post near the
counter:

AN ILLUSTRATION of a burning HOUSE. In the rising smoke, the negative
space between the curls of ink almost forms the sigil.

Almost.

He blinks. The impression is gone.

Kant stands, crosses to the travelers’ table.

                      KANT
          Pardon the intrusion, gentlemen.

They look up, surprised. One recognizes him.

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          Professor Kant.

                      KANT
          You spoke of Emanuel Swedenborg. You met him?

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          Aye. In Stockholm.

                      KANT
          And you say he described a fire as it occurred?

The first traveler shifts, uncomfortable under Kant’s precise gaze.

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          We were at dinner. He turned pale. Said my
          neighbor’s house was burning — three streets
          over. Said the wind would carry it to the
          stables, but no further.

                      SECOND TRAVELER
          An hour later, the alarm. Just as he’d said.

A ripple runs through the coffeehouse. Someone mutters a prayer.

Kant weighs the story, searching for the familiar cracks — coincidence,
embellishment, superstition.

                      KANT
          You are certain you recall his words correctly?

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          I wagered half a month’s pay that he was mad. I
          recall every word.

A beat.

                      KANT
          Did he offer an explanation?

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          Said the spirits had shown him. That they walk
          among us, in ordered societies. Cities of light
          above and below.

Swedenborg’s phrase lingers.

Kant glances back at the broadsheet illustration. The curls of smoke
are just curls of smoke.

                      KANT
          (dry)
          Spirits have little interest in architecture.

A small chuckle from nearby tables. The tension loosens.

The first traveler gives a half-smile.

                      FIRST TRAVELER
          With respect, sir, I’m more troubled that he was
          right than by why he claimed he was.

Kant studies him. This is not the look of a credulous fool. It’s the
look of a man who wishes he could disbelieve what he saw.

                      KANT
          And these... other reports? Conversations with
          the dead?

                      SECOND TRAVELER
          They say he speaks with a queen. One who died
          years ago. Knows things only she could know.

The room goes quiet at that.

Kant’s expression hardens — a defensive calm.

                      KANT
          You have my thanks for the account.

He inclines his head, returns to his table.

He takes a sip of cold coffee, face unreadable.

Then he reaches into his portfolio, pulls out a folded LETTER. The
seal already broken.

INSERT – THE LETTER

A neat hand. No signature.

          “There is a man who sees what should not be seen.
           He speaks of other worlds as if he had walked
           their streets.

           You have devoted yourself to the limits of human
           reason. He claims to have crossed them.

           I suggest you meet.”

At the bottom, in faint ink, someone has drawn a small GEOMETRIC
FIGURE. The same sigil.

BACK TO SCENE

Kant’s thumb rests on the figure. He presses his nail into the paper,
as if he could erase it by force.

He folds the letter precisely, tucks it back.

Across the room, a SERVER carries a tray of cups. As she passes a
window, the REFLECTION in the glass shows her walking a half-beat
out of sync with her actual motion.

It’s so subtle it feels like a trick of the light.

Kant doesn’t see it. He stares at nothing, thinking.


INT. KANT’S STUDY – EVENING

Bookshelves, neat stacks of paper, a carefully organized DESK.

The faint light of late afternoon filters through a small window.

Kant stands at a lectern, reading from a draft manuscript. He murmurs
phrases, testing their weight.

                      KANT
          ...thus we see that experience is bounded by the
          forms of sensibility and the pure concepts of
          the understanding...

He pauses, dissatisfied.

He crosses out a line, rewrites.

A light knock at the door.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          Yes?

The door opens. A STUDENT (early 20s, serious) peeks in.

                      STUDENT
          Professor, forgive me. There is a visitor asking
          for you.

                      KANT
          I am not receiving visitors this evening.

                      STUDENT
          He was... insistent, sir. He said you would wish
          to speak with him.

                      KANT
          Did he give a name?

The student hesitates.

                      STUDENT
          Emanuel Swedenborg.

The name hangs in the air.

A tiny, almost inaudible shift in the ambient room tone — as if the
silence itself tightened.

Kant’s hand stills over the manuscript. He looks up slowly.

                      KANT
          (measured)
          Show him in.

The student nods, disappears.

Kant moves a stack of papers, straightens a chair, adjusts his cuffs.
Tiny rituals of order.

From the hallway, the muffled sound of approaching FOOTSTEPS.

As the footsteps reach the threshold, the candle flame on Kant’s desk
flickers sideways — not from any apparent draft, but as if for a
moment gravity had turned.

The door opens.

EMANUEL SWEDENBORG (late 50s, reserved, with an unsettling calm)
stands there, hat in hand. His eyes are bright, almost feverishly
attentive, but his manner is controlled.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Professor Kant. Thank you for receiving me.

Kant studies him, the barest trace of curiosity beneath his formal
composure.

                      KANT
          Mister Swedenborg. Please, come in.

Swedenborg steps inside. The door closes with a soft click.

For a fraction of a second, their SHADOWS on the far wall lag behind
their bodies by a heartbeat — then catch up.

They do not notice.

                                                          CUT TO:

INT. KANT’S STUDY – EVENING

Swedenborg removes his gloves, places them neatly on the edge of
Kant’s desk — the gesture strangely deliberate, as though mimicking
Kant’s own precision.

Kant gestures toward a chair.

                      KANT
          Please. Be seated.

Swedenborg sits. Kant remains standing for a moment, studying him with
the quiet scrutiny of a man assessing an equation.

Then Kant sits opposite him.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          Your reputation precedes you, Mister Swedenborg.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Not always to my benefit.

Swedenborg’s voice is calm, slightly worn, but with an undercurrent —
a subtle vibration — of someone who has seen into something he
shouldn’t have.

                      KANT
          I am told you made certain... predictions.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (nodding)
          I am shown many things. I report what I see.

Kant absorbs this without reaction.

                      KANT
          You understand, I hope, that extraordinary claims
          require extraordinary demonstration.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (soft, almost amused)
          You would prefer I speak in the language of your
          science. I will try.

Swedenborg reaches into his coat and withdraws a FOLDED SHEET OF
PAPER. He places it gently on Kant’s desk.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          This arrived in your home three days ago. You
          have not yet shown it to anyone.

Kant’s composure cracks — just slightly.

He slowly unfolds the paper.

INSERT — THE PAPER  
It is the exact letter from the coffeehouse scene. Sigil included.

Kant looks up, troubled.

                      KANT
          How did you come by this?

                      SWEDENBORG
          (quietly)
          It was shown to me.

Kant’s voice sharpens.

                      KANT
          By whom?

Swedenborg hesitates. Not out of uncertainty — out of a search for
language.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Not by the dead, as the stories say. They see
          nothing more than we do.

                      KANT
          Then by what?

Swedenborg meets his eyes.

                      SWEDENBORG
          By the structures that hold the world in place.

A beat of silence — too long.

Kant folds the paper carefully, hands steady.

                      KANT
          Mister Swedenborg, I deal in forms of intuition
          and in the categories of the understanding. I
          have little patience for metaphors dressed as
          revelations.

                      SWEDENBORG
          They are neither metaphors nor revelations. They
          are errors. Misalignments in the manifold.
          Places where the weave is too thin.

Kant’s attention sharpens.

                      KANT
          Manifold?

                      SWEDENBORG
          Yes. The framework in which experience occurs.
          That which sorts the world into before and after,
          above and below, within and without.

Kant’s jaw tightens. These are his own ideas — half-formed, unwritten,
still coagulating.

                      KANT
          You describe structures no one can see.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (gently)
          No one should.

A long silence.

Then, Swedenborg speaks with quiet precision:

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          You have seen it too.

Kant stiffens.

                      KANT
          I have seen nothing of the sort.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (leaning in)
          The sign. The figure. In your tables, your
          charts, your observations. It returns where it
          should not. That is how it begins.

Kant’s breath catches; only his training keeps his face composed.

                      KANT
          Hypothetically... if such a pattern existed... it
          could only be the product of error.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Indeed. The world errs. The great clock falters.
          You have heard it stutter before.

FLASH — in Kant’s mind, the CLOCK TOWER stop-starting in the storm.

Kant suppresses the reaction.

                      KANT
          You presume much from coincidence.

Swedenborg’s tone is almost compassionate.

                      SWEDENBORG
          You believe that reason is a fortress. I believed
          the same. It is, instead, a scaffolding. A narrow
          walkway built over an abyss.

Kant’s eyes flick toward his manuscript on the lectern.

                      KANT
          Reason is the only means by which chaos becomes
          order.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (with genuine sadness)
          That is what the abyss wants you to think.

A subtle sound resonates — not a noise, exactly — more like a drop in
air pressure, or the room’s silence tightening another notch.

Kant rises abruptly, needing motion.

                      KANT
          Enough. You claim knowledge of things no human
          being can know. If you wish my intellectual
          engagement, provide evidence. Not mysticism.

Swedenborg watches him with the calm of a man who has crossed too many
thresholds.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Very well.

He reaches into his coat. For a moment Kant tenses — then Swedenborg
withdraws a small POCKET JOURNAL.

He opens it to a blank page.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          Ask a question.

                      KANT
          What kind of question?

                      SWEDENBORG
          One whose answer you believe only you possess.

Kant hesitates. Then:

                      KANT
          What is the angle between Jupiter and the fixed
          star Aldebaran at this hour?

Swedenborg smiles faintly — as if relieved Kant chose something so
benign.

He sets the journal down.

The blank page begins to show faint MARKS. As if drawn by an invisible
hand.

The marks resolve into Kant’s OWN HANDWRITING, perfect in form.

The answer is correct.

Kant goes pale.

                      KANT
          You did that with sleight of hand.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (shaking his head)
          With structure.

Kant approaches the table, trembling with restrained fury.

                      KANT
          How?

Swedenborg touches the paper. The sigil — the same geometric figure —
unfurls on the page, overlaying the calculations with a ghostly glow.

                      SWEDENBORG
          The world is speaking. It speaks through patterns,
          through misalignments, through you and through me.
          And now—

A SHIFT — a faint trembling of the walls, like a room under tension.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          —it is watching.

The CANDLE on the desk elongates its flame unnaturally, stretching as
if drawn toward an unseen center.

Kant’s SHADOW on the wall stretches a half-second behind his movement.

Kant stumbles back, face tight with controlled terror.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          They have noticed your mind.

The trembling stops.

The candle snaps back to normal.

Silence reclaims the room with surgical precision.

Swedenborg closes the journal.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          I did not come here for myself, Professor. I came
          because the structures are shifting. They are not
          stable. And you—

He stands.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          —you are the one who will try to hold them
          together.

Swedenborg puts on his gloves, lifts his hat.

                      KANT
          (voice low, shaken)
          I have not agreed to anything.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (soft)
          You were chosen long before you agreed.

He moves to the door.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          I will return when the next breach appears.

Then he opens the door.

For an instant — only an instant — the hallway beyond is filled with
an impossible geometry, angles that do not sum to what they should.
Then the hallway is ordinary again.

Swedenborg steps out. The door closes.

Kant stands alone in the stillness of his study, breath shallow.

On the desk, the sigil glows faintly — then fades, leaving only the
blank page.

Kant’s eyes fix on it.

He sits slowly, stunned, unable to speak.

                                                          CUT TO:

EXT. KÖNIGSBERG STREETS – NIGHT

Wind sweeps through narrow stone alleys. Lanterns flicker in their
glass housings.

Kant walks briskly, cloak wrapped tight, boots striking a rapid tempo
on wet cobblestones. His breath comes fast — too fast for the cold
alone.

He turns a corner—

And stops.

Ahead, a WOMAN drops an APPLE from her basket. Kant watches it fall.

The apple HITS the ground — a dull thud.

Then—

It FALLS AGAIN.  
The second impact is identical. Sound, motion, everything, perfectly
repeated.

The woman does not react. She continues gathering her belongings.

Kant’s breath freezes in his chest.

He steps forward. Slowly. His eyes wide.

The apple lies on the stone in a single, ordinary position.

Kant kneels. Touches it.

It’s real. Cool to the touch. Only one apple, not two.

He straightens, shaken.

A faint CLOCK TICKING echoes down the street — irregular, stuttering.


INT. KANT’S BEDROOM – LATE NIGHT

Kant sits on the edge of his bed, half-dressed, staring into the
darkness. The room is still.

His breathing steadies. His rational mind reaching — grasping —
reasserting itself.

                      KANT (V.O.)
          It is the mind that errs. Not the world. A
          misperception. A lapse of judgment. A trick of
          the senses. Nothing more.

His eyes shift toward the window.

The SHADOW of his chair is cast on the floor — but skewed at an
impossible angle, as if lit from a direction no lamp occupies.

Kant blinks.

The shadow snaps into its proper place.

He exhales shakily.

                      KANT (V.O.)
          (slower)
          Nothing... more.

He lies down, pulling the blanket tight, eyes wide open.

The clock on his small bedside table TICKS.

TICK. TICK.  
Then an EXTRA tick — too close together — like a stutter.

Kant turns away, forcing himself to ignore it.


EXT. TOWN SQUARE – MORNING

Bright dawn light. Merchants set up stalls. There is a faint hum of
normalcy.

Kant strides across the square, jaw set, determined to restore order
by sheer force of will.

He stops at a coffeehouse window. His reflection stares back.

For a moment, the reflection BLINKS a fraction of a second *after* he
does.

Kant jerks back.

A passerby glances at him oddly.

Kant forces his expression neutral. He moves on briskly.


INT. UNIVERSITY LECTURE HALL – MORNING

A spacious hall with wooden benches and chalkboards. Students chatter
as they settle.

Kant enters, composed but pale. He sets his notes on the lectern and
picks up a piece of chalk.

                      KANT
          Today, we shall consider the manner in which
          sensibility and understanding cooperate to
          produce experience.

He turns to the board and begins to draw: neat axes, a clear diagram.

The STUDENTS quiet.

As Kant draws a line, the chalk in his hand RESISTS — then DIVERTS,
pulling into a curve he did not intend.

Kant freezes.

On the board, the curve intersects the diagram at improbable angles,
forming a fragment of the SIGIL.

The students murmur.

                      STUDENT #1
          Sir...?

Kant drops the chalk. It CLATTERS loudly.

He forces himself to pick it up, hands trembling.

                      KANT
          (strained)
          Forgive me. A moment of distraction.

He wipes part of the board, but the sigil fragment remains faintly
etched underneath, like a watermark in the slate.

The students exchange worried glances.

Kant draws a new line.

The chalk behaves correctly this time.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          (more firmly)
          As I was saying: intuition without concepts is
          blind. Concepts without intuition are empty.

His voice steadies. Rationality reasserts itself.

But in the shadowed corner of the room — unseen — the diagram he
erased SLOWLY RETURNS on the board, line by line, as if rewritten by a
hand that does not exist.


EXT. UNIVERSITY COURTYARD – DAY

Classes have let out. Students scatter.

Kant exits the building, shaken. He clutches his lecture notes far too
tightly.

A faint breeze ripples his coat.

He hears the irregular CLOCK TICK again — faint, but present.

He looks up toward the distant tower.

The clock hands move steadily. Perfectly.

He closes his eyes, steadies himself — when a voice behind him:

                      SWEDENBORG (O.S.)
          You saw it, did you not?

Kant’s eyes open. He turns.

Swedenborg stands beneath an archway, half in shadow.

                      KANT
          (cold)
          You have no business here.

                      SWEDENBORG
          That is what I told myself, once.

Kant approaches, trying to regain dominance.

                      KANT
          I am not a superstitious man. I am a philosopher
          of reason. My work does not bend to visions or
          omens.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Nor mine. But the world does not ask whether we
          wish to perceive its deeper layers.

Kant stiffens.

                      KANT
          There are no “layers,” Mister Swedenborg.
          Experience has a structure. It is stable.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (gently)
          Is it?

They stand face to face.

A pause.

Then Swedenborg reaches into his coat.

Kant’s eyes flash with fear — but Swedenborg only withdraws a folded
piece of PAPER.

He hands it to Kant.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          This arrived this morning.

Kant opens the paper.

His breath leaves him.

INSERT – THE PAPER  
It contains an EXACT SKETCH of the figure that appeared on his lecture
board — the sigil carefully constructed from the same angles.

Handwriting: unknown.

                      KANT
          (hoarse)
          This is impossible.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (softly)
          That word is losing its meaning.

Kant looks up, pale.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          Walk with me. There is something you must see.

Kant hesitates.

But he follows.

                                                          CUT TO:
EXT. BACK ALLEY – MIDDAY

Narrow, quiet. Snowmelt drips from a nearby gutter. A muffled,
irregular WIND moves through the passageway — almost like breathing.

Swedenborg leads Kant between two stone buildings.

                      KANT
          If this is some contrivance—

                      SWEDENBORG
          It is not of my making.

They stop at a wall of soot-darkened brick.

Swedenborg points.

A faint, chalk-like MARK — the sigil — is inscribed on the stone.
Not drawn. Not smeared.  
**Etched** into the surface as though by heat or pressure.

Kant touches it. The lines are warm.

He recoils.

                      KANT
          How long has this been here?

                      SWEDENBORG
          Days. Weeks. Perhaps longer.

                      KANT
          This could be a natural formation—

                      SWEDENBORG
          (firm)
          No. Look closer.

Kant steels himself.

He leans in—

—and the grooves ASSEMBLE themselves into perfect mathematical
proportions *as he perceives them*, adjusting subtly with his
viewpoint.

The MARK is not static. It warps — not visibly, but perceptually —
finding coherence through Kant’s observation, as if **co-authored by
his mind.**

He staggers back.

                      KANT
          (breathless)
          No… no, this is a trick of the senses.

                      SWEDENBORG
          The senses are the trick.

Kant glares at him, furious at his own fear.

                      KANT
          Enough. Enough of your riddles. You speak as
          though the world were not governed by causal
          order.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (soft)
          I speak as one who has seen that order fail.


EXT. BRIDGE OVER THE PREGEL RIVER – AFTERNOON

The river flows under an arching stone bridge. Ice clings in jagged
patterns at the banks.

Swedenborg rests his hands on the railing. Kant stands beside him,
stiff, skeptical, trying to reclaim authority.

                      KANT
          What is it you want from me?

                      SWEDENBORG
          I want nothing.  
          (beat)  
          I was told to come.

                      KANT
          Told? By whom?

Swedenborg lifts his gaze toward the sky.

                      SWEDENBORG
          They have no proper name. They are not beings,
          as we understand them. They are relations.
          Structures. The forms beneath form.

Kant’s expression tightens — annoyed, but also unnerved.

                      KANT
          I will not indulge these phantasms. If you
          have evidence, provide it. If not—

He stops.

A PATCH OF RIVER begins to FREEZE.  
Instantly. A glass-smooth sheet of ice spreads outward in a perfect
circle, crisp and silent.

The circle halts EXactly five feet from the bank — a precise radius.

Kant stares.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          No wind. Temperature hasn’t changed—

                      SWEDENBORG
          (quiet)
          Watch.

The ice rotates.  
Not drifting. Rotating as a rigid disc.

A single leaf trapped in it moves in a perfect circle around the
center.

Kant cannot move.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          It is not the river that is changing. It is the
          framework by which you perceive the river.

Kant whispers to himself.

                      KANT
          (shaking)
          This is impossible.

                      SWEDENBORG
          It is the world as it is when the curtain slips.

The disc of ice stops. Melts. The river returns to normal.

Kant backs away, gripping the rail for support.


INT. KANT’S STUDY – EVENING (LATER)

The room is dusky. Only embers glow in the stove. Kant sits at his
desk, pouring over diagrams, star charts, and notes.

His hand shakes as he tries to copy the sigil with quill and ink.

He fails.  
Every attempt is *slightly* wrong. The angles don’t align. The ratios
don’t match.

                      KANT
          (mutters)
          No... no, the base must be—  
          (angry)
          Why can’t I fix it?

He slams the quill down, breath ragged.

He stares at a blank sheet. Then slowly writes:

          “What is the origin of the forms of intuition?”

His hand freezes mid-sentence.

Behind him, faintly—

CHALK SCRAPES.

Kant turns.

The chalkboard is blank.

He steps closer.

CHALK SCRAPES AGAIN — but the chalk does not move.

The BOARD ITSELF carves the lines.  
Two strokes. Three. The beginning of something.

Kant approaches slowly, eyes wide.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          No… please no…

The chalkboard completes the sigil — not drawn on the surface, but
**appearing beneath the slate**, like a shadow of a drawing.

Kant touches the board. The sigil feels *concave*, as if engraved from
inside the stone.

He jerks his hand away.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          (whisper)
          Why me?

A KNOCK at the door.

Kant freezes.

                      SWEDENBORG (O.S.)
          May I come in?

Kant closes his eyes. A man facing his own collapse.

                      KANT
          (quiet)
          Yes.


INT. KANT’S STUDY – CONTINUOUS

Swedenborg enters.

He sees the engraving on the chalkboard.

                      SWEDENBORG
          It is accelerating.

                      KANT
          (trembling)
          I do not understand what it wants.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (corrects him gently)
          Not “it.”  
          *Them.*

Kant looks at him sharply.

                      KANT
          (hoarse)
          What… are they?

                      SWEDENBORG
          (searches for words)
          Imagine a city made not of stone, but of pure
          relation. Streets of intuition. Walls of time.
          Beings who stand not in space, but in the
          structure that gives rise to space.

Kant steps backward, horrified.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          They touch you because you are close to the truth.

                      KANT
          No—

                      SWEDENBORG
          You are thinking the thoughts that reveal them.

                      KANT
          Stop—

                      SWEDENBORG
          And they wish to see what sees them.

Kant’s knees weaken.

                      KANT
          (broken whisper)
          I am a philosopher. Nothing more.

Swedenborg’s voice softens into something like pity.

                      SWEDENBORG
          You are the man who will write the architecture
          that keeps them at bay.

A long silence.

Kant’s breath trembles.

                      KANT
          (barely audible)
          And if I refuse?

Swedenborg meets his eyes.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (quiet, fatalistic)
          Then the curtain tears.

A faint HUM vibrates the air — a low, resonant tone, like the sound
a cathedral might make if it dreamed.

Both men hear it.

Swedenborg closes his eyes, steadying himself.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          It has begun again.

Kant looks around, terrified.

                      KANT
          What has?

Swedenborg opens his eyes.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Another breach.

                                                          CUT TO:
EXT. KÖNIGSBERG – RIVERSIDE PATH – NIGHT

Snow gathers in thin drifts along the cobblestones. The river is dark,
moving sluggishly beneath patches of ice.

Kant and Swedenborg walk side-by-side, the silence thick with
unspoken dread. Swedenborg moves with purpose; Kant with growing,
trembling resistance.

                      KANT
          Tell me what we are walking toward.

                      SWEDENBORG
          A fault-line. A place where the manifold thins.

                      KANT
          (angrily)
          Use plain language.

                      SWEDENBORG
          I am.

The wind dies suddenly — too suddenly. The entire soundscape drops
into an unnatural stillness.

Kant shivers.

                      KANT
          This place… it feels—

                      SWEDENBORG
          Hollow. Yes.


EXT. ABANDONED BATHHOUSE – CONTINUOUS

A dilapidated stone structure sits beside the riverbank. A faint
orange glow flickers through the narrow windows — candlelight?
Firelight?

But no smoke rises from the chimney.

Kant stops a few feet away, uneasy.

                      KANT
          There is someone inside.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Yes.

                      KANT
          Who?

Swedenborg’s answer is slow. Measured.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Someone who is not here.

Kant scowls at the paradox — but follows him inside.


INT. ABANDONED BATHHOUSE – NIGHT

Moist stone floors. Echoes of dripping water. Empty wooden tubs line
a central chamber.

A faint HUM resonates in the air — not mechanical, not musical. A
frequency that seems to come from *inside the mind.*

On the far wall, illuminated by a single trembling lantern flame,
another SIGIL has been etched deep into the stone — larger, more
geometrically perfect than the others.

Kant approaches slowly.

                      KANT
          This is more precise than any drawn figure.
          More perfect than Euclid himself could—  
          (beat)
          Who did this?

                      SWEDENBORG
          It did itself.

                      KANT
          (fierce)
          Impossible!

                      SWEDENBORG
          The world is becoming aware of its observer.

Kant turns on him.

                      KANT
          That is *nonsense*. The world does not see. It is
          we who see.

Swedenborg steps closer to the sigil.

                      SWEDENBORG
          And when you stare long enough into the forms of
          intuition, they begin to stare back.

Kant recoils as if slapped.

The lantern flickers violently.

Then—

The SHADOWS of the wooden tubs stretch across the floor, growing
longer, longer—

—until they reach the opposite wall.

But the tubs themselves do not move.

Kant’s breathing goes ragged.

                      KANT
          No… this is a trick of light—

                      SWEDENBORG
          There is no light here strong enough for shadows
          of that length.

As Kant stares, the shadows TWITCH.

A fraction of a second. A stutter in existence.

Kant steps back.

                      KANT
          I can’t—

                      SWEDENBORG
          (low)
          Do not flee. If you run now, it will follow.

Kant freezes.

A SOUND resonates behind them — a soft scraping, like something
dragging itself across stone.

Kant turns.

The sigil on the far wall begins to rotate.

Not physically.  
The lines shift inside the stone *as if the figure itself were turning
in a space deeper than the wall.*

Kant can barely breathe.

                      KANT
          (whisper)
          How is this happening?

                      SWEDENBORG
          The manifold is adjusting its relation to you.

The sigil stops.

Silence. Total.

Then—

A HUMAN FIGURE appears in the steam rising from a cracked basin.  
A silhouette. No face. No features.  
Just a relational outline — a person-shaped absence.

Kant gasps.

                      KANT
          Who—?

                      SWEDENBORG
          Not who. **What.**

The silhouette tilts its head.

Every shadow in the room tilts with it — but the objects casting them
remain still.

Kant collapses to one knee.

                      KANT
          (broken)
          Please… no more…

The silhouette lifts an arm.

The sigil on the wall BRIGHTENS — glowing like lines of molten geometry.

Kant shields his face.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Professor Kant—

Swedenborg kneels beside him.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          —you must not yield to terror. Terror widens the
          breach.

The silhouette’s head twitches — a glitch of movement.

A low DRONING TONE fills the chamber, vibrating the bones.

Kant screams.

The sigil FLASHES — white-blinding light—


CUT TO:


INT. VOID-LIKE SPACE – UNKNOWN

Blackness. Weightlessness. No up or down.

KANT stands alone, trembling.

Geometric FORMS appear around him — circles, axes, logical structures,
the shapes of categories themselves — **pure thought made visible.**

Then they shift.

Rotate.

Merge.

The categorical structures arrange themselves into a vast PLANE of
light, shimmering with impossible depth.

A VOICE — not heard, but *known*:

                      VOICE (V.O.)
          You seek the limits of reason.

Kant tries to speak — no sound emerges.

                      VOICE (V.O.)
          The limits are not where you believe.

The plane fractures into infinite tessellations.

                      VOICE (V.O.)
          Reason is a scaffold. You walk upon it. But we—
          (beat)
          We are the ground beneath.

Kant clutches his head, overwhelmed.

                      VOICE (V.O.) (CONT’D)
          You will restore the structure.  
          Or the veil will fail.

The tessellations fold into a SHAPE — the sigil — vast, cosmic,
turning like a gear in the machinery of thought.

A final whisper:

                      VOICE (V.O.)
          Do not look again.

The sigil collapses inward—


CUT TO:


INT. KANT’S STUDY – NIGHT

Kant snaps upright, seated on the floor. Papers scattered. Candles
burned low.

He gasps, drenched in cold sweat.

Swedenborg stands near the doorway, pale, shaken but conscious.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (soft)
          You saw them.

Kant tries to stand and fails. His voice breaks.

                      KANT
          What… what are they?

                      SWEDENBORG
          (carefully)
          The structures beneath experience.  
          The skeleton of the real.

Kant pulls himself to a chair.

                      KANT
          (hollow)
          No human could survive the sight of such things.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Most do not.

He steps closer.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          But you… you will write the work that keeps the
          rest from seeing.

Kant stares at him, horror and destiny warring in his eyes.

                      KANT
          (hoarse)
          Why me?

Swedenborg’s answer is simple.  
Soft. Inevitable.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Because you are the only one who can convince the
          world that it is blind.


A beat.

The wall behind Kant — for an instant — ripples like a surface of
water.

Kant doesn’t see it.

Swedenborg does.

He closes his eyes.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          The breach will return.

He turns to leave.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          Be ready, Immanuel.

He exits.

Kant sits alone in the dim room, shaking, staring at his trembling
hands.

On the desk, a blank page.

He reaches for a quill.

And begins to write:

          “Thoughts on the True Nature of Reason…”  
          (crossed out)  
          “The Limits of Human Understanding…”  
          (crossed out)  
          “Critique of Pure—”

He stops.

The candle flame flickers violently. Then steadies.

Kant places the quill down.

                      KANT
          (barely audible)
          It must hold.  
          It must hold the world together.

He closes his eyes.

The sigil forms faintly in the corner of the room — a ghost of light
on plaster — then disappears.


FADE OUT.

                                                          CUT TO:
EXT. KÖNIGSBERG – MARKET SQUARE – MORNING

The square bustles with life. Vendors shout prices; chickens flutter
in wicker cages; carts creak over uneven stones.

Everything appears normal.

Kant enters the square slowly, scanning faces, movements, shadows —
as though expecting the world to falter again.

He forces himself forward.

A fruit vendor calls out cheerfully:

                      VENDOR
          Professor Kant! More apples today — finer than
          yesterday’s!

Kant freezes.

The vendor laughs.

                      VENDOR (CONT’D)
          Come now — surely they didn’t fall twice this
          time?

Kant’s face drains of color.

The vendor’s smile falters, confused.

Kant backs away without answering.


EXT. UNIVERSITY STEPS – CONTINUOUS

Kant climbs the stone steps deliberately, gripping the railing.

His vision wavers — for a brief moment the STEPS appear steeper,
longer, stretching like a nightmare corridor.

He shuts his eyes.

Opens them.

The stairs are normal again.

                      KANT (V.O.)
          My senses betray me. Or the world…  
          (beat)  
          Or both at once.


INT. UNIVERSITY LIBRARY – DAY

Tall shelves. Tallow candles. Dust floating in sunbeams like drifting
thoughts.

Kant moves through the stacks with a manic precision, pulling books
without hesitation:

– Leibniz  
– Wolff  
– Newton  
– Euclid  
– Mystical tracts he would never be seen holding publicly

He drops them onto a table.

SLAMS himself into a chair.

He opens Euclid. Flips pages.

Stops.

Page 67 — his favorite proposition — has been subtly altered. The
diagrams now possess angles alien to Euclidean geometry.

                      KANT
          (whisper)
          This cannot—  
          (beat)
          It cannot shift in print.

He flips to Newton.

Another sigil, faint as pencil rubbing, lies in the margin.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          No… no, no…

He grabs an old mystical text — Der Geistwelt.

Opens it.

Every page is blank.

Then — as he watches — faint letters rise from the paper, forming
sentences in a language he doesn’t know.

He SLAMS the book shut.


INT. LIBRARY – EAST WINDOW – MOMENTS LATER

Kant stands rigid at a window, breathing hard.

Students pass outside, laughing, unaware.

His reflection in the glass turns its head BEFORE he does.

Kant’s breath catches.

Then the reflection realigns, like nothing happened.

He staggers backward, knocking over a stack of books.


INT. UNIVERSITY CORRIDOR – MOMENTS LATER

Kant hurries down the hall, past portraits of scholars.

As he passes each portrait, the painted EYES track him.

He doesn’t notice — he is too consumed by a growing panic.

He reaches his office door—

A SHADOW is visible beneath the doorframe.

But there is no one inside.


INT. KANT’S OFFICE – CONTINUOUS

Kant enters.

Empty.

The shadow vanishes.

He closes the door carefully.

Then locks it.


INT. KANT’S OFFICE – CONTINUOUS

Kant paces. Wild-eyed. Pale. Whispering to himself:

                      KANT
          Space must be absolute—  
          Time must be absolute—  
          The categories must—  
          Must—  
          MUST—

His breath breaks.

A soft knock.

Kant freezes.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          (whisper)
          No… not him again.

He hesitates, then opens the door a crack.

A STUDENT (female, 19) stands there, terrified.

                      STUDENT
          Professor… something is wrong.

Kant unlocks the door fully.

                      KANT
          What did you see?

She trembles.

                      STUDENT
          I… I don’t know how to say it.

                      KANT
          Say it plainly.

She swallows.

                      STUDENT
          My father died last winter.

Kant waits.

                      STUDENT (CONT’D)
          I saw him. In the courtyard.  
          He was… speaking with a man in a red coat.

Kant stiffens.

                      KANT
          (gentle, trying)
          Grief is disorienting. It can—

                      STUDENT
          He walked across the snow.  
          There were no footprints.

Kant’s face goes white.

The girl begins crying softly.

                      STUDENT (CONT’D)
          Why did he look at me, Professor? Why did he know
          my name?

Kant steps back, gripping the desk to stay upright.

                      KANT
          (barely audible)
          The breaches are widening.


INT. KANT’S OFFICE – LATER

The student gone. Silence.

Kant sits slumped at the desk, staring at his shaking hands.

A soft rasp.

Like paper sliding across stone.

Kant snaps to attention.

A folded NOTE has appeared on his desk.

He knows that handwriting.

INSERT – THE NOTE

          “The manifold has thinned again.
           At the riverside. By the east bank.
           Come at once.”

No signature. Just a final mark:

The sigil.


EXT. KÖNIGSBERG – EAST RIVERBANK – SUNSET

The sky is a blood-orange gradient fading into deep blue. Mist hangs
low over the water.

Swedenborg waits.

Kant approaches slowly, visibly shaken.

                      KANT
          You summon me like an apprentice.

                      SWEDENBORG
          I summon no one. If the note reached you, it is
          because it wished to.

Kant bristles — terrified of how true this feels.

                      KANT
          What breach this time?

Swedenborg turns toward the river.

                      SWEDENBORG
          See for yourself.


EXT. RIVER SURFACE – CONTINUOUS

The mist parts.

Revealing—

A CITY.

Upside-down.  
Suspended beneath the surface of the water.  
Buildings rising downward into the depths, lit by ghost-pale light.

Kant gasps violently.

                      KANT
          That is… impossible.  
          (beat)  
          The water refracts— the light bends— the angle of
          incidence—

                      SWEDENBORG
          (soft)
          Speak no geometry. The world is not obliged to
          obey it.

Kant stares as the inverted city seems to SHIFT — reconfiguring itself
in response to his attention.

                      KANT
          (voice breaking)
          This is the dream I saw.  
          (beat)
          The same… the same structures…

                      SWEDENBORG
          I saw them too.

Kant turns to him, horrified by the implication.

                      KANT
          (hoarse)
          This is not shared hallucination.

                      SWEDENBORG
          No.

                      KANT
          Then what is it?

Swedenborg meets his eyes.

                      SWEDENBORG
          A place where the border between phenomena and
          structure has thinned.  
          A place where the forms beneath form can be seen.

Kant stares at the inverted city as lights flicker through the
structures like synapses in a giant brain.

                      KANT
          (whisper)
          They’re building something.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Yes.


EXT. RIVERBANK – CONTINUOUS

They stand, the mist coiling around their feet.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          There is more.

Kant tears his gaze from the city.

                      KANT
          More?

                      SWEDENBORG
          Look at your shadow.

Kant hesitates.

Then looks down.

His SHADOW is standing upright.  
Facing him.  
A full second before he moves.

Kant recoils in utter terror.

                      KANT
          No— NO—

                      SWEDENBORG
          Do not break.

The shadow moves again.

But this time, it is NOT synchronized.  
It moves independently.

And then—

It WAVES.

A gesture of recognition.

Kant collapses to the ground, scrambling backward.

                      KANT
          (screaming)
          WHAT ARE YOU?!

Swedenborg kneels beside him.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (hushed)
          They are what remain when the scaffolding of the
          world is stripped bare.

Kant’s shadow dissolves back into its normal shape — but the damage is
done.

                      SWEDENBORG (CONT’D)
          Come.  
          You must see the final breach.

Kant stares at him with raw terror.

                      KANT
          Final?

                      SWEDENBORG
          For tonight.

                                                          CUT TO:
EXT. WOODED PATH – NIGHT

The forest is skeletal in winter — bare branches clawing at the sky.
Snow muffles all sound.

Kant and Swedenborg move between trees, their lantern swinging. The air
is colder here. Too cold. Their breath plumes unnaturally slowly, as
if time lags behind it.

                      KANT
          My mind is not meant to witness these things.

                      SWEDENBORG
          No mind is meant to. But some are… utilized.

Kant stops walking.

                      KANT
          Utilized? By what?

Swedenborg doesn’t answer. He simply continues forward.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Hurry.

Kant follows reluctantly.


EXT. FOREST CLEARING – CONTINUOUS

A clearing opens before them — circular, unnaturally perfect, as if
something pressed outward from beneath the earth.

In the center:  
A STONE STRUCTURE the size of a small shrine.  
Flat, geometric.  
Not carved — but extruded from the bedrock, as if grown from pure
form.

Kant’s eyes widen.

                      KANT
          This was not here before—

                      SWEDENBORG
          (quiet)
          It was. But not for you.

The lantern flame flickers wildly, then steadies.

Kant approaches the structure.

Its surface is inscribed with the SIGIL — faint, as though still
cooling.

                      KANT
          (barely breathing)
          Why show me this?

                      SWEDENBORG
          Because you will define the boundaries.  
          And they require you to see what happens when
          boundaries fail.

Kant touches the stone.  
It hums — a vibration with no source.

Then—

A FRACTURE splits open across the structure.  
A hairline tear.

But it is not a crack in stone — it is a crack in *space*.

The air inside bends, luminous and impossible. Like looking through
the logic of the world itself.

Kant stumbles back, choking on terror.

                      KANT
          Close it— CLOSE IT—

                      SWEDENBORG
          I cannot.

The crack widens.

A distortion forms at its center — a knot of geometry twisting on
itself, rotating through impossible axes, like a shadow of a 4D object
bleeding into 3D space.

Kant’s eyes well with tears.

                      KANT
          (weeping)
          This is the end of reason—

                      SWEDENBORG
          (softly)
          No.  
          This is its beginning.

Kant falls to the ground. Snow dusts his hair.

The geometric distortion pulses.

A SOUND emerges — not heard, but *felt* — like the deep resonance of
a thought too large to fit inside a mind.

                      THE VOICE (V.O.)
          Immanuel Kant.

Kant’s blood runs cold.

                      KANT
          (whispers)
          No…

                      THE VOICE (V.O.)
          The scaffolding weakens.  
          The veil thins.  
          The lattice shifts.

Kant shakes his head violently.

                      KANT
          Stop— STOP—

                      THE VOICE (V.O.)
          You will define the boundaries.  
          You will bind the manifold.  
          You will write the rule by which blindness is
          preserved.

Tears stream down Kant’s face — a mixture of anguish and awe.

                      KANT
          Why me? WHY ME?

The distortion flash-flares — a white shockwave through the clearing.

                      THE VOICE (V.O.)
          Because you asked what could not be asked.

The fracture shudders.

Swedenborg steps forward, trembling.

                      SWEDENBORG
          Enough. He is still flesh-bound.  
          He cannot hold this much.

The anomaly pulses again — dimmer now.

                      THE VOICE (V.O.)
          Then let him be unbound.

Kant screams — a sound ripped out of him by pure cognitive overload —


FLASH TO WHITE.


INT. KANT’S BEDROOM – NIGHT

Kant wakes on the floor, violently, as if dropped from height.

Sweat-soaked. Shivering. Hoarse from screaming.

The room is quiet.

Too quiet.

He sits up, body trembling.

                      KANT
          (weak)
          A dream… a nightmare…

He tries to calm himself.

But then — the shadows in the room shift.

Just slightly.

Kant freezes.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          (breathless)
          No…

A FIGURE stands in the corner — the same silhouette from the steam.
Undefined. Featureless.

Kant scrambles back.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          PLEASE—

The silhouette raises its hand.

A single whisper escapes it — not voiced, but KNOWLEDGED.

                      SHADOW
          Write.

The figure dissipates into smoke.

Kant collapses against the wall, sobbing.


INT. KANT’S STUDY – DAWN

The first light of day creeps through the frost-lined window.

Kant sits at his desk.

Eyes hollow.  
Hands trembling.  
Ink frozen in the nib of his quill.

Pages scattered everywhere — notes, diagrams, ravings.

He stares at a fresh sheet of parchment.

Blank.

The quill hovers.

                      KANT
          (whisper)
          To save them…  
          I must define the limits.  
          I must confine the breach.

He lowers the quill.

And writes:

          “THOUGHTS ON THE NATURE AND LIMITS  
           OF HUMAN REASON.”

He stops.

Crosses it out.

Writes again:

          “CRITIQUE OF PURE REASON.”

He exhales, shaking.

                      KANT (CONT’D)
          It must hold.  
          God help me — it must hold.


EXT. KÖNIGSBERG STREET – MORNING

Snow glitters. People bustle past.

Kant walks slowly, cloak drawn tight, eyes scanning the world — every
shadow, every reflection.

He sees a puddle in the street.

Pauses.

The surface reflects the sky — clouds drifting.

Then—

The SIGIL forms in the water, just for an instant.

Kant stares, paralyzed.

The image fades.

He turns away, shaken, and continues walking.


EXT. CITY OUTSKIRTS – SAME TIME

Swedenborg watches Kant from a distance.

He turns toward the forest.

The trees seem… wrong. Bent. Shifted. Watching.

He shudders.

                      SWEDENBORG
          (soft)
          They are not done.

He walks into the mist.

Disappears.


FADE OUT.


                               THE END
