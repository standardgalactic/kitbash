We develop a unified geometric framework in which unistochastic quantum mechanics,
relativistic field theory, and information geometry arise as projections of a single
shifted-symplectic plenum. Both quantum phases (complex rotations) and Lorentz boosts

\[
(c\Delta t)^2 = d^2 + (v\Delta t)^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \Delta t = \gamma\Delta\tau,
\]

This paper develops the thesis that both special relativity and quantum mechanics arise from rotations in appropriate metric spaces---hyperbolic (split-complex) and circular (complex) respectively---and that Barandes's unistochastic quantum mechanics \cite{Barandes2020} provides the stochastic shadow of these rotations. Our approach builds on the geometric quantization program \cite{Kibble1979, Ashtekar1999} while incorporating insights from information geometry \cite{Goyal2010, Petz1996} and category theory \cite{Baez2015}.

\section{The Light-Clock Derivation and the Geometry of Rotation}

The transverse light-clock provides the fundamental geometric connection, with roots in Einstein's original 1905 paper. For mirrors with proper separation $d$ moving at velocity $v$, light travels along the hypotenuse:
\[
(c\Delta t)^2 = d^2 + (v\Delta t)^2.
\]
Solving yields:
\[
\Delta t = \frac{d}{\sqrt{c^2 - v^2}} = \frac{d}{c\sqrt{1-\beta^2}} = \gamma\Delta\tau,
\]
where $\Delta\tau = d/c$ is the proper time and $\beta = v/c$.

This admits two geometric interpretations that reveal the deep connection to complex and hyperbolic geometry \cite{Yaglom1968, Needham1997}:

\begin{definition}[Circular Interpretation]
Define $\cos\theta = \sqrt{1-\beta^2} = 1/\gamma$, $\sin\theta = \beta$. Then:
\[
\Delta t = \frac{\Delta\tau}{\cos\theta}.
\]
The factor $\gamma$ appears as a secant, suggesting a projection effect familiar from complex analysis \cite{Ahlfors1979}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Hyperbolic Interpretation]  
Define rapidity $\phi$ via $\tanh\phi = \beta$. Then:
\[
\cosh\phi = \gamma, \quad \sinh\phi = \gamma\beta, \quad \Delta t = \Delta\tau\cosh\phi.
\]
This reveals the boost as a hyperbolic rotation in spacetime, extending the geometric view of \cite{Misner1973}.
\end{definition}

The hyperbolic interpretation is fundamental: \emph{Lorentz boosts are rotations in Minkowski space}. The rapidity $\phi$ serves as the natural hyperbolic angle, with velocity composition corresponding to angle addition: $\phi_{\text{total}} = \phi_1 + \phi_2$.

\section{Rotations, Matrices, and Complex Numbers}

\subsection{Complex Rotations and Matrix Representation}

A 2D rotation through angle $\theta$ is represented by:
\[
R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}.
\]
Identifying $(x,y)$ with $z = x + iy$, this corresponds to complex multiplication:
\[
R(\theta)\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix} \longleftrightarrow e^{i\theta}z.
\]

This equivalence, emphasized in \cite{Needham1997}, shows that complex numbers provide a coordinate-free representation of rotations. The same structure appears in quantum mechanics, where time evolution $e^{-iHt/\hbar}$ rotates state vectors in Hilbert space, as developed in geometric quantum mechanics \cite{Kibble1979, Ashtekar1999}.

\subsection{Hyperbolic Rotations and Split-Complex Numbers}

Lorentz boosts in the $tx$-plane are represented by:
\[
\Lambda(\phi) = \begin{pmatrix} \cosh\phi & -\sinh\phi \\ -\sinh\phi & \cosh\phi \end{pmatrix}.
\]
Using split-complex numbers $w = ct + jx$ with $j^2 = +1$, this corresponds to:
\[
\Lambda(\phi)\begin{pmatrix}ct\\x\end{pmatrix} \longleftrightarrow e^{-j\phi}w.
\]

The split-complex unit $j$ generates hyperbolic rotations, preserving the Minkowski interval $c^2t^2 - x^2$ rather than the Euclidean distance, extending the geometric algebra approaches of \cite{Dorst2007}.

\subsection{Pseudo-Quaternionic Unification}

\begin{definition}[Pseudo-Quaternion Algebra]
The pseudo-quaternion algebra $\mathbb{PQ}$ has generators $\{1, i, j, ij\}$ with:
\[
i^2 = -1, \quad j^2 = +1, \quad ij = -ji.
\]
\end{definition}

This non-commutative algebra contains both complex ($\mathbb{C} \cong \langle 1, i \rangle$) and split-complex ($\mathbb{S} \cong \langle 1, j \rangle$) subalgebras as commutative subsets. The anti-commutation $ij = -ji$ ensures the algebra is consistent and four-dimensional over the reals, relating to Clifford algebraic approaches in physics \cite{Dorst2007}.

The exponential map gives:
\[
e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \quad \text{(circular rotation)}
\]
\[
e^{j\phi} = \cosh\phi + j\sinh\phi \quad \text{(hyperbolic rotation)}
\]

These represent quantum unitary evolution and Lorentz boosts respectively, unified within a single algebraic structure that extends the geometric unification programs of \cite{Penrose2004, Frankel2011}.

\subsection{Matrix--Wave Equivalence}

The fundamental equivalence:
\[
\text{Matrix action on vectors} \longleftrightarrow \text{Complex multiplication of wavefunctions}
\]
reveals that wavefunctions are coordinate-free representations of linear operators. In quantum mechanics, this means the Schrödinger equation $i\hbar\partial_t\psi = H\psi$ can be viewed either as a matrix differential equation or as governing the complex rotation of a wavefunction.

This extends to the pseudo-quaternionic case: both quantum and relativistic transformations can be represented as multiplication by exponentials in the appropriate algebra, connecting to the information-geometric approaches of \cite{Goyal2010, Petz1996}.

\begin{definition}[Coarse-Graining Endofunctor]

\[

\end{definition}

This operation takes a unitary matrix (complex rotations) to a doubly stochastic matrix (probability transitions). The term "endofunctor" indicates it maps a category to itself while preserving the categorical structure---in this case, the composition of operations, extending the categorical approaches of \cite{Baez2015].

\begin{proposition}
$\mathsf{C}$ satisfies:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathsf{C}^2 \simeq \mathsf{C}$ (idempotence: squaring twice is the same as squaring once)
\item $\sum_i (\mathsf{C}(U))_{ij} = 1$ (probability conservation: columns sum to one)  
\item $\mathsf{C}(U_1 \circ U_2) = \mathsf{C}(U_1) \circ \mathsf{C}(U_2)$ (functoriality: respects composition)
\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}

This formalizes the hierarchy:
\[
\text{Unsquared numbers } U_{ij} \in \mathbb{C} \xrightarrow{\mathsf{C}} \text{Squared numbers } \Gamma_{ij} = |U_{ij}|^2.

The unsquared level contains complete information about phases and interference; the squared level contains only probabilities, having lost the capacity to exhibit quantum interference effects, relating to the information-theoretic approaches of \cite{Goyal2010].

\subsection{Operator-Theoretic Treatment of $\mathsf{C}$}

We introduce an operator norm or metric, such as the Bures or Fisher metric, on $\mathcal{U}(n)$. The functor $\mathsf{C}$ acts as a contraction mapping in this metric, reducing dimensionality while preserving essential probabilistic structures.

\begin{theorem}
$\mathsf{C}$ is measure-preserving on the Haar measure but entropy-increasing on the Shannon measure.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Let $\mu_H$ be the Haar measure on $\mathcal{U}(n)$ and
$H(U)=-\!\sum_{ij}|U_{ij}|^2\ln|U_{ij}|^2$.
Because $\mu_H$ is invariant under left/right multiplication,
$\int H(U)\,d\mu_H \le \int H(\mathsf{C}(U))\,d\mu_H$,
with equality only for permutation matrices.
Hence $\mathsf{C}$ preserves $\mu_H$ but increases Shannon entropy.
\end{proof}

\section{Dimensional Threshold and Necessity of Complex Numbers}

\begin{definition}[Unistochastic Manifold]
The set $\mathcal{US}(n)=\{\,\Gamma\in\mathcal{DS}(n)\mid
\exists\,U\in\mathcal{U}(n),\ \Gamma_{ij}=|U_{ij}|^2\,\}$
inherits a stratified Riemannian metric
$g_{ij,kl} = \Re(\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl}/U_{ij}\overline{U}_{kl})$
from $\mathcal{U}(n)$ via the projection $\mathsf{C}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:complex_necessity}
For $n \geq 3$, $\mathcal{OS}(n) \subsetneq \mathcal{US}(n) \subseteq \mathcal{DS}(n)$.
\end{theorem}

Here $\mathcal{DS}(n)$ denotes doubly stochastic matrices (nonnegative matrices with rows and columns summing to 1), $\mathcal{US}(n)$ denotes unistochastic matrices (those arising from unitary matrices via $\Gamma_{ij} = |U_{ij}|^2$), and $\mathcal{OS}(n)$ denotes orthostochastic matrices (those arising from real orthogonal matrices), extending the geometric quantum mechanics of \cite{Bengtsson2017].

\begin{proof}
Consider the $3 \times 3$ Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) matrix:
\[
U = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & 1 \\
1 & \omega & \omega^2 \\
1 & \omega^2 & \omega
\end{pmatrix}, \quad \omega = e^{2\pi i/3}.
\]
This yields $\Gamma_{ij} = 1/3$ for all $i,j$. However, no real orthogonal matrix $O$ satisfies $O_{ij}^2 = 1/3$ for all $i,j$, as this would require a $3 \times 3$ real Hadamard matrix, which doesn't exist. Therefore, this $\Gamma$ is unistochastic but not orthostochastic \cite{Zyczkowski2003}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
Complex numbers are structurally necessary for covering all unistochastic processes when $n \geq 3$.
\end{corollary}

This dimensional threshold explains why quantum mechanics for systems with at least three states fundamentally requires complex numbers, while classical probability theory and two-state quantum systems can be described using only reals, providing a new perspective on Hardy's axiomatic approach \cite{Hardy2001].

\section*{Appendix C: Historical Development of Rotational Ontology}

The lineage of rotational ontology in physics represents a progressive realization
that rotation—circular, hyperbolic, or complex—is not merely a kinematic
phenomenon but the generative structure of physical law itself. From Euclid’s
geometry to modern field theory, the concept of rotation has served as the
bridge between symmetry, conservation, and ontology.

\subsection*{C.1. Euclidean Foundations}

In classical Greek geometry, rotation defined the essence of spatial invariance.
Euclid’s \textit{Elements} formalized congruence and isometry, establishing that
rotation leaves the metric form invariant:
\[
x'^2 + y'^2 = x^2 + y^2.
\]
This invariance under rotation constituted the earliest articulation of what would
become the group-theoretic idea of symmetry. For the Greeks, geometry was
ontology—form and being were unified through spatial proportion.

\subsection*{C.2. Hamilton and the Birth of Algebraic Rotation}

William Rowan Hamilton’s invention of the quaternions in 1843 extended rotation
from geometry into algebra. His defining relation,
\[
i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1,
\]
encoded rotation as algebraic multiplication. The quaternion represented a
direct bridge between geometry and motion, a “rotation operator” acting in
three-dimensional space. This move from geometric figure to algebraic operator
was a conceptual revolution: it replaced description with generation.  
Hamilton’s dictum, “Time is to be thought of as one of the coordinates,”
foreshadowed the relativistic and complex-unity perspectives to come.

\subsection*{C.3. Minkowski and the Hyperbolic Turn}

Hermann Minkowski’s 1908 unification of space and time as a four-dimensional
pseudo-Euclidean manifold,
\[
s^2 = c^2t^2 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2,
\]
transformed rotation into the fabric of reality. Lorentz transformations were
interpreted as hyperbolic rotations in spacetime, preserving the invariant
interval. The “hyperbolic turn” revealed that time dilation and length
contraction were not dynamical distortions but projections of a deeper geometric
symmetry.  
Rotation, now extended to the imaginary axis, became the law of relativity:
geometry and physics merged under the same transformation group.

\subsection*{C.4. Kibble and Geometric Quantum Mechanics}

In 1979, Thomas W. B. Kibble proposed the geometrization of quantum mechanics,
treating pure states as points on a complex projective Hilbert space endowed with
a Fubini–Study metric. Quantum evolution,
\[
i\hbar \frac{d\psi}{dt} = H\psi,
\]
was recast as geodesic flow on a symplectic manifold. This formalism unified the
Schrödinger equation and Hamiltonian mechanics, showing that quantum
superposition is a form of continuous rotation in complex projective space.
Kibble’s insight revived the rotational ontology within the quantum domain:
amplitude and phase were no longer abstract quantities but geometric coordinates
on a manifold of possible realities.

\subsection*{C.5. Barandes and the Unistochastic Reformulation}

Jacob Barandes’s unistochastic quantum mechanics (2020) reframed the probabilistic
structure of quantum theory as the shadow of an underlying unitary rotation.
Each transition probability,
\[
P_{ij} = |U_{ij}|^2,
\]
was identified as the squared projection of an unsquared amplitude—rotation
squared. In this sense, unistochastic matrices generalize classical stochastic
ones by reintroducing the missing geometric phase. Barandes restored the link
between stochasticity and rotation, showing that randomness itself can emerge
from incomplete observation of rotational invariants in complex space.

\subsection*{C.6. RSVP and the Rotational Plenum}

The Relativistic Scalar–Vector Plenum (RSVP) framework extends the lineage by
embedding both quantum and relativistic rotations within a single pseudo-quaternionic
field ontology. Here, circular (complex) and hyperbolic (split-complex)
rotations appear as complementary sections of a unified algebra:
\[
e^{i\theta + j\phi}
= \cos\theta\cosh\phi
  + i\sin\theta\cosh\phi
  + j\cos\theta\sinh\phi
  + ij\sin\theta\sinh\phi.
\]
This construction reconciles the two great rotation groups of physics—unitary
and Lorentzian—within a common geometric substrate. In RSVP, rotation is no
longer an operation upon space but the generative mechanism of space itself.
Quantum amplitudes and relativistic intervals are twin aspects of a single
rotational process in a higher-order symplectic plenum.

\subsection*{C.7. Philosophical Continuum}

Across these epochs, rotation evolves from a spatial transformation to an
ontological principle.  
\begin{itemize}
  \item For Euclid, rotation preserves form.  
  \item For Hamilton, rotation generates algebraic structure.  
  \item For Minkowski, rotation constitutes reality’s metric.  
  \item For Kibble, rotation governs state evolution.  
  \item For Barandes, rotation underlies stochastic emergence.  
  \item For RSVP, rotation becomes the fabric of being itself.
\end{itemize}

In this cumulative trajectory, the geometry of rotation transforms into a theory
of existence. RSVP completes this historical arc by identifying rotation as the
unifying action underlying curvature, probability, and consciousness: the
continuous turning of the plenum upon itself.
