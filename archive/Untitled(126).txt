🧠 What You’ve Created

➤ A Philosophical Parable of Misattribution:

A vial spills. A dog drinks. The dog dies.
Ergo, the liquid was hemlock.
Ergo, Socrates dispenses hemlock.
Ergo, Socrates is corrupting the youth.
Therefore, Socrates must die by... hemlock.

Each step is a fallacy of inference, yet the whole thing has the eerie internal logic of myth or dream—making it seem inevitable, even righteous.

🔍 The Core Fallacy: Affirming the Consequent

The logic of the court (or the mythmakers) went something like:

If a substance is hemlock, then it will kill the dog. ✅

The dog died.

Therefore, it was hemlock. ❌

This is textbook affirming the consequent, a formal fallacy.
But here it’s no longer an academic mistake—it’s a lethal moral and political error, disguised as certainty.

🧪 Poison as Ambiguity

The real brilliance here:
The vial wasn’t hemlock—but it might as well have been, because:

It killed something

It came from Socrates

It symbolized danger to the social order

The poison doesn't need to be chemically identified, because its meaning is more important than its composition. That’s scapegoating logic in action.

⚖️ Socrates as Victim of Semiotic Substitution

“We couldn’t find the real threat, so we picked the thing that looked like a threat.”

Socrates became the signifier of corruption.
The poison became the signifier of blasphemy.
The dog’s death became the signifier of guilt.

No science. No analysis. Just meaning masquerading as fact.

🐾 The Dog as Divine Irony

The dog, Andros, becomes:

Martyr

Witness

Scapegoat of the scapegoat

His death is the false clue upon which true punishment is built.
A little Oedipal twist: the truth comes from a body, but it is misread, and tragedy follows.

The poison was not hemlock.
